            <p><strong><img width="600" height="453" alt="" src="/user_files/443/image/Corrigan%20resized.jpg" /><br />
            </strong><em>Dennis Corrigan, member of the research faculty and battery expert, holding nickel metal hydride (NiMH) and cylindrical battery module (left) and Prius Prismatic NiMH battery module. Besides new Electric-drive Vehicle Engineering degree and certificate programs, the College of Engineering is venturing into advanced battery technology research, particularly lithium ion batteries.&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp; Photo by David Reich</em><strong><br />
            </strong></p>
            <p><br />
            <br />
            Beginning this fall, the College of Engineering will offer classes leading toward bachelor's and master's of science degrees in electric drive vehicle engineering. <br />
            <br />
            As the auto industry ramps up manufacturing of hybrid and electric drive vehicles, the need for engineers trained in advanced propulsion and battery technology and integration has become critical.</p>
            <p>Funded by a $5 million grant from the U.S. Department of Energy through the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, Wayne State's Electric-Drive Vehicle Engineering programs reflect a transformative shift in the automobile industry from petroleum-powered engines to renewable, resource-based, electric-powered motors.<br />
            <br />
            The new programs include a bachelor of science in Electric Transportation Technology (ETT), a master of science in Electric-Drive Vehicle Engineering and a graduate certificate program in Electric-Drive Vehicle Engineering, a subset of the master's degree.<br />
            <br />
            Wayne State students enrolled in the new programs will develop a skill set for each sector of the industry. The bachelor of science in ETT, the first of its kind in the nation, formalizes a 2+2 articulation agreement with area community colleges or equivalent lower division programs. The structure enables students to enroll at the community college level, receive an electric vehicle technician associate's degree after two years, and then complete the bachelor's degree in ETT at Wayne State after an additional two years of instruction.</p>
            <p>In addition to the college's standing alternative energy technology faculty, the programs have attracted top battery research scientists from industry who have joined the adjunct faculty.</p>
            <p>The classes will be taught in conjunction with required laboratory components in energy storage, vehicle integration and power control electronics.&nbsp; In addition, industry partners will open their facilities and resources for Wayne State students in the program to study the technology at their sites.</p>
            <p>Wayne State has a long history of working hand in hand with the automotive industry to produce the world's top engineering talent and place students in highly competitive jobs. Wayne State started its Alternative Energy Technology program in 2000, leading universities worldwide and creating a platform upon which it was able to immediately utilize the U.S. Department of Energy funds for educating students.<br />
            <br />
            &quot;Our vision is to make this program strong and accessible,&quot; said Mumtaz Usmen, interim dean. &quot;We are leading the education effort in the nation by teaching and training people to work for companies across the automotive supply chain and help make the transition to renewable energy sources more efficient and systematic. Our goal is to teach students to continue to learn even after they graduate and equip them to seek out the resources they need to remain current and job-ready for the future.&quot;</p>
            <p>For more information about the program and enrollment, please visit:<br />
            <a href="http://www.eng.wayne.edu/page.php?id=5909">http://www.eng.wayne.edu/page.php?id=5909</a></p>
