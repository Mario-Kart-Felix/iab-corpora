		<h2><a href="http://homebusiness.about.com/b/2013/08/26/when-provocative-goes-to-far-or-what-we-can-learn-from-miley-cyrus.htm" zT="1/1jS">When Provocative Goes Too Far or What We Can Learn from Miley Cyrus</a></h2>
		
		<div class="date">Monday August 26, 2013</div>	
	<div class="entry">I watched the VMAs last night with my daughter, and was surprised, as many were, at Miley Cyrus' performance. Miley has said before that she doesn't want to be a role model and you can't blame her for not wanting to be Hannah Montana forever. But in her attempt to be provocative, she overstepped. I think she made Robin Thicke blush, which isn't easy when you consider what his song "Blurred Lines" is about. <BR><BR>
Overdoing provocative can happen in business as well. I've always thought the public service announcement about pertussis that told moms hugging their babies could kill them went too far. I remember several years a go, a group promoting a "green" direct sales company said moms where killing their babies with cleaning products. It's true that pertussis is bad and that cleaning products can be toxic, but in an attempt to shock and be provocative, these ads ended up offending and alienating their market. <BR><BR>
Coming up with ads that make people take notice and talk is a great way to expand your business or a fast way to ruin your brand. I would suggest that before running any promotional piece meant to cause a stir, you should show it to people you know and trust for feedback.<BR><BR>
What ads have you come across that went to far?</div>

		<h2><a href="http://homebusiness.about.com/b/2013/08/22/are-you-zoning-compliant.htm" zT="1/1jS">Are You Zoning Compliant?</a></h2>
		
		<div class="date">Thursday August 22, 2013</div>	
	<div class="entry">I recently ran across an<a href="http://www.championnewspapers.com/articles/2013/08/20/news/doc520e9c7ab6d54561629808.txt" zT="1/XL/X[" target="_new"> article online in which a woman was fighting her city over a zoning fee </a>of $250. Debb Childs, a hotel broker from Chino, California argued that her business doesn't generate traffic or require signs, and that people wouldn't know she was running a business from home. She further objected the $250 because it's so much more than other locations,  such as Pasadena, which according the article, charges only $26.89.<BR><BR>
Originally, zoning was established to designate residential and commercial areas. It's a good thing because you don't want a toxic waste dump or strip joint as a neighbor. Residential areas are protected from commercial activity to retain the aesthetics and safety of the neighborhood. However, technology and in particular the Internet, has made it possible for people to run a business without having clients in their home, signs, equipment or other materials that neighbors might object to. <BR><BR>
Some locales have created waivers for home businesses that don't impact a neighborhood. In 1991, I obtained a waiver from the town I lived in at the time, Santa Rosa, California. But apparently, Chino, California doesn't have a waiver, and at the end of Ms. Childs' impassioned speech, it still required her to pay. You don't get to be a success in business without tenacity and a willingness to ask for what you want, which Ms. Childs' proved when she told the City Council that she'd be back every meeting to make her request. At that point, the council granted her a waiver. <BR><BR>
Zoning is an issue many home business owners don't check on, but they should. An angry neighbor could turn you in, and then you'd be stuck with the fee and possibly a penalty. While you're at it, you should also check home owner association regs and your lease/rental agreement to see if there is any restrictions on home business. <BR><BR>
Have you checked with your city or county about zoning for your home business?</div>

There are many reasons to work at home. 		<h2><a href="http://homebusiness.about.com/b/2013/08/20/is-gmails-new-inbox-hurting-open-rates.htm" zT="1/1jS">Is Gmail's New Inbox Hurting Open Rates?</a></h2>
		
		<div class="date">Tuesday August 20, 2013</div>	
	<div class="entry">Several weeks ago, I took Friday off to go to Busch Gardens with my daughter and her friend. The only business I needed to attend to was to make sure my email newsletter went out. As I sat by Mach Tower checking my email, I didn't see the newsletter. I was sure I scheduled it. Had I forgotten to confirm it? That's when I noticed the option for "Social" and "Promotions" in Gmail. Clicking on promotions, I discovered the newsletter. <BR><BR>
My first thought was concern. How many people who use Gmail would notice that the newsletter was sent to the "Promotions" tab? How would that effect the open rates of my newsletter? I went back to check open rates since March and found no significant changes. Whew. Email is such an important part of my business, I worried how this change would impact me. Fortunately, my Gmail subscribers seem to have figured it out and are reading the newsletter as usual.<BR><BR>
Still, the change is new enough, that it's something I'll continue to monitor.
