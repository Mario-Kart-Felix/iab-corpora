        var previousState = __squid_like.member_likes;
        __squid_like.member_likes ? dec() : inc();

        SquidgamingJS.post(

          // this logic is opposite because the state class is applied above 
          { 'action': $J('li.social_like').hasClass('social_liked') ? 'like' : 'unlike' },

              $Sq.notify.error("Hey there! You've reached the maximum number of SquidLikes for today. Come back again tomorrow to SquidLike this lens.", { hideAfter: 5000 });

          },
          'text', 
          function() { // error
            previousState ? inc() : dec();
            allButtons.removeClass('disabled');
          }
        );
      }

      // if not logged-in, redirect to login
      // this only happens if the Squidoo.login hasn't executed

<div class="text_module">
		<p style="border:double 5px #000033; -moz-border-radius: 15px; -khtml-border-radius: 15px; -webkit-border-radius: 15px; border-radius:15px; background:#FCF8C0; color: #000; padding: 10px;"><img style="float: right;padding-left: 10px" src="http://www.my66cuda.com/pics/1915-ford-depot-hack-2.jpg" alt="1915 Ford Station Wagon" />The very first Station Wagons were called &quot;depot hacks.&quot; Depot hacks weren't production vehicles; they were based on a commercial chassis (usually Ford) with a custom body built by independent companies for extra passenger and cargo space. These <b><em>hacks</em></b> (taxis) were used to haul people and their luggage to and from the train <b><em>depot</em></b>, hence the term <b><em>depot hack</em></b>. Over time, the term <b><em>station</em></b> replaced depot, <b><em>wagon</em></b> replaced hack, and these specialty vehicles became known as <b><em>Station Wagons</em></b>. <section data-lazyload="no" data-lmi="89687511" data-mod-pos="11" data-module-type="amazon" class="novib module  amazon " id="module89687511">
  
    
            <hgroup class="module_header">
                  <h2 class="module_title">
            The Golden Age          </h2>
                          <h3 class="module_subtitle">
            Station Wagons 1950 - 1975          </h3>
              </hgroup>
        
  
  
<div class="module_commerce" >
  
<div class="module_description"><p style="border:double 5px #000033; -moz-border-radius: 15px; -khtml-border-radius: 15px; -webkit-border-radius: 15px; border-radius:15px; background:#FCF8C0; color: #000; padding: 10px;">In my opinion, the best station wagons are American wagons made between 1950 and 1973. In the years following WWII, demand for cars started to grow. Wagon sales started out slowly, but grew rapidly during the 1950's. In 1951, station wagons accounted for only 3.3% of all car sales in the U.S. By 1959, they accounted for almost 17% of U.S. car sales. They were no longer considered utility vehicles, and for many car makers represented the <b>top of the line in styling and functionality</b>. The low end wasn't ignored either - both Ford and Plymouth introduced compact wagons in 1960 (Falcon and Valiant respectively), with Chevy releasing the compact Corvair station wagon in 1961 and the much more popular Chevy II (Nova) in 1962. Throughout the 1960's, innovation was the order of the day as auto makers tried to "one-up" each other. As the 60's came to a close though, auto makers turned more towards product refinement, although some innovation continued, like GM's "Clam Shell" tailgate design. Ford and Chevy introduced 2 door compact wagons in the early 1970's (Pinto and Vega respectively), just in time for the energy crisis of 1974. The energy crisis, along with new emissions regulations imposed by Congress, marked the beginning of the end. Production would continue for 2 more decades, but for the most part the Station Wagon's best years were behind it.</p></div>

                          <h3 class="module_subtitle">

      <!--INFOLINKS_ON-->
  
  
<!-- NOTE: image alignment classes are text_img_left, text_img_right, and text_img_block -->
<div class="text_module">
		The best way to avoid getting ripped off is to know as much as possible about the car <b><em>before</em></b> you commit to buy (i.e. place a bid). Talk to the seller early and often. If the car is within driving distance, inspect it yourself. If not, contact a professional vehicle inspection company in the seller's area. Yes it costs money, but it could save you from losing much more. It can also help weed out bad sellers... if the seller isn't willing to allow an inspection then you should probably look elsewhere for your station wagon. Also, talk to the seller about transportation. Almost all sellers will expect you to arrange transportation, but some are more helpful than others. Many sellers will expect an advance payment at the end of the auction. If you will be picking up the car yourself, see if they will wave the deposit and accept full payment at time of pickup (delivery).
