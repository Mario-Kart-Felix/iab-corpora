				<h3><a href="http://www.classiccar.co.nz/news-7900/audi-quattro-celebrates-30th-anniversary" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Audi Quattro celebrates 30th Anniversary">Audi Quattro celebrates 30th Anniversary</a></h3>
				<div class="postmeta">February 16th, 2010 by NZ Classic Car</div>

				 					<p><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-13280" title="Audi Quattro 30th Anniversary fq" src="http://www.classiccar.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2010/02/Audi-Quattro-30th-Anniversary-fq1.jpg" alt="" width="455" height="321" /></p>
<p>Audi is celebrating another two major milestones this year, just a year after its big 100th birthday celebrations. The first milestone is the 30th birthday of quattro, a name that humbled Audi&#8217;s rally rivals when it was first unleshed. Several events are lined up to commemorate and pay tribute to the quattro brand. These include a special exhibition at the Audi Forum Ingolstadt and another exhibition of four Audi quattro models of outstanding interest at the Techno Classica in Essen which starts on April 7.</p>
<p>The company will also be putting on a epic display at the Grand Prix Historique in Monaco between May 1 and 2. There spectators will be able to see an Auto Union Type C from 1936 and an Auto Union Type D with a twin-supercharger engine from 1939. Other celebratory events include participation at the Goodwood Festival of Speed in July and taking part in various classic rallies.</p>
<p>Audi&#8217;s second reason to celebrate is the 125th anniversary of the Wanderer brand. Wanderer made cars and motor cycles until the start of WWII and is represented in one of the four rings on the famous Audi badge along with Audi itself, Horch and DKW.</p>

				<h3><a href="http://www.classiccar.co.nz/news-7900/auto-union-d-type-back-on-the-auction-block-next-month" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Auto Union D-Type back on the auction block next month">Auto Union D-Type back on the auction block next month</a></h3>
				<div class="postmeta">June 12th, 2009 by NZ Classic Car</div>

				 					<p><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-5446" src="http://www.classiccar.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2009/06/Auto-Union-D-Type-fq.jpg" alt="Auto Union D-Type fq" width="455" height="237" /></p>
<p>In the years leading up to WWII, Adolf Hitler was bent on world conquest &mdash; not just on the battlefield, but on the race track as well. Motor racing became a favourite venue for Hitler to exhibit what he viewed as Germany&#8217;s superiority, Germany&#8217;s top engineers at both Mercedes-Benz and Auto Union were commissioned to develop a generation of advanced racing machines that would later become known as the Silver Arrows. The 1938 Auto Union D-Type was Hitler&#8217;s favourite.</p>
<p>The cars reigned victorious through the 1930s, but when the war began, motorsport in Europe was postponed. The following decade Nazi Germany had fallen, Hitler was dead, and the Soviets seized the D-Types from the Auto Union factory in East Germany. With an Iron Curtain closed, motor historians optimistically thought the Silver Arrows cars were safe somewhere in the Soviet Union, but all the while they were being dismantled. Although many of the Mercedes pre-war racers survived, few of the Auto Unions did. Only three of these D-Types are known to still exist.</p>
<p>The example for sale, Chassis 19, was completely restored to original condition, and was set to hit the auction block at Christie&#8217;s two years ago until it was withdrawn at the last minute due to suspicion over its heritage. With the questions answered and its pedigree confirmed, Bonhams is now offering this extremely rare vehicle at its upcoming auction this August, where it is expected to fetch in excess of $8 million USD. But some previous estimates have placed it closer to $12 million USD, it stands a real chance of breaking the $12.2 million USD record set by the 1957 Ferrari 250 Testarossa at the RM Auction in Maranello just last month.</p>
<p>Check out the press release below for a full history of this fascinating vehicle.</p>
<h3>PRESS RELEASE</h3>
<p><em>Bonhams &amp; Butterfields to Offer Hans Stuck&#8217;s Legendary Auto Union Grand Prix Racer at Quail Lodge in August</em></p>
<p><em>Bonhams &amp; Butterfields is delighted to offer for sale by auction nothing less than one of the most charismatic Grand Prix racing cars ever built &mdash; the 1939 Auto Union &#8216;D-Type&#8217; with rear-mounted 3-liter twin-stage supercharged V12-cylinder engine. The annual collector&#8217;s motorcar car auction is set for August 14, 2009 in Carmel, CA.</em></p>
<p><em>This legendary racing car &#8211; absolutely confirmed today as chassis number &#8217;19&#8242; &#8211; was driven to placing finishes in the 1939 Grand Prix racing season. Handled by Auto Union factory team drivers Rudolf Hasse and Hans Stuck, this pioneering rear-engined Grand Prix projectile finished fifth in the German EifelRennen event on the North Circuit of the Nurburgring, and sixth in the Grand Prix de l&#8217;Automobile Club de France around the super fast public road course at Reims-Gueux.</em></p>
<p><em>The 1938-39 V12-cylinder Auto Union racing car &mdash; retrospectively classified postwar as the Chemnitz company&#8217;s &#8216;D-Type&#8217; model &mdash; was developed to meet a new set of international regulations governing Grand Prix racing. They specified a maximum engine capacity of 3-liters and a minimum weight limit of 850-kilograms. The &#8216;D-Type&#8217; Auto Union was based upon a highly sophisticated and advanced new chassis design, featuring de Dion rear suspension and its fuel load centralized in pannier tanks hung along each side, within the wheelbase. The 3-cam V12-cylinder engine developed some 420bhp in 1938 single-stage supercharged form, rising to some 485bhp at 7,000rpm when two-stage supercharging was adopted for 1939.</em></p>
<p><em>That final pre-war season &mdash; whose leading cars such as this Auto Union represent the absolute high-tide of &#8216;Silver Arrows&#8217; period technology &#8211; then opened on May 21 with the EifelRennen, at Germany&#8217;s Nurburgring, where Nuvolari&#8217;s &#8216;D-Type&#8217; finished second and Rudi Hasse fifth in chassis &#8217;19&#8242; now being offered by Bonhams &amp; Butterfields.</em></p>
<p><em>During the 1939 racing season, Auto Union deployed 11 &#8216;D-Type&#8217; chassis in the six significant Grand Prix Formula events contested. In addition to Nuvolari&#8217;s second place in the EifelRennen, Hasse finished second in the Belgian GP, before his team-mates H.P. &#8216;Happy&#8217; Muller and &#8216;Schorsch&#8217; Meier brought the team a wonderful 1-2 success in the French race at Reims-Gueux.</em></p>
<p><em>It was there that chassis &#8217;19&#8242; raced for the last time, driven by Hans Stuck, the veteran Austrian star. In his hands, this &#8216;D-Type&#8217; Auto Union completed the works team&#8217;s day by finishing sixth.</em></p>
<p><em>Today, Auto Union &#8216;D-Type&#8217; chassis &#8217;19&#8242; is the only proven surviving Grand Prix car of its type with contemporary 1939 racing history. It is one of the classic car world&#8217;s most charismatic machines, and is exquisitely well-restored to running order. In a world hungry for genuine intrinsic value, it has much to commend it.</em></p>
<p><em><strong>Post-war Myth and Mystery</strong></em></p>
<p><em>For nearly half a century the survival in Communist Russia of ex-works German &#8216;Silver Arrow&#8217; Grand Prix cars from the 1930s seemed little more than unproven myth. The search for any such cars from Mercedes-Benz or &mdash; much more so &mdash; Auto Union &#8211; was regarded as historic motor sport&#8217;s quest for the Holy Grail. While several 1930s Mercedes-Benz Grand Prix cars survived at the Stuttgart factory and in private Western hands, the only known Auto Union was a sectioned 1936 V16 model exhibited in the Deutsches Museum in Munich.</em></p>
<p><em>It was known that the surviving Auto Union team cars had been expropriated by Soviet forces in the Autumn of 1945. In fact, no fewer than 13 Auto Union cars were transported by train from the company&#8217;s devastated factories in Zwickau and Chemnitz, Lower Saxony, in what was to become Communist East Germany.</em></p>
<p><em>They were delivered to the Soviet Union&#8217;s NAMI motor industry research institute in Moscow, where early in 1946 a working group of engineers was established to investigate these dazzlingly high-tech German designs. Four Auto Unions &#8211; one with wheel-enveloping streamlined bodywork &mdash; were dismantled and effectively destroyed during the NAMI group&#8217;s inspection and analysis.</em></p>
<p><em>Two sister cars were delivered to Moscow&#8217;s ZIS production car factory for parallel examination and research. One, a V16-cylinder, was subsequently scrapped. The other &#8211; which was a hill-climb car comprising a 16-cylinder-type chassis powered by the later V12 engine &#8211; escaped destruction, eventually passing into a museum in Riga, Latvia, and subsequently to Audi.</em></p>
<p><em>Four other Auto Unions &#8211; three 1938-39 V12 Grand Prix cars, plus one streamliner &#8211; went to the GAS factory in Gorky (now renamed Nizhniy Novgorod) where some components were cannibalized for use in GAS, Moskvich and ZIL-based competition cars. When one staffer required a trailer, a stripped Grand Prix chassis frame was cut in half to suit&#8230;!</em></p>
<p><em>Generally, the Soviet technicians were unable to run the cars, with the exception of one V12 &#8216;D-Type&#8217; at Gorky, whose tanks were found to contain the correct sophisticated German fuel mix. This car was started successfully and tested at high speed, only for driver Leonid Sokolov to find his path obstructed by encroaching roadside crowds. He lost control under braking, and crashed into them, killing as many as 18.</em></p>
<p><em>Around 1950, two surviving open-wheel GP Auto Unions and one 16-cylinder streamliner were assigned to engineer Vladimir Nikitin in Kharkov, Ukraine. He cannibalized the streamliner to build his &#8216;Kharkov&#8217; racing car, powered by a 4-cylinder Podeba street engine. A fellow Ukrainian engineer, Eduard Lorent, also benefited from Auto Union study in building his small- capacity &#8216;Kharkov L1&#8242; and &#8216;L2&#8242; racing cars.</em></p>
<p><em>One complete open-wheeler chassis, the trailer-frame and their major mechanical components survived surplus to Nikitin and Lorent&#8217;s requirements, and after the collapse of the Soviet Union, Russian-born American Paul Karassik &mdash; a Florida-based antique car enthusiast &mdash; spent much time in Russia hunting down the truth of the Auto Union legend. Karassik accumulated an incredible treasure-trove of pre-war Grand Prix car components, including Auto Union serial &#8217;19&#8242;s complete, unspoiled chassis and the late-model V12-cylinder engine which powers it today. Mr Karassik entrusted restoration of this car to the renowned British &#8216;Silver Arrow&#8217; specialists, Crosthwaite &amp; Gardiner in Buxted, England, and they rebuilt it in as-original two-stage supercharged form.</em></p>
<p><em>Seventy years later, Auto Union &#8216;D-Type&#8217; chassis &#8217;19&#8242; will star in the Bonhams &amp; Butterfields sale at Quail Lodge in California on August 14, when it is expected to realize in excess of $8-million. It represents a uniquely attractive investment in cutting-edge history, and will likely provide a new owner with a unique and enduring entry ticket to every one of the classic car world&#8217;s most prestigious events.</em></p>

				<h3><a href="http://www.classiccar.co.nz/news-7900/porsche-vw-merger-could-see-revival-of-auto-union-name" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Porsche VW merger could see revival of Auto Union name">Porsche VW merger could see revival of Auto Union name</a></h3>
				<div class="postmeta">May 18th, 2009 by NZ Classic Car</div>

				 					<p><img class="alignnone size-full wp-image-5585" src="http://www.classiccar.co.nz/wp-content/uploads/2009/05/Auto-Union-Silver-Arrow-fq.jpg" alt="Auto Union Silver Arrow fq" width="455" height="275" /></p>
<p>According to recent reports, the Auto Union name could be revived with the  merger of Porsche and Volkswagen. During the Great Depression in 1932 the Auto  Union moniker was coined when four German automakers &mdash; Audi, DKW, Horch and  Wanderer &mdash; merged to create what&#8217;s now effectively present-day Audi. The four  interlocked rings of the Audi logo represented the alliance, but the Auto Union  name does carry some negativity.</p>
<p>First made famous by the Silver Arrow grand prix cars of the &#8217;30s, and made  infamous when Adolf Hitler commissioned Ferdinand Porsche in 1933 to create a  German competitor to dominate the series, the name still carries an unfortunate  stigma post-WWII. Apparently the chairman of the Volkswagen Group, Dr. Ferdinand  Piech, suggested the title as an umbrella corporation that would incorporate the  two automakers, but considering its history and reputation, the name&#8217;s  resurrection is a tough call to make.</p>
