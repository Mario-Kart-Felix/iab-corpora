<p>A <b>hatchback</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Car_body_style" title="Car body style" class="mw-redirect">car body configuration</a> with a rear door<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-merriam-webster_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merriam-webster-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> that swings upward to provide access to a cargo area. Hatchbacks may feature fold-down second row seating, where the interior can be flexibly reconfigured to prioritize passenger vs. cargo volume. Hatchbacks may feature <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">two- or three-box</a> design.</p>
<p>While early examples of the body configuration can be traced to the 1930s, the <a href="/wiki/Merriam-Webster" title="Merriam-Webster">Merriam-Webster</a> dictionary dates the term itself to 1970.<sup id="cite_ref-merriam-webster_2-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-merriam-webster-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The hatchback body style has been marketed worldwide on cars ranging in size from <a href="/wiki/Supermini_car" title="Supermini car" class="mw-redirect">superminis</a> to <a href="/wiki/Small_family_car" title="Small family car" class="mw-redirect">small family cars</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Executive_cars" title="Executive cars" class="mw-redirect">executive cars</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Toyota_Yaris_(XP130)_%E2%80%93_Heckansicht,_21._Juli_2012,_Heiligenhaus.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Toyota_Yaris_%28XP130%29_%E2%80%93_Heckansicht%2C_21._Juli_2012%2C_Heiligenhaus.jpg/220px-Toyota_Yaris_%28XP130%29_%E2%80%93_Heckansicht%2C_21._Juli_2012%2C_Heiligenhaus.jpg" width="220" height="127" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Toyota_Yaris_%28XP130%29_%E2%80%93_Heckansicht%2C_21._Juli_2012%2C_Heiligenhaus.jpg/330px-Toyota_Yaris_%28XP130%29_%E2%80%93_Heckansicht%2C_21._Juli_2012%2C_Heiligenhaus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Toyota_Yaris_%28XP130%29_%E2%80%93_Heckansicht%2C_21._Juli_2012%2C_Heiligenhaus.jpg/440px-Toyota_Yaris_%28XP130%29_%E2%80%93_Heckansicht%2C_21._Juli_2012%2C_Heiligenhaus.jpg 2x" /></a>

Toyota Yaris III Hatchback</div>

<p>Hatchbacks may be described as <i>three-door</i> (two entry doors and the hatch) or <i>five-door</i> (four entry doors and the hatch) cars. A <a href="/wiki/Model_range" title="Model range" class="mw-redirect">model range</a> may include multiple configurations, as with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Focus_(North_America)#Body_styles_and_trims" title="Ford Focus (North America)" class="mw-redirect">2001–2007 Ford Focus</a> which offered sedan (ZX4), wagon (ZXW), and three or five-door hatchback (ZX3 and ZX5) models. The models typically share a <a href="/wiki/Automobile_platform" title="Automobile platform">platform</a>, drivetrain and bodywork forward of the <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">A-pillar</a>. Hatchbacks may have a removable rigid parcel shelf,<sup id="cite_ref-Hilliers_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilliers-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> liftable with the tailgate, or flexible roll-up <a href="/wiki/Tonneau_cover" title="Tonneau cover" class="mw-redirect">tonneau cover</a> to cover the cargo space behind the rear seats.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hatchback_vs._station_wagon">Hatchback vs. station wagon</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hatchback vs. station wagon">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hatchback vs. station wagon" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Hatch_and_wagon_with_pillar_layout.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Hatch_and_wagon_with_pillar_layout.jpg/220px-Hatch_and_wagon_with_pillar_layout.jpg" width="220" height="85" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Hatch_and_wagon_with_pillar_layout.jpg/330px-Hatch_and_wagon_with_pillar_layout.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Hatch_and_wagon_with_pillar_layout.jpg/440px-Hatch_and_wagon_with_pillar_layout.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Hatch_and_wagon_with_pillar_layout.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf12/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Diagram of a five-door hatchback <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">(two-box)</a> superimposed over the station wagon <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">(two-box)</a> from the same <a href="/wiki/Model_range" title="Model range" class="mw-redirect">model range</a>—in this case, both with a <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">D-pillar</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Both station wagons and hatchbacks typically feature a <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">two-box design</a> configuration, with one shared, flexible, interior volume for passengers and cargo<sup id="cite_ref-Vehicle_Dynamics_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vehicle_Dynamics-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AutomotiveTech_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AutomotiveTech-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup>—and a rear door for cargo access.<sup id="cite_ref-cdn2_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cdn2-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Further distinctions are highly variable:</p>
<p><b>Pillars:</b> Both configurations typically feature <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">A, B &amp; C pillars</a>, station wagons more likely also feature a <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">D pillar</a> as well.</p>
<p><b>Cargo Volume:</b> Station wagons prioritize passenger and cargo volume—with windows aside the cargo volume. Of the two body styles, a station wagon's roof (viewed in profile) more likely extends to the very rearmost of the vehicle, enclosing a full-height cargo volume<sup id="cite_ref-AutomotiveTech_8-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AutomotiveTech-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup>—a hatchback roof (especially a <a href="/wiki/Liftback" title="Liftback" class="mw-redirect">liftback</a> roof) might more likely rake down steeply behind the <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">C-Pillar</a>, prioritizing style<sup id="cite_ref-Hilliers_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilliers-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> over interior volume, with shorter rear overhang and with smaller windows (or no windows) aside the cargo volume.</p>
<p><b>Cargo floor contour:</b> Favoring cargo capacity, a station wagon may prioritize a fold-flat floor, where a hatchback would more likely allow a cargo floor with pronounced contour (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Mini_(BMW)" title="Mini (BMW)" class="mw-redirect">new Mini</a> or the sixth generation <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fiesta#Fiesta_Mark_VI_.282008.E2.80.93.29:The_sixth_generation" title="Ford Fiesta">Ford Fiesta</a>).</p>
<p><b>Seating:</b> Station wagons have two or three rows of seats (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Taurus" title="Ford Taurus">Ford Taurus</a> wagons) while hatchbacks have one<sup id="cite_ref-Vehicle_Dynamics_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vehicle_Dynamics-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/MGB_GT" title="MGB GT" class="mw-redirect">MGB GT</a>) or two rows of seats.</p>
<p><b>Rear suspension:</b> A station wagon may include reconfigured rear suspension for additional load capacity<sup id="cite_ref-Hilliers_6-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilliers-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> and to minimize intrusion into the cargo volume, (e.g., worldwide versions of the first generation <a href="/wiki/Ford_Focus_(international)" title="Ford Focus (international)" class="mw-redirect">Ford Focus</a>).</p>
<p><b>Rear Door:</b> Hatchbacks typically feature a top-hinged <a href="/wiki/Liftgate" title="Liftgate" class="mw-redirect">liftgate</a> for cargo access, with variations from a single liftgate to a complex tailgate that can function either as a full tailgate or as a trunk lid (e.g., the 2008 <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Superb#B6_Superb_.28Typ_3T.29_.282008-present.29" title="Škoda Superb">Škoda Superb</a>'s TwinDoor). Station wagons also have numerous <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon#Tailgate_evolution" title="Station wagon">tailgate configurations</a>. Typically, a hatchback's hatch or liftgate does not extend down to the bumper, as on wagons — with exceptions including the Skoda Superb.</p>
<p>Automotive journalist <a href="/wiki/Dan_Neil" title="Dan Neil">Dan Neil</a>, in a 2002 <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times" class="mw-redirect">New York Times</a></i> report described verticality of the rear cargo door as the prime distinction between a hatchback and a station wagon: "Where you break the roofline, at what angle, defines the spirit of the vehicle," he said. "You could have a 90-degree break in the back and have a station wagon."<sup id="cite_ref-nyt1_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Liftback">Liftback</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Liftback">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Liftback" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <b>liftback</b> is a broad marketing term for a hatchback, which incorporates a shared passenger and <a href="/wiki/Cargo" title="Cargo">cargo</a> volume, with rearmost accessibility via a rear third or fifth door, typically a top-hinged <a href="/wiki/3-way_tailgate" title="3-way tailgate" class="mw-redirect">tailgate</a>—especially where the profile aspect of the rear cargo door is more horizontal than vertical, with a sharply raked or fastback profile.<sup id="cite_ref-Vehicle_Dynamics_7-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vehicle_Dynamics-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In comparison with the hatchback the back opening area is more sloped and longer and is lifted up to open, offering more luggage space. Very similar is the "<a href="/wiki/Fastback" title="Fastback">fastback</a>", but it miss the "step" visually resembling <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">sedan</a>. Liftback is not used as a term in the UK, fastback or hatchback are used instead.</p>

<p>In 1938, <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn" title="Citroën">Citroën</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_Traction_Avant" title="Citroën Traction Avant">Traction Avant Commerciale</a><sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> initially with a two-piece tailgate, of which the upper piece hinged upwards, cutting well into the roof, and after 1954 with a one-piece top-hinged tailgate.</p>
<p>In 1946, <a href="/wiki/DeSoto_(automobile)" title="DeSoto (automobile)">DeSoto</a> marketed the Suburban, a full-size sedan of which the bootlid was still conventionally hinged below the rear window, however the passenger and luggage compartments had been merged into one large single section with a flat floor that had the fuel tank underneath it, and with folding rear seats it gave a flexibity similar to hatchbacks.<sup id="cite_ref-station_wagons_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-station_wagons-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1949, <a href="/wiki/Kaiser-Frazer" title="Kaiser-Frazer">Kaiser-Frazer</a> introduced the Vagabond and Traveler hatchbacks. Although these were styled much like the typical 1940s sedan, they incorporated an innovative split rear tailgate, folding rear seats and no separate trunk.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1953, <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin" title="Aston Martin">Aston Martin</a> marketed the <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB2/4" title="Aston Martin DB2/4">DB2</a> with a top-hinged rear tailgate, manufacturing 700 examples. Its successor, the 1958 <a href="/wiki/Aston_Martin_DB_Mark_III" title="Aston Martin DB Mark III">DB Mark III</a> also offered a folding rear seat. The 1954 <a href="/wiki/AC_Aceca" title="AC Aceca">AC Aceca</a> and later <a href="/wiki/Aceca-Bristol" title="Aceca-Bristol" class="mw-redirect">Aceca-Bristol</a> from <a href="/wiki/AC_Cars" title="AC Cars">AC Cars</a> had a similar hatch tailgate, though only 320 were built.</p>

<p>In 1961 Renault introduced the <a href="/wiki/Renault_4" title="Renault 4">Renault 4</a>, a small car with a top-hinged tailgate incorporating the rear window, and only short side windows between C &amp; D-<a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">pillars</a> aside the luggage space and a steep angle from roof to rear bumper. Although during its production run the R4 was called a small <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a> - even after the term <i>hatchback</i> appeared around 1970 - with over eight million cars built, this was the first mass-produced hatchback.<br />
In 1964, <a href="/wiki/Fiat" title="Fiat">Fiat</a> subsidiary <a href="/wiki/Autobianchi" title="Autobianchi">Autobianchi</a> marketed the <a href="/wiki/Autobianchi_Primula" title="Autobianchi Primula">Primula</a> hatchback. In 1965, Renault marketed the <a href="/wiki/Renault_16" title="Renault 16">Renault 16</a>, a mid-market hatchback design with a folding rear seat.</p>
<p>In 1965, the <a href="/wiki/MG_MGB" title="MG MGB">MGB-GT</a> was launched with a hatchback designed by Pininfarina, the first volume-production sports car so equipped. In 1966 <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a> offered a shortened three-door coupe version of their <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_204" title="Peugeot 204">204</a>, essentially a hatchback. In 1967, the <a href="/wiki/Simca_1100" title="Simca 1100">Simca 1100</a> used a transverse engine and gearbox layout, and incorporated a hatchback without side windows at the C-pillar. In 1969, <a href="/wiki/British_Leyland" title="British Leyland">British Leyland</a> launched <a href="/wiki/Austin_Maxi" title="Austin Maxi">Austin Maxi</a>, a five-speed, transverse front-wheel drive hatchback.</p>
<p>In 1973, <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a> marketed the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Passat" title="Volkswagen Passat">Passat/Dasher</a> hatchback, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Golf" title="Volkswagen Golf">Golf/Rabbit</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Giorgetto_Giugiaro" title="Giorgetto Giugiaro">Giorgetto Giugiaro</a>, as well as the <a href="/wiki/Audi_50" title="Audi 50">Audi 50</a>/<a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Polo" title="Volkswagen Polo">Volkswagen Polo</a> in 1974. <a href="/wiki/Sports_cars" title="Sports cars" class="mw-redirect">Sports cars</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Jaguar_E-Type" title="Jaguar E-Type">Jaguar E-Type</a> with its side-hinged opening, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_2000GT" title="Toyota 2000GT">Toyota 2000GT</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Datsun_240Z" title="Datsun 240Z" class="mw-redirect">Datsun 240Z</a> carried rear tailgates, with one row of seats, and Ford relaunched the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Capri" title="Ford Capri">Capri</a> with a hatchback in 1974. In the 1970s, the <a href="/wiki/Rover_SD1" title="Rover SD1">Rover SD1</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renault_30" title="Renault 30" class="mw-redirect">Renault 30</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saab_900" title="Saab 900">Saab 900</a> introduced the hatchback style into the <a href="/wiki/Executive_car" title="Executive car">executive car</a> market.</p>
<p>The 1980s <a href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford" class="mw-redirect">Ford</a> launched the <a href="/wiki/Front-wheel_drive" title="Front-wheel drive">front-wheel drive</a> <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort_(Europe)" title="Ford Escort (Europe)">Ford Escort</a> hatchback, followed by the <a href="/wiki/Lancia_Delta" title="Lancia Delta">Lancia Delta</a> and many others. The 1983 <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Uno" title="Fiat Uno">Fiat Uno</a> designed by <a href="/wiki/Giorgetto_Giugiaro" title="Giorgetto Giugiaro">Giorgetto Giugiaro</a>, its tall, square body utilising a <a href="/wiki/Kamm_tail" title="Kamm tail" class="mw-redirect">Kamm tail</a> achieved a low drag coefficient of 0.34 won much praise for airy interior space and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_economy_in_automobiles" title="Fuel economy in automobiles">fuel economy</a>. It incorporated many packaging lessons learnt from Giugiaro's 1978 <a href="/wiki/Lancia_Megagamma" title="Lancia Megagamma">Lancia Megagamma</a> concept car (the first modern people carrier / <a href="/wiki/Minivan" title="Minivan">MPV</a> / mini-van) but miniaturised. Its tall car / high seating packaging is imitated by every small car today. It reversed the trend for lower and lower built cars. It showed that not just low sleek cars could be aerodynamic, but small, roomy, boxy well packaged cars could be too. In 1984 it was voted <a href="/wiki/European_Car_of_the_Year" title="European Car of the Year">European Car of the Year</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>More hatchback cars followed over the decade, including the updated <a href="/wiki/Opel_Kadett" title="Opel Kadett">Opel Kadett</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austin_Maestro" title="Austin Maestro">Austin Maestro</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Astra" title="Vauxhall Astra">Vauxhall Astra</a>, <a href="/wiki/Renault_19" title="Renault 19">Renault 19</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Tipo" title="Fiat Tipo">Fiat Tipo</a>, and second generation <a href="/wiki/Rover_200" title="Rover 200" class="mw-redirect">Rover 200</a>. Ford replaced the long-running Escort with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Focus_(International)" title="Ford Focus (International)" class="mw-redirect">Ford Focus</a> in 1998, featuring a model range with sedan, station wagon, and three and five door hatchbacks.</p>
<p>The hatchback was seen as a leap forward in practicality in Europe. From the 1960s it was adopted as a standard feature on most European cars, with saloons declining in popularity apart from at the top of the market where it was unpopular, where a saloon is seen as a sign of status. The <a href="/wiki/Ford_Granada_(Europe)" title="Ford Granada (Europe)">Ford Granada</a> Mk3 / <a href="/wiki/Ford_Scorpio" title="Ford Scorpio">Ford Scorpio</a> executive car, that was launched in 1985 only as a hatchback, had to be relaunched in saloon and estate form. The hatchback was discontinued years before the other variants. Small <a href="/wiki/Economy_car" title="Economy car">economy cars</a> that were more limited in load carrying ability than larger cars benefited most - long light loads like furniture could be hung out of the back of the car.</p>
<p>Early examples of hatchbacks in Europe started with the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Ritmo" title="Fiat Ritmo">Fiat Strada</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Golf_Mk1" title="Volkswagen Golf Mk1">Volkswagen Golf</a>. <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a>'s <a href="/wiki/BMW_Compact" title="BMW Compact" class="mw-redirect">3-series hatchback</a> was offered from 1995–99.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> The liftback version of the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_C-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz C-Class">Mercedes-Benz C-Class</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_CLC-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz CLC-Class" class="mw-redirect">Sportcoupé</a>, was sold in North America from 2002 to 2005, lacking many amenities of German luxury imports (especially a Mercedes).<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Audi subsequently marketd the <a href="/wiki/Audi_A3#Second_generation_A3_.288P.29:_2003.E2.80.93present" title="Audi A3">Audi A3</a> In North America. The <a href="/wiki/Mini_Hatch" title="Mini Hatch">Mini Hatch</a> range incorporates a hatchback design.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: North America">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: North America" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">American Motors Corporation</a> (AMC) marketed the subcompact <a href="/wiki/AMC_Gremlin" title="AMC Gremlin">Gremlin</a> from 1970, in a single hatchback <a href="/wiki/Kammback" title="Kammback">Kammback</a> body design.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The Gremlin used the <a href="/wiki/AMC_Hornet" title="AMC Hornet">AMC Hornet</a> <a href="/wiki/Automobile_platform" title="Automobile platform">automobile platform</a>, but its abrupt hatchback rear end cut the car's overall length from 179 to 161 inches (4,500 to 4,100 mm). American Motors added a semi-<a href="/wiki/Fastback" title="Fastback">fastback</a> hatchback version to its larger <a href="/wiki/Compact_car" title="Compact car">compact-sized</a> Hornet line for the 1973 model year.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The design and fold-down rear seat more than doubled cargo space and the Hornet was claimed to be the "first compact hatchback" manufactured by U.S. automaker.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Additional utility from the car's hatchback, space, and long load floor was that it could be turned into a "mini-camper" with the optional factory "Hornet Hutch" tent accessory.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

</div>
<p>Introduced by AMC in 1975, "like recent European variations of the theme, the <a href="/wiki/AMC_Pacer" title="AMC Pacer">Pacer</a> had a rear door or hatchback, which further increased its utility".<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> For 1977, AMC added a longer Pacer model with a wagon-type configuration describing its large rear "hatch" as one of the car's three doors, all having different sizes.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The Hornet's hatchback body design was continued in the redesigned "luxury" <a href="/wiki/AMC_Concord" title="AMC Concord">Concord</a> line for 1978<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> and 1979, in a "sporty model designed for performance-oriented buyers".<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/AMC_Spirit" title="AMC Spirit">AMC Spirit</a> replaced the Gremlin starting with the 1979 models and was available in two designs, both featuring rear doors: a hatchback "sedan" and a semi-fastback "liftback" version.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Built in AMC's <a href="/wiki/Kenosha,_Wisconsin" title="Kenosha, Wisconsin">Kenosha, Wisconsin</a> factories, the 1984–1987 <a href="/wiki/Renault_Alliance" title="Renault Alliance">Encore</a> was a two and four-door hatchback based on the European <a href="/wiki/Renault_11" title="Renault 11" class="mw-redirect">Renault 11</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Corporation" title="Chrysler Corporation" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler Corporation</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Omni" title="Dodge Omni">Dodge Omni</a> and <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Horizon" title="Plymouth Horizon" class="mw-redirect">Plymouth Horizon</a> hatchbacks in 1978.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> These were followed by the <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Charger" title="Dodge Charger">Dodge Charger</a>/<a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Turismo" title="Plymouth Turismo" class="mw-redirect">Plymouth Turismo</a>. They released the liftback <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Daytona" title="Dodge Daytona">Dodge Daytona</a>/<a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Laser" title="Chrysler Laser" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler Laser</a> in 1984, and then the <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Shadow" title="Dodge Shadow">Dodge Shadow</a>/<a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Sundance" title="Plymouth Sundance">Plymouth Sundance</a>—as well as the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_LeBaron" title="Chrysler LeBaron">LeBaron GTS</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Lancer" title="Dodge Lancer">Dodge Lancer</a> hatchbacks. <a href="/wiki/Captive_import" title="Captive import">Captive import</a> subcompact models included the <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Colt" title="Dodge Colt">Dodge Colt</a> and the almost identical Plymouth Champ.</p>
<p>Dodge replaced its <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Neon" title="Dodge Neon" class="mw-redirect">Neon</a> sedan with the <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Caliber" title="Dodge Caliber">Dodge Caliber</a>, a platform on which the <a href="/wiki/Jeep" title="Jeep">Jeep</a> branded based its <a href="/wiki/Jeep_Compass" title="Jeep Compass">Compass</a> and <a href="/wiki/Jeep_Patriot" title="Jeep Patriot">Patriot</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford Motor Company</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Pinto" title="Ford Pinto">Pinto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Bobcat" title="Mercury Bobcat" class="mw-redirect">Bobcat</a> in 1972. The German-built <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Capri" title="Mercury Capri">Mercury Capri II</a> hatchbacks were imported to U.S. Lincoln-Mercury dealers for the 1976–1977 model years, and the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fiesta" title="Ford Fiesta">Ford Fiesta</a> hatchback was imported for U.S. Ford dealers later in the decade. Ford offered a hatchback version of its third-generation <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort_(North_America)" title="Ford Escort (North America)">Escort</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Ford_Mustang_(third_generation)" title="Ford Mustang (third generation)">third generation Mustang</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Capri" title="Mercury Capri">Mercury Capri</a> introduced in 1979 were offered in hatchback models. Between 1988 and 1993 Ford marketed the imported <a href="/wiki/Ford_Festiva" title="Ford Festiva">Festiva</a> subcompact hatchback that was later restyled and renamed the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Aspire" title="Ford Aspire" class="mw-redirect">Aspire</a> for the 1994 through 1997 model years.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1973_Chevrolet_Vega_Hatchback.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5f/1973_Chevrolet_Vega_Hatchback.jpg/220px-1973_Chevrolet_Vega_Hatchback.jpg" width="220" height="139" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/5/5f/1973_Chevrolet_Vega_Hatchback.jpg/330px-1973_Chevrolet_Vega_Hatchback.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/5/5f/1973_Chevrolet_Vega_Hatchback.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1973_Chevrolet_Vega_Hatchback.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf12/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
1973 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Vega" title="Chevrolet Vega">Chevrolet Vega</a> Hatchback</div>

<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Vega" title="Chevrolet Vega">Chevrolet Vega</a>, introduced in September 1970, was the first hatchback model from <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a>. Over a million Vega hatchbacks were produced for the 1971–1977 model years accounting for about half of the Vega's total production.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">GM</a> introduced <a href="/wiki/Badge_engineering" title="Badge engineering">rebadged</a> Vega hatchback variants, the 1973–1977 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Vega#Pontiac_Astre" title="Chevrolet Vega">Pontiac Astre</a> and the 1978 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Vega#Chevy_Monza_.27S.27_.26_Monza_Wagon" title="Chevrolet Vega">Chevrolet Monza S</a>.</p>
<p>The Vega-derived <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Monza" title="Chevrolet Monza">Chevrolet Monza 2+2</a>, <a href="/wiki/Buick_Skyhawk" title="Buick Skyhawk">Buick Skyhawk</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile_Starfire#1975-1980" title="Oldsmobile Starfire">Oldsmobile Starfire</a> introduced for the 1975 model year, were produced exclusively as hatchbacks with the <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Sunbird" title="Pontiac Sunbird">Pontiac Sunbird</a> hatchback introduced for the 1977 model year. All were produced through 1980.</p>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Nova" title="Chevrolet Nova" class="mw-redirect">Chevrolet Nova</a> hatchback was introduced for the 1973 model year, and was offered through 1979, another hatchback Nova was reintroduced in the 80's based on the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Sprinter" title="Toyota Sprinter">Toyota Sprinter</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Chevette" title="Chevrolet Chevette">Chevrolet Chevette</a> was introduced in 1975 as a two-door hatchback, engineered in cooperation with <a href="/wiki/Isuzu" title="Isuzu" class="mw-redirect">Isuzu</a>. A four-door hatchback on a longer wheelbase was introduced with the 1978 models. In early 1979 the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Citation" title="Chevrolet Citation">Chevrolet Citation</a> was introduced as a 1980 model offered in 2 and 4-door hatchbacks continuing through the 1987 model year. In the 1981, General Motors included a hatchback model as part of its J-car series that included the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Cavalier" title="Chevrolet Cavalier">Chevrolet Cavalier</a>. Chevrolet offered captive import hatchbacks built by <a href="/wiki/Suzuki" title="Suzuki">Suzuki</a> and Izuzu. The <a href="/wiki/NUMMI" title="NUMMI">NUMMI</a> U.S.-made <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Nova" title="Chevrolet Nova" class="mw-redirect">Chevrolet Nova</a> was also offered in a hatchback model in 1987 and 1988. Its replacement, the Geo <a href="/wiki/Prizm" title="Prizm" class="mw-redirect">Prizm</a>, was also available in a hatchback model and the domestic designed <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Corsica" title="Chevrolet Corsica">Chevrolet Corsica</a> was briefly available in a hatchback version.</p>
<p>The third generation <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Camaro_(third_generation)" title="Chevrolet Camaro (third generation)">Chevrolet Camaro</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Firebird" title="Pontiac Firebird">Pontiac Firebird</a> produced for the 1982–1992 model years, featured a curved glass hatchback liftgate. GM marketed a series of hatchbacks in North America as a joint venture with Suzuki, the <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_Cultus" title="Suzuki Cultus">Swift/Metro/Firefly</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Chevrolet offered a longer wheelbase, hatchback vesion of the Malibu, the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Malibu" title="Chevrolet Malibu">Malibu Maxx</a> from 2004 to 2007. In 2008, GM introduced the 3-door and 5-door Belgian-assembled <a href="/wiki/Saturn_Astra" title="Saturn Astra" class="mw-redirect">Saturn Astra</a>. Chevrolet added a hatchback version of its Korean-built <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Aveo" title="Chevrolet Aveo">Aveo</a> in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Corvette" title="Chevrolet Corvette">Chevrolet Corvette</a> was first offered with an opening rear glass hatch for the 1982 Collector Edition model.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> It was adopted on all Corvette Coupes beginning in 1984, with the fourth generation models.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">Japan</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Japan">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Japan" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1971_Datsun_240Z_Series_I.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/1971_Datsun_240Z_Series_I.jpg/220px-1971_Datsun_240Z_Series_I.jpg" width="220" height="121" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/1971_Datsun_240Z_Series_I.jpg/330px-1971_Datsun_240Z_Series_I.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/1971_Datsun_240Z_Series_I.jpg/440px-1971_Datsun_240Z_Series_I.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>One of the first hatchbacks offered was the 1970 <a href="/wiki/Nissan_S30" title="Nissan S30">Nissan Fairlady Z</a>, 1972 <a href="/wiki/Honda_Civic_(first_generation)" title="Honda Civic (first generation)">Honda Civic</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Datsun_B210" title="Datsun B210" class="mw-redirect">Nissan Sunny B210</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Cherry" title="Nissan Cherry">Nissan Cherry</a>, with the Civic and Cherry offering front wheel drive powertrains. In addition to specific models of <a href="/wiki/Captive_imports" title="Captive imports" class="mw-redirect">captive imports</a> mentioned above, a number of Japanese brands have been available in the hatchback body style as a primary model: <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Pulsar" title="Nissan Pulsar">Nissan Pulsar</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Allex" title="Toyota Allex" class="mw-redirect">Toyota Allex</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Prius" title="Toyota Prius">Toyota Prius</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honda_CR-X" title="Honda CR-X">Honda CR-X</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Honda_Insight" title="Honda Insight">Honda Insight</a>. Almost all Japanese "city cars", called "<a href="/wiki/Kei_jidosha" title="Kei jidosha" class="mw-redirect">kei jidosha</a>" use a hatchback bodystyle for cargo carrying ability in a regulated vehicle size, such as the <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Minica" title="Mitsubishi Minica">Mitsubishi Minica</a>, <a href="/wiki/Honda_Life" title="Honda Life">Honda Life</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_Fronte" title="Suzuki Fronte">Suzuki Fronte</a>, <a href="/wiki/Subaru_Vivio" title="Subaru Vivio">Subaru Vivio</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Daihatsu_Mira" title="Daihatsu Mira">Daihatsu Mira</a>. Other large-sized hatchback body style vehicles include <a href="/wiki/Lexus_CT" title="Lexus CT">Lexus CT</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Murano" title="Nissan Murano">Nissan Murano</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Skyline" title="Nissan Skyline">Nissan Skyline Crossover</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Fairlady_Z" title="Nissan Fairlady Z" class="mw-redirect">Nissan Fairlady Z</a> is often classified as hatchbacks because of its styling, including the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_350Z" title="Nissan 350Z">Z33</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_370Z" title="Nissan 370Z">Z34</a>. Other sports cars with hatchback body style in Japan are <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Celica" title="Toyota Celica">Toyota Celica</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nissan_180SX" title="Nissan 180SX">Nissan 180SX</a>.</p>

<p>The first Soviet hatchback was the rear-wheel drive <a href="/wiki/IZh" title="IZh" class="mw-redirect">IZh</a>-2125 <a href="/wiki/Izh_Comby" title="Izh Comby">Kombi</a>, which entered production in 1973. This was followed in the 1980s by the front-wheel drive <a href="/wiki/Lada_Samara" title="Lada Samara">Lada Samara</a>, <a href="/wiki/ZAZ_Tavria" title="ZAZ Tavria">ZAZ Tavria</a> and <a href="/wiki/Aleko" title="Aleko">Moskvich Aleko</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="India">India</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: India">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: India" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Maruti_800_AC.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Maruti_800_AC.jpg/220px-Maruti_800_AC.jpg" width="220" height="165" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Maruti_800_AC.jpg/330px-Maruti_800_AC.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Maruti_800_AC.jpg/440px-Maruti_800_AC.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>Hatchbacks are popular in India.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Maruti_800" title="Maruti 800">Maruti 800</a> sold over 2.5 million units since its launch in 1983.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Since 2004, Maruti 800 has been overtaken by <a href="/wiki/Maruti_Alto" title="Maruti Alto" class="mw-redirect">Maruti Alto</a> as the car with highest annual sales. In March 2009, <a href="/wiki/Tata_Motors" title="Tata Motors">Tata Motors</a> launched the <a href="/wiki/Tata_Nano" title="Tata Nano">Nano</a> hatchback, the least expensive road car in the world.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_regions">Other regions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other regions">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hatchback&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other regions" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Hatchbacks have proved to be less popular in <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">South America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">Africa</a>, and some parts of <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">Asia</a> than in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>, and as a result, manufacturers have had to develop sedan versions of their small cars.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">Brazil</a>, for example, the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Duna" title="Fiat Duna">Fiat Premio</a> was developed from the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Uno" title="Fiat Uno">Fiat Uno</a> in the 1980s, with Ford and GM subsequently offering sedan versions of the <a href="/wiki/Opel_Corsa" title="Opel Corsa">Opel Corsa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fiesta" title="Ford Fiesta">Ford Fiesta</a> in the 1990s. (The first generation Opel Corsa was sold in Europe as a sedan as well as a hatchback, but proved unpopular, and the three-box sedan was not replaced in 1993). These models were also sold in <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">South Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">China</a>.</p>
