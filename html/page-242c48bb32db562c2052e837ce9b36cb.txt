<p>When it comes to racking up concours d&#8217;elegance wins and &#8220;Best in Show&#8221; ribbons, there&#8217;s generally no such thing as an inexpensive car. Investment-grade iron is generally what wins, and cars in this category can be priced at stratospheric values. Here&#8217;s where thinking outside the box helps: Instead of mortgaging the house to buy something from Europe, a savvy investor can acquire this <a title="1954 Packard Clipper" href="http://www.hemmings.com/classifieds/carsforsale/packard/clipper/1579978.html?refer=blog">1954 Packard Clipper sedan</a> for sale on Hemmings.com for about the same price as a stripped 2013 Dodge Charger. The Charger may be the more practical daily driver, but the Packard is an AACA Senior First and AACA Best in Show winner, as well as a winner at the Toronto Concours d&#8217;Elegance and at Packard Club meets. It&#8217;s described as rust-free, with a mere 26,000 miles showing on the odometer, and the chrome and trim is said to be flawless. It&#8217;s unclear if the Mackinaw Gray paint is original to the car, but it appears to be in impressive condition as well. From the seller&#8217;s description:</p>

This car has been a multiple show winner including AACA Senior First, Packard Club First, Toronto Concours d&#8217;Elegance First, AACA Best in Show. 100% rust free, looks like it just rolled off the showroom floor, flawless chrome and trim, 327-cu.in. 165 HP straight-eight engine, 3-speed transmission, radio, deluxe heater, bumper guards, exhaust deflector, stainless steel exhaust system, new wide white wall radial tires, runs and drives like new, ready for show or touring! This is truly a unique opportunity for a buyer who understands a classic car in original condition. Past trophies and other supporting documents included.</p></blockquote>

		<p>I have a theory I call the Plotkin Theorem which says that most low mileage well preserved antique automobiles are low-rent models devoid of optional equipment which matches the tastes of the sort of buyer who would save his or her car from extensive use and preserve it for untold generations. </p>
<p>How often do we see high-line models loaded with optional equipment that have sat mostly unused their entire lives? Who buys an expensive feature laden car and goes only to church on Sundays? </p>
<p>Hence this Packard, which is in my estimation superseded in cool factor only by one other car I can think of&#8230;a 60 Buick with no equipment &amp; a 3-speed stick on the tree.</p>
<p>For those who are not aware, the Packard Ultra-matic automatic transmission in its various forms was underdeveloped and unreliable. Many antique automatic transmission rebuilders treat it like a skunk. So this Packard, aside from being few and far between will make someone one nice hobby car, easy to work on and good to drive.</p>
<p>Danny Plotkin</p>

		<p>This is an excellent question. I own 8 antiques. 3 of them are a Ford, Chevy &amp; Plymouth. 3 others are a Pontiac, Buick &amp; Cadillac They are close enough in age and therefore technology as to be fair comparisons. </p>
<p>Knowing these cars as well as my own body I can say that there is a big difference in the substance of the higher end cars compared to the lower. Chevrolets and Fords for example don&#8217;t use as good a seat with the same amount of springs and/or foam. The weight of carpets differs. The better cars use stainless steel trim (in the case of Cadillacs, Lincolns &amp; Packards stainless steel flash chromed) where lesser cars make due with anodized aluminum. Chevy and Ford used cheap power ram power steering whereas the better cars used integral power boxes. The better cars would get better transmissions (although debatable) Fordomatic vs Turbodrive, Powerglide vs HydraMatic). And while a small block Chevy was a fine engine, a Cadillac motor was better balanced and smoother in operation. Better cars had more instruments and less idiot lights, and so on.</p>
<p>I cannot speak to what might make the subject Packard better than the same year Chevy, but I don&#8217;t doubt that of the two of them in similar mechanical condition, the Packard would be the smoother, more comfortable and better driving car.</p>
<p>I think in mid century days you got far more car when you spent more money. Today, a Lexus ES350 or what ever they are called is not significantly discernible from its humble Camry cousin. </p>
