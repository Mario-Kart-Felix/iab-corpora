    <p><b>One Six, So Sixes for All</b></p><p>Buyers who equate power with cylinder count might scoff at the XTS&rsquo;s V-6. Every XTS will feature Cadillac&rsquo;s direct-injection 3.6-liter V-6 with 304 hp and 264 lb-ft of torque. Fuel economy comes in at 17 mpg city and 28 highway (17/27 highway for all-wheel-drive versions). Power is adequate, although finding the power requires sweeping the tachometer past 4000 rpm&mdash;torque peaks at 5200 rpm. Customers raised on big displacement likely will never work their engines that hard. The automatic travels fluidly through its six gears and never draws attention to itself. There&rsquo;s no sport program for the gearbox, but there is a manual mode, complete with paddle shifters on the back of the steering wheel should you decide to pretend you bought a CTS-V.</p><p>There&rsquo;s no mistaking the XTS&rsquo;s interior for that of the CTS, or any other Cadillac of the past. Top-dog Platinum models (price of entry: $59,080) wrap the instrument and door panels in soft leather; the other three trim levels (Standard, Luxury, and Premium) get synthetic hides that look convincing. The front seats are plush, but the headrests smack the back of your head like a homeroom bully. We weren&rsquo;t huge fans of the aforementioned virtual gauges, which appear on a 12.3-inch LCD display. There is the option of four gauge layouts, but unless you want to perform whiz-bang tricks like swiping nav screens from the center stack to the cluster, there really is nothing wrong with the standard analog gauges. Clean, original, and elegant, this is arguably the best interior Cadillac has ever done.</p><p>Clean and elegant also applies to most of the exterior design. Sharp angles and creases are out and subtle folds are in, likely to appease a Chinese market that favors softer designs. Getting bystanders to notice your XTS is the sole task of the shiny and massive grille. Aside from that flashy piece, the brightwork and detailing are tasteful. There&rsquo;s even a bit of the Rolls-Royce Phantom in the C-pillar.</p>

<p><b>Some Things Haven&rsquo;t Changed</b></p><p>Big American luxury has changed. It&rsquo;s not very big anymore, there&rsquo;s no V-8, and it doesn&rsquo;t float or conceal the road. But while there might not be a &ldquo;Body by Fisher&rdquo; badge on the XTS, it does keep a few old traditions alive. It&rsquo;s substantial looking, it&rsquo;s filled with gadgets, it has a spacious back seat, and its trunk looks lonely and barren when devoid of golf clubs. Unlike the <a href="/features/dissected-2013-cadillac-ats-feature" target="_self">upcoming ATS</a> and the CTS, the XTS doesn&rsquo;t go directly after the European competition. Its handling and interior execution move it closer to the European brands, but it&rsquo;s not aimed directly at the big German sedans. A <a href="http://blog.caranddriver.com/cadillac-unveils-new-cue-infotainment-system-ahead-of-debut-in-xts-we-try-it/" target="_self">RWD car already tapped to supplant the XTS</a> at the top of Caddy&rsquo;s lineup is due in 2014 and may offer an optional V-8; this model will more directly target those cars. In the meantime, the XTS seems content to be the last, big American luxury car.</p><div id="verdict_box" class="article_sidebar">
