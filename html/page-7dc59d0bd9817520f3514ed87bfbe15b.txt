            <td><h1 class="pageHeading">Universal Compression Ignition</h1>
              <span class="category_desc"><p>Universal products for compression ignition (diesel cycle) engines.</p><p>The type of operation that results when a high octane fuel is supplied to a diesel engine so that a premixed fuel-air mixture enters the combustion chamber similar to an Otto Cycle engine.&nbsp; This is commonly done for stationary engines used for power generation when natural gas is used as fuel premixed with air.</p><p>It is possible to use LPG in an automotive CI engine but this is very dangerous to the life of the engine if it is not done properly.&nbsp;&nbsp;The critical compression ratio (CCR) of&nbsp;a fuel is the minimum CR&nbsp;which&nbsp;will cause the fuel to self-ignite from the heat of compression.&nbsp; The CCR for propane is ~12:1 and for natural gas (methane) is ~15:1.&nbsp; Since automotive diesel and turbo-diesel engines are easily higher than 16:1, the amount of propane or natural gas must be supplied at a rate that is too lean to self-ignite with automotive diesel compression ratios.&nbsp; Diesel fuel may safely be substituted up to 25% by volume (i.e., 1 gallon of propane consumed with 4 gallons of diesel). </p><p>The most practical automotive fuel for diesel dual fuel systems at this time is propane (LPG) because it can be transported in useful quantities and because it is commonly available.&nbsp; There are a variety of diesel dual fuel systems&nbsp;on the market today and all can improve the power of diesel engines.&nbsp; The claim that they save fuel is only half correct because they&nbsp;substitute one fuel for another.&nbsp; On an <em>energy basis</em>, only when propane is 65.5% of the price of diesel fuel is&nbsp;a dual fuel system cheaper to operate than straight diesel.&nbsp; While manufacturers of these systems claim to improve the overall fuel efficiency of the engine with improved combustion, we have not seen collaborating third party tests to confirm these claims.&nbsp; We prefer to promote these products as cost-saving products rather than fuel-saving or power-adding products.</p><p>Another benefit of using a gaseous fuel in conjunction with diesel fuel is the reduction of particulate emissions (soot).&nbsp; The reduction in particulate emissions is dependent upon the amount of diesel fuel being substituted at any given time.&nbsp; Although modern diesel engines have diesel particulate filters (DPF), the use of a gaseous alternative fuel can potentially reduce the fuel consumed during a DPF cleaning cycle. </p></span>            </td>
