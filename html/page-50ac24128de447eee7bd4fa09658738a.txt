<p>An <b>off-road vehicle</b> is considered to be any type of <a href="/wiki/Vehicle" title="Vehicle">vehicle</a> which is capable of <a href="/wiki/Off-roading" title="Off-roading">driving</a> on and off <a href="/wiki/Pavement_(material)" title="Pavement (material)" class="mw-redirect">paved</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gravel" title="Gravel">gravel</a> surface.<sup id="cite_ref-New_Brunswick_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-New_Brunswick-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> It is generally characterized by having large <a href="/wiki/Tires" title="Tires" class="mw-redirect">tires</a> with deep, open treads, a flexible <a href="/wiki/Suspension_(vehicle)" title="Suspension (vehicle)">suspension</a>, or even <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar_tracks" title="Caterpillar tracks" class="mw-redirect">caterpillar tracks</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Other vehicles that do not travel public streets or highways are generally termed off-highway vehicles, including <a href="/wiki/Tractor" title="Tractor">tractors</a>, <a href="/wiki/Forklift" title="Forklift" class="mw-redirect">forklifts</a>, <a href="/wiki/Crane_(machine)" title="Crane (machine)">cranes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Backhoe" title="Backhoe">backhoes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bulldozer" title="Bulldozer">bulldozers</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Golf_cart" title="Golf cart">golf carts</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Off-road vehicles have an enthusiastic following because of their many uses and versatility. Several types of <a href="/wiki/Motorsport" title="Motorsport">motorsports</a> involve racing off-road vehicles. The three largest "4-wheel vehicle" off-road types of competitions are <a href="/wiki/Rallying" title="Rallying">Rally</a>, <a href="/wiki/Desert_Racing" title="Desert Racing" class="mw-redirect">Desert Racing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rockcrawling" title="Rockcrawling" class="mw-redirect">Rockcrawling</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The three largest types of <a href="/wiki/All_Terrain_Vehicle" title="All Terrain Vehicle" class="mw-redirect">All Terrain Vehicle</a> (<a href="/wiki/All-terrain_vehicle" title="All-terrain vehicle">ATV</a>) / <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle" title="Motorcycle">Motorcycle</a> competitions are <a href="/wiki/Motocross" title="Motocross">Motocross</a>, <a href="/wiki/Enduro" title="Enduro">Enduro</a>, and also Desert Racing like <a href="/wiki/Dakar_Rallye" title="Dakar Rallye" class="mw-redirect">Dakar Rallye</a> and <a href="/wiki/Baja_1000" title="Baja 1000">Baja 1000</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The most common use of these vehicles is for <a href="/wiki/Sight_seeing" title="Sight seeing" class="mw-redirect">sight seeing</a> in areas distant from <a href="/wiki/Pavement_(material)" title="Pavement (material)" class="mw-redirect">pavement</a>. The use of higher clearance and higher traction vehicles enables access on <a href="/wiki/Trails" title="Trails" class="mw-redirect">trails</a> and <a href="/wiki/Forest_road" title="Forest road" class="mw-redirect">forest roads</a> that have rough and low traction surfaces.</p>

<p>One of the first modified off-road vehicles was the <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A9gresse_track" title="Kégresse track">Kégresse track</a>, a conversion undertaken first by <a href="/wiki/Adolphe_K%C3%A9gresse" title="Adolphe Kégresse">Adolphe Kégresse</a>, who designed the original while working for <a href="/wiki/Czar" title="Czar" class="mw-redirect">Czar</a> <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Nicholas II of Russia</a> between 1906 and 1916.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The system uses an unusual <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar_track" title="Caterpillar track" class="mw-redirect">caterpillar track</a> which has a flexible belt rather than interlocking metal segments. It can be fitted to a conventional car or truck to turn it into a <a href="/wiki/Half-track" title="Half-track">half-track</a> suitable for use over rough or soft ground.</p>
<p>After the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1917" title="Russian Revolution of 1917" class="mw-redirect">Russian Revolution of 1917</a>, Kégresse returned to his native <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> where the system was used on <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn" title="Citroën">Citroën</a> cars between 1921 and 1937 for off-road and <a href="/wiki/Military_vehicle" title="Military vehicle">military vehicles</a>. The Citroën company sponsored several overland expeditions with their vehicles crossing <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">North Africa</a> and <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">Central Asia</a>.</p>
<p>A huge wheeled vehicle designed from 1937 to 1939 under the direction of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Poulter" title="Thomas Poulter">Thomas Poulter</a> called <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Snow_Cruiser" title="Antarctic Snow Cruiser">Antarctic Snow Cruiser</a> was intended to facilitate transport in the <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">Antarctica</a>. While having several innovative features, it generally failed to operate as hoped under the difficult conditions, and was eventually abandoned in Antarctica.</p>

<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, a huge surplus of light off-road vehicles like the <a href="/wiki/Jeep" title="Jeep">Jeep</a> and heavier lorries were available on the market.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The Jeeps in particular were popular with buyers who used them as <a href="/wiki/Utility_vehicle" title="Utility vehicle">utility vehicles</a>. This was also the start of off-roading as a hobby. The wartime Jeeps soon wore out, though, and the Jeep company started to produce civilian derivatives, closely followed by similar vehicles from <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">British</a> <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover" title="Land Rover">Land Rover</a> and <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japanese</a> <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a>, <a href="/wiki/Datsun" title="Datsun">Datsun</a>/<a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan" class="mw-redirect">Nissan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suzuki" title="Suzuki">Suzuki</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Motors" title="Mitsubishi Motors">Mitsubishi</a>. These were all alike: small, compact, four-wheel-drive vehicles with at most a small hardtop to protect the occupants from the elements.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>From the 1960s and onward, more comfortable vehicles were produced.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> For several years they were popular with rural buyers due to their off-road and load-lugging capabilities.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Jeep_Wagoneer" title="Jeep Wagoneer">Jeep Wagoneer</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Bronco" title="Ford Bronco">Ford Bronco</a>, the British <a href="/wiki/Range_Rover" title="Range Rover">Range Rover</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a>-bodied Japanese <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Land_Cruiser" title="Toyota Land Cruiser">Toyota Land Cruiser</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Patrol" title="Nissan Patrol">Nissan Patrol</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suzuki" title="Suzuki">Suzuki</a> Lj's series were all essentially just station wagon bodies on <a href="/wiki/Light_truck" title="Light truck">light truck</a> frames with <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel-drive" title="Four-wheel-drive" class="mw-redirect">four-wheel-drive</a> <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">drivetrains</a>. Later, during the 1990s, manufacturers started to add even more luxuries to bring those off-road vehicles on par with regular cars. This eventually evolved into what we call the <a href="/wiki/SUV" title="SUV" class="mw-redirect">SUV</a> today. It also evolved into the newer <a href="/wiki/Crossover_(automobile)" title="Crossover (automobile)">Crossover vehicle</a>, where utility and off-road capability was sacrificed for better on-road handling and luxury.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Technical">Technical</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technical">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Technical" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>To be able to drive off the pavement, off-road vehicles need several characteristics: They need to have a low <a href="/wiki/Ground_pressure" title="Ground pressure">ground pressure</a>, so as not to sink into soft ground, they need <a href="/wiki/Ground_clearance" title="Ground clearance" class="mw-redirect">ground clearance</a> to not get hung up on obstacles, and they need to keep their wheels or tracks on the ground so as not to lose traction. Wheeled vehicles accomplish this by having a suitable balance of large or additional tires combined with tall and flexible suspension. <a href="/wiki/Tracked_vehicles" title="Tracked vehicles" class="mw-redirect">Tracked vehicles</a> accomplish this by having wide tracks and a flexible suspension on the road wheels.</p>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:%D0%93%D0%A2-%D0%A1%D0%9C_at_Vankorskoe_oilfield.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/%D0%93%D0%A2-%D0%A1%D0%9C_at_Vankorskoe_oilfield.jpg/220px-%D0%93%D0%A2-%D0%A1%D0%9C_at_Vankorskoe_oilfield.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/%D0%93%D0%A2-%D0%A1%D0%9C_at_Vankorskoe_oilfield.jpg/330px-%D0%93%D0%A2-%D0%A1%D0%9C_at_Vankorskoe_oilfield.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/%D0%93%D0%A2-%D0%A1%D0%9C_at_Vankorskoe_oilfield.jpg/440px-%D0%93%D0%A2-%D0%A1%D0%9C_at_Vankorskoe_oilfield.jpg 2x" /></a>

<p>The choice of wheels versus tracks are one of cost and suitability. A tracked drive-train is more expensive to produce and maintain. Wheeled drivetrains are cheaper and give a higher top speed. The tracked drive-train has greater off-road capability.</p>
<p>Most off-road vehicles are fitted with especially <a href="/wiki/Gear" title="Gear">low gearing</a>. This allows the operator to make the most of the <a href="/wiki/Engine" title="Engine">engine</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">available power</a> while moving slowly through challenging terrain. An <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion engine</a> coupled to a normal <a href="/wiki/Gearbox" title="Gearbox" class="mw-redirect">gearbox</a> often has an output speed too high. The vehicle often has one of two things, either a very low ("granny") first gear (like the all wheel drive <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Transporter" title="Volkswagen Transporter">Volkswagen Transporter</a> versions) or an additional gearbox in line with the first, called a <a href="/wiki/Reduction_drive" title="Reduction drive">reduction drive</a>. Some vehicles, like the Bv206 in the picture on the right, also have <a href="/wiki/Torque_converter" title="Torque converter">torque converters</a> to further reduce the gearing.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Many wheeled off-road vehicles have <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel_drive" title="Four-wheel drive">four-wheel drive</a> to keep traction on slippery surfaces. Vehicles designed for use both on and off road may be designed to be switched between two-wheel drive and four-wheel drive so that the vehicle uses fewer driven wheels when driven on the road.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Copper_in_heat_exchangers" title="Copper in heat exchangers">Copper-alloy heat exchangers</a> such as <a href="/wiki/CuproBraze" title="CuproBraze">CuproBraze</a> are particularly suited for off-road vehicles that must operate for long periods of time under harsh conditions without premature failures. The technology is being used by Russian <a href="/wiki/OEM" title="OEM" class="mw-redirect">OEMs</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Kamaz" title="Kamaz">Kamaz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ural_Automotive_Plant" title="Ural Automotive Plant">Ural Automotive Plant</a>, UAZ and GAZ (<a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">Russia</a>), MAZ (<a href="/wiki/Belarus" title="Belarus">Belarus</a>), The Finnish Radiator Manufacturing Company (FinnRadiator),<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Nakamura Jico Co., Ltd. (<a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>), Young Touchstone <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">North America</a>, <a href="/wiki/Siemens_AG" title="Siemens AG" class="mw-redirect">Siemens AG</a> Transportation Systems, and <a href="/wiki/Bombardier_Transportation" title="Bombardier Transportation">Bombardier Transportation</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_off-road_vehicles">Criticism of off-road vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism of off-road vehicles">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Criticism of off-road vehicles" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Safety" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_sport_utility_vehicles" title="Criticism of sport utility vehicles">Criticism of sport utility vehicles</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">SUV's</a> are built with higher <a href="/wiki/Ride_height" title="Ride height">ground clearance</a> for <a href="/wiki/Off-roading" title="Off-roading">off-road</a> use and thus have a higher <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">center of gravity</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Slater_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Slater-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> therefore increasing the risk of rollover. When an SUV turns, the vehicle's mass resists the turn and carries the weight forward, thus allowing the traction from the tires to create a lateral <a href="/wiki/Centripetal_force" title="Centripetal force">centripetal force</a> as the vehicle continues through the turn. The conflict between the top <a href="/wiki/Weight" title="Weight">weight</a> of the SUV's desire to go straight while the <a href="/wiki/Friction" title="Friction">friction</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Tire" title="Tire">tires</a> on the <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">road</a> cause the bottom of the vehicle to move away and out from under the vehicle during a turn.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">Pickups</a> and SUVs are more likely to be in rollover <a href="/wiki/Car_accident" title="Car accident" class="mw-redirect">accidents</a> than <a href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">passenger cars</a>. According to a study conducted in the United States, pickups have twice the fatality rate of cars and SUVs have nearly triple the fatality rate in rollover accidents.<sup id="cite_ref-RUNGE_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RUNGE-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Of vehicles in the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>, <a href="/wiki/Light_truck" title="Light truck">light trucks</a> (including <a href="/wiki/SUV" title="SUV" class="mw-redirect">SUVs</a>) represent 36 percent of all registered vehicles. They are involved in about half of the fatal two-vehicle crashes with passenger cars, and 80 percent of these fatalities are to occupants of the passenger cars.<sup id="cite_ref-RUNGE_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-RUNGE-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Environment">Environment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environment">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Environment" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the United States, the number of ORV users since 1972 has climbed sevenfold—from five million to 36 million in 2000.<sup id="cite_ref-ENS1_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENS1-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Government policies that protect wilderness but also allow recreational ORV use have been the subject of some debate within the United States and other countries.<sup id="cite_ref-Wagtendonk_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wagtendonk-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>All trail and off-trail activities impact <a href="/wiki/Vegetation" title="Vegetation">natural vegetation</a> and wildlife, which can lead to <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">erosion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Invasive_species" title="Invasive species">invasive species</a>, <a href="/wiki/Habitat_loss" title="Habitat loss" class="mw-redirect">habitat loss</a>, and ultimately <a href="/wiki/Species_loss" title="Species loss" class="mw-redirect">species loss</a><sup id="cite_ref-CPC_8-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-CPC-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-latimes_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-latimes-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-lvr_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lvr-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> decreasing an <a href="/wiki/Ecosystem" title="Ecosystem">ecosystem</a>'s ability to maintain <a href="/wiki/Homeostasis" title="Homeostasis">homeostasis</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-DoD_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-DoD-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> ORV's cause greater stress to the environment than <a href="/wiki/Hiking" title="Hiking">foot traffic</a> alone, and ORV operators who attempt to test their vehicles against natural obstacles can do significantly more damage then those who follow legal trails.<sup id="cite_ref-Izembek_12-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Izembek-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-WADNR_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-WADNR-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Illegal use of off-road vehicles has been identified as a serious <a href="/wiki/Land_management" title="Land management">land management</a> problem ranked with dumping <a href="/wiki/Waste" title="Waste">garbage</a> and other forms of <a href="/wiki/Vandalism" title="Vandalism">vandalism</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Pine_Barrens_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Pine_Barrens-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Many user organizations, such as <a href="/wiki/Tread_Lightly!" title="Tread Lightly!">Tread Lightly!</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Club" title="Sierra Club">Sierra Club</a>, publish and encourage appropriate <a href="/wiki/Trail_ethics" title="Trail ethics">trail ethics</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Ethics_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Ethics-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>ORVs have also been criticized for producing more pollution in areas that might normally have none. In addition to <a href="/wiki/Noise_pollution" title="Noise pollution">noise pollution</a> that can cause hearing impairment and stress in wildlife,<sup id="cite_ref-wild_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wild-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> according to the <a href="/wiki/U.S._Forest_Service" title="U.S. Forest Service" class="mw-redirect">U.S. Forest Service</a>, old-style <a href="/wiki/Two-stroke_cycle" title="Two-stroke cycle" class="mw-redirect">two-stroke engines</a> (no longer a component of new off-road vehicles, although some are still in use) "emit about 20 to 33 percent of the consumed fuel through the exhaust" and "discharge from two-stroke snowmobile engines can lead to indirect pollutant deposition into the top layer of snow and subsequently into the associated surface and ground water."<sup id="cite_ref-forestservice_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-forestservice-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wildernessorg_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-wildernessorg-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2002, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> adopted emissions standards for all-terrain vehicles that "when fully implemented in 2012...are expected to prevent the release of more than two million tons of <a href="/wiki/Air_pollution" title="Air pollution">air pollution</a> each year--the equivalent of removing the pollution from more than 32 million cars every year."<sup id="cite_ref-ENS2_19-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ENS2-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-EPA_20-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial.2C_military_and_less_common_off-road_vehicles">Commercial, military and less common off-road vehicles</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial, military and less common off-road vehicles">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Off-road_vehicle&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Commercial, military and less common off-road vehicles" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">European</a> militaries and utilities have used <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover_Defender" title="Land Rover Defender">Land Rover Defenders</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haflinger_(vehicle)" title="Haflinger (vehicle)">Haflingers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pinzgauer_High_Mobility_All-Terrain_Vehicle" title="Pinzgauer High Mobility All-Terrain Vehicle" class="mw-redirect">Pinzgauers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Volvo_L3314" title="Volvo L3314">Volvo L3314</a>, and Mercedes-Benz <a href="/wiki/Unimog" title="Unimog">Unimogs</a> for all-terrain transportation. The <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">Portuguese</a> <a href="/wiki/UMM_Alter" title="UMM Alter" class="mw-redirect">UMM Alter</a> is less common.</p>
<p>The military market for off-road vehicles used to be large, but, since <a href="/wiki/Revolutions_of_1989" title="Revolutions of 1989">the fall of the Iron Curtain</a> in the 1990s, it has dried up to some extent. The U.S. <a href="/wiki/Jeep" title="Jeep">Jeep</a>, developed during <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, coined the word many people use for any type of light off-road vehicle. In the U.S., the Jeep's successor from the 1980s on was the AM General <a href="/wiki/HMMWV" title="HMMWV" class="mw-redirect">HMMWV</a>. The Eastern Bloc used the <a href="/wiki/GAZ-69" title="GAZ-69">GAZ-69</a> and <a href="/wiki/UAZ-469" title="UAZ-469">UAZ-469</a> in similar roles.</p>
