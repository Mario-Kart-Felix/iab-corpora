<div class="entry-content">
<p><P><BR><B>
<FORM class="mt-enclosure mt-enclosure-photo" style="DISPLAY: inline" mt:asset-id="8132808"><SPAN class="photo-breakout photo-left medium"><IMG alt=CClass2010.jpg src="http://media.al.com/assembly-lines/photo/cclass2010jpg-562275c466c9a0c6_medium.jpg"><SPAN class=byline>Mercedes-Benz</SPAN><SPAN class=caption>Some production of the Mercedes-Benz C-Class sedan is moving to the automaker's Alabama factory in Vance.</SPAN></SPAN></FORM>Mercedes-Benz parent Daimler AG</B> said today that it will move some production of the C-Class sedan to the automaker's <A href="http://www.mbusi.com/">Alabama assembly plant in Vance.</A><BR><BR>Local production is expected to start in 2014, and the move will coincide with the launch of the next-generation version of the popular C-Class, the German automaker said.</P>
<P>"This step is essential for strategic and operational reasons, and helps the company to remain competitive and to fully utilize future growth opportunities," Daimler Chief Executive Dieter Zetsche said in a prepared statement.</P>
<P>C-Class production in Vance will require additional investment at the facility, which is currently expanding its body shop in a $290 million project. The plant will build C-Class&nbsp;sedans for North American markets, which&nbsp;will represent&nbsp;roughly 20 percent of the model's total volume.</P>
<P>Building the C-Class in Vance also will create&nbsp;about&nbsp;1,000 jobs there, Daimler officials said in a conference call with German media this morning, according to the Dow Jones Newswires. The plant's work force, which now stands at about 3,000, will rise to 4,000 by 2014.</P>
<P>That&nbsp;number actually represents a return to the range of the plant's previous employment level,&nbsp;before&nbsp;the global auto sales slump forced cost cuts.</P>
<P>Moving Mercedes' best-selling vehicle in the U.S. market to a U.S. production site is expected to help offset the rise of the euro against the dollar, which squeezes profits on imported cars from Germany.<BR><BR>It also will be a stabilizing factor for the Vance plant, which now produces slow-selling sport utilities and crossovers. During the past year, the plant has slashed production in response to the global auto sales slump, resulting in four-day work weeks, the elimination of temporary jobs and buyouts among the permanent work force.</P>
<P>The plant will continue to produce its current lineup along with the C-Class.</P>
<P>There has long been talk that Daimler and Mercedes might move the C-Class to Vance, but it has heightened lately. Zetsche, speaking to reporters in New York last month, fueled speculation by saying the company was considering the move.<BR><BR>Thousands of workers at Mercedes' Sindelfingen plant in Germany, one of the C-Class' current production sites, have protested the move, saying it would cost jobs there.</P>
<P>Daimler said it will maintain employment at Sindelfingen and will transfer production of the SL premium roadster&nbsp;to that facility.&nbsp;&nbsp;Production of the C-Class for European markets will be concentrated at the Bremen plant in Germany.</P>
<P>Currently, the C-Class is built at Bremen and Sindelfingen, as&nbsp;well as at plants in South Africa and China.&nbsp;From 2014 on, about 60 percent of C-Class&nbsp;output will come from Bremen, with the&nbsp;balance coming from Alabama, South Africa and&nbsp;China.&nbsp;</P>
<P>So far this year, Mercedes has sold 47,578 C-Class vehicles in the U.S., making it the company's best-selling model domestically.<BR><BR>The Vance plant now produces the M-Class sport utility, GL-Class full-sized sport utility and R-Class crossover. Year-to-date U.S. sales of all three vehicles combined total 38,228, although the plant also supplies them to markets around the globe.</P>
<P><BR><BR>&nbsp;<BR><BR><BR><BR><BR><BR><BR><BR><BR><BR></P></p>
