										<h2>Learn More about Brakes & Accessories</h2>
										<p>
											<p>
	Performance brakes give you better stopping power and are a must when changing to different tire and wheel combinations on your vehicle. Piece of mind and additional safety at a price you can&#39;t beat--what&#39;s stopping you now?</p>
<p>
	<strong>Cut Lap Times</strong></p>
<p>
	Every good driver knows that reducing lap times and setting records has as much to do with braking power as it does acceleration.&nbsp; Your stock brakes are configured to work for your car out of the factory.&nbsp; So if you&#39;ve modded your power, it&#39;s time to balance it out with brakes.</p>
<p>
	<strong>Pads</strong></p>
<p>
	Let&#39;s start with your brake pads.&nbsp; When it comes to pads, you&#39;ve got a lot of options.&nbsp; <strong>Ceramic </strong>pads are quiet and don&#39;t produce much dust, so they&#39;re perfect for every day driving.&nbsp; But they&#39;re not recommended for high-performance driving, so you may want to change them out for track days.&nbsp; On those joyous occasions, you need <strong>metallic </strong>pads.&nbsp; These hold out longer when the heat is on, but generate more noise, which you won&#39;t really hear over the roar of your engine as you downshift on your way to a corner.&nbsp; If you don&#39;t feel like switching back and forth all the time, you should look into <strong>semi-metallic</strong> pads, which work for a wide range of needs.</p>
<p>
	<strong>Rotors</strong></p>
<p>
	Then there&#39;s the other half.&nbsp; Your pads are only as good as your rotors.&nbsp; If your discs are grooved or thin, or just not slowing you like you need them to, it&#39;s time to explore some performance brake rotors.&nbsp; Once again, you&#39;ve got options.&nbsp; <strong>Drilled rotors</strong> are laced with ventilation holes that keep your brakes cool.&nbsp; Unless you&#39;re driving an F1 car (and we wouldn&#39;t put it past you), the cooler your brakes stay, the more stopping power you&#39;ll have, and the longer they&#39;ll last.&nbsp; Dirt, water, and brake dust can get between your pads and rotors, reducing friction.&nbsp; That&#39;s where <strong>slotted rotors</strong> come in handy.&nbsp; Shallow slots are machined into these rotors, directing the dirt, water, and brake dust away from your brake system and out of your way.&nbsp; And if you can&#39;t decide which of these is better, you don&#39;t have to.&nbsp; Several models are <strong>drilled and slotted</strong>, giving you the maximum stopping power you need for late braking and quicker times.</p>
<p>
	<strong>Complete the System</strong></p>
<p>
	Even if you have the best pads and rotors on the market, they won&#39;t make a difference if the rest of your brake system isn&#39;t getting the pressure to the wheels.&nbsp; It starts with a high performance <strong>master cylinder </strong>that&#39;s up to the job.&nbsp; It isn&#39;t called a master cylinder for nothing.&nbsp; It controls the flow of brake fluid to the brakes.&nbsp; The stock brake lines that came with your vehicle are probably made of a rubber stretchy enough to absorb some of the pressure coming through them.&nbsp; <strong>Braided brake lines</strong> are stiffer and cut down on your brake system&#39;s reaction time.&nbsp; The vast majority of calipers on the road only have one piston each, but <strong>performance brake calipers</strong> come loaded with as many as four.&nbsp; Why not get the edge on the competition?</p>
