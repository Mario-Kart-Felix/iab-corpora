<div class="entry" id="entry-6a00d83451b3c669e2019103991825970c">
	
		<h1 class="entry-header">Does Roadside Assistance Live Up to Expectations?</h1>
	
	<div class="entry-content">
		<div class="entry-body">
			<p>
 <img alt="RLX1" border="0" class="asset  asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b3c669e2019103dbd052970c" src="http://blogs.cars.com/.a/6a00d83451b3c669e2019103dbd052970c-800wi" title="RLX1" /> 
 </p>
<p>Automakers, both luxury and non, promote complimentary roadside assistance as a selling point. But does the reality of using the service meet with the expectations car owners may have? </p>
<p>We unfortunately had the chance to find out.</p>
<p>While driving through the city of Chicago, a brand-new 2014 Acura RLX test car I was piloting picked a fight with a large pothole and lost, leaving a nasty gash in the side wall of the front passenger&#39;s side tire. </p>
<p>After beeps and warning notifications informed us of the immediate drop to &quot;0 PSI,&quot; I carefully pulled over and found a safe spot to change the blown-out tire with a spare. </p>
<p>No dice.</p>

		</div>
		
			<a id="more"></a>
				
<p><img alt="RLX2" border="0" class="asset  asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b3c669e201901de5d264970b" src="http://blogs.cars.com/.a/6a00d83451b3c669e201901de5d264970b-800wi" title="RLX2" /></p>
<p>Like many new cars on the market, the RLX doesn&#39;t have a full-size spare or a compact (donut) tire. The solution: an air pump and gel that inflates and temporarily seals the tire. This answer to our problem proved useless due to the sheer size of the damage to the tire. </p>
<p>Why don&#39;t carmakers put full-size or compact &quot;donut&quot; spare tires in cars as often as they used to? Three reasons: fuel-economy, cost and space saving. The absence of a spare tire in the RLX may have left a generous under-floor cargo area, but the side effect proved undesirable in this situation. </p>
<p>I then did what any owner would do. I made a phone call to Acura roadside assistance to get a fix or a tow. </p>
<p>We were told help would arrive within an hour, a tolerable amount of time we figured. </p>
<p>Nearly 90 minutes later, we were still waiting, even after automated calls from Acura to inquire about whether a tow truck had arrived. A few more automated calls later, and a call from the contracted tow truck driver, we were still waiting.</p>
<p> 
 <img alt="RLX3" border="0" class="asset  asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451b3c669e201901de5d32d970b" src="http://blogs.cars.com/.a/6a00d83451b3c669e201901de5d32d970b-800wi" title="RLX3" /></p>
<p>A little more than two hours had passed when the sight of a flatbed tow truck with yellow lights flashing showed up in our rearview mirror.</p>
<p>How does Acura&#39;s roadside assistance program match up against other manufacturers&#39; programs?</p>
<p>Most car companies today either contract their roadside assistance programs to third-party service providers or handle all the calls in-house. Mercedes-Benz, for example, has 99% of its calls answered by dealer-trained technicians. Companies like Agero, which represents 70% of the top auto brands, responds to more than 12 million calls a year. Here&#39;s a chart showing the average response times for some of the most popular car brands as reported by the automakers:</p>
<p><img alt="ResponseTimes" border="0" class="whiteimage" src="http://blogs.cars.com/.a/6a00d83451b3c669e20192abd00ab0970d-800wi" title="ResponseTimes" /></p>
<p>We&#39;re left wondering why our wait was more than two hours for a local tow truck to arrive — headquartered three blocks from where we were — and take the car to a dealership nine minutes away? If the RLX test car had come with a full-size spare or donut, we could have been back on the road within minutes of the popped tire fiasco.</p>
<p>However, we also wonder how many Acura owners would know how to change a spare tire even if there was one.</p>
<p>Let us know your experiences using roadside assistance programs.&#0160;</p>
