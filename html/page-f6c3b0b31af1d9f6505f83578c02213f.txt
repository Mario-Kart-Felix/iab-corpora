    <h2>What's behind our tire Ratings?</h2>
    <div>
        Experts at our National Testing and Research Center tested 173 models in tires to see which ones perform best.    </div>

    
    <div class="ratings-reason-title">We look for:</div>
    <ul>
                    <li>
                Overall score
                <div class="information">Overall score emphasizes safety-related tests, including braking, handling, and resistance to hydroplaning. Scores and ratings are held to a comparative standard within a tire category.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Dry braking
                <div class="information">Dry braking is from 60 to 0 mph.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Wet braking
                <div class="information">Wet braking is from 60 to 0 mph.  Refer to the wet braking iText at the top of the Ratings chart for the specific speed used.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Handling
                <div class="information">Handling includes how well the tires gripped in an avoidance maneuver involving a swerve into the left lane and back into the right lane; dry and wet cornering grip; and subjective steering feel.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Hydroplaning
                <div class="information">Hydroplaning reflects how quickly we could drive through standing water before the tires begin to lose contact with the pavement.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Snow traction
                <div class="information">Snow traction reflects the distance our test car needed to accelerate from 5 to 20 mph on moderately packed snow.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Ice braking
                <div class="information">We tested ice braking on a skating rink from 10 to 0 mph.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Ride comfort
                <div class="information">Ride comfort reflects our on-road judgments.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Noise
                <div class="information">Noise reflects our on-road judgments.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Rolling resistance
                <div class="information">Rolling resistance, measured on a dynamometer, is a factor in fuel economy.</div>
            </li>
                    <li>
                Tread life
                <div class="information">Tread life is an indicator of wear potential from CR's vehicle road test on the Federal Government's treadwear course.</div>
            </li>

            <p>Despite advances in longer-lasting tires, actual tread life will vary by car type, tire type (such as all season or high performance), driving aggressiveness, and even road and weather conditions. Car owners still need to replace their tires a few times or more throughout the life of a typical vehicle. As the adage goes, nothing lasts forever.</p><p>Proper maintenance and responsible driving can maximize the mileage in a set of tires. Monthly tread inspections can inform when the tires warrant replacement, well in advance of the federally mandated tread-wear indicators. In most states, tires are legally worn out when their tread depth reaches 1/16 inch (or 2/32 inch as found on standardized tread-depth gauges). The easiest way to measure this, if you don't have a gauge, is to hold a penny upside down in the tread. If the top of Lincoln's head is visible, you need new tires. But using a penny standard doesn't work for all weather conditions. We have found in our tests that a tire with just 1/8-inch tread was notably worse in hydroplaning resistance and snow traction. By the time only 1/16 inch remains, wet-pavement cornering and braking drop off too. Based on our experience, when your tires have less than 1/8 inch of tread left, it's a good time to start shopping for replacement tires.</p><p>As a better indicator of tread wear, place a quarter upside down in a tire groove. The distance from the coin's rim to George Washington's hairline is about 1/8 inch. If you see all of his head in any one groove where a tread-wear indicator appears, consider shopping for new tires.</p><p>Once a need for new tires is determined, it is necessary to identify the best tires for your vehicle and driving demands.</p>
