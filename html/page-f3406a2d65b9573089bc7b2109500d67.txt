<DIV></DIV><!-- EdSysObj ID="SandboxLede" FRAGMENTID="12043619" ebracke --><span class="inside-head">Nissan adds 'stylish flair' to minivan</span><div class="by-line">By Theresa Howard, USA TODAY</div>
<div class="intro-copy">NEW YORK &#151; Automakers and consumers have long valued substance over style in marketing of minivans, but that's changing. </div>
<table class="sidebar" border="0" cellspacing="0" cellpadding="0">
<tr>
<td colspan="4"><img src="http://images.usatoday.com/_common/_images/clear.gif" width="1" height="20" border="0"></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td rowspan="2"><img src="http://images.usatoday.com/money/_photos/2003/12-01-adtrack.jpg" width="180" height="136"></td>
<td rowspan="2"><img src="http://images.usatoday.com/_common/_images/clear.gif" width="10" height="1" border="0"></td>
<td class="sidebar" width="75" valign="top"><span class="sidebar"><span class="sidebar">Nissan's new Quest takes aim at an  anti-minivan mind-set among some women, says George Peterson of Auto-Pacific.</span></span></td>
<td rowspan="2"><img src="http://images.usatoday.com/_common/_images/clear.gif" width="10" height="1" border="0"></td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td align="left" valign="bottom"><span class="sidebar"></span></td>
</tr>
</table>
<p class="inside-copy">Armed with sexy product makeovers and ads that try to load some hip into the utilitarian vehicles, automakers are vying for a bigger piece of the shrinking minivan pie. Sales are forecast to be 1.1 million in 2003, down 7% from 2002, according to tracker Autodata. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">Among minivan makeovers: </p>
<p class="inside-copy">&#8226;<b>Toyota Sienna</b>. A sleek redesign, with such kid-friendly features as 14 cup holders, a third-row foldaway seat and rear-seat headphones helped Toyota boost car sales 11.7% in October. Sales for Sienna are up 14.6% year-to-date.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">&#8226;<b>Honda Odyssey. </b> Odyssey sales are up 4.2% year-to-date. The latest version of the No. 2 van is credited with setting new minivan standards, including rear DVD systems.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">&#8226;<b>Ford Freestar</b>. Say goodbye to Windstar and hello to Freestar. The new version of Ford's minivan just rolled into dealerships.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">&#8226;<b>Chrysler Town &amp; Country. </b> The company that is the minivan leader with 35% of the market will refresh its top-end Town &amp; Country this spring. But don't look for Celine Dion in the new ads. While Chrysler signed an estimated $14 million deal to have her spruce up the Chrysler image in ads and to sponsor her Las Vegas show, dealers complained she isn't selling cars or bringing in younger buyers.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">But no minivan maker has tried harder to break the minivan mold than Nissan. Its new Quest, dubbed the glam van during its design, aims directly at the anti-minivan mind-set.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">"There's really a negative image issue with minivans," says George Peterson, an analyst at AutoPacific. "That's exactly the issue Nissan is trying to get around. They are trying to attract the young, hip mom who doesn't like to feel relegated to driving a mommy-mobile." </p>
<p class="inside-copy">Ads by TBWA/Chiat/Day, Los Angeles, make the usual pitch about the van's utility secondary to highlighting style and design innovations. There's seating for seven, the minivan magic number for capacity, a foldaway third-row seat and automatic side doors.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">But the ads try to show that the safe vehicle is also safe to be seen in on a Saturday night out, and that it's useful for grown-up sporting equipment, too &#8212; such as golf clubs and yoga balls.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">The ads dwell on features such as a SkyView glass-paneled roof and a stylish exterior akin to Nissan's sleek new Murano crossover. The campaign theme: "Moms have changed; shouldn't minivans?"</p>
<p class="inside-copy">"Everybody pays attention to the passenger, but nobody paid attention to the features for the driver," says Fred Suckow, Nissan marketing director. "Make no mistake, every minivan has to hit the cues. There are certain things you have to execute, but we tried to throw a stylish flair out there."</p>
<p class="inside-copy">The national TV ads are the first in three years for the van brand, which went without a model last year as Nissan prepared for the July rollout of the new Quest. The message and new design resonated with women, the target market, among consumers polled by Ad Track, USA TODAY's weekly survey.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">Just 10% of consumers overall say they like the ads "a lot," vs. the Ad Track average of 21%. But by gender, just 2% of men like the ads "a lot," vs. 17% of women. Given that auto ads as a rule score below the Ad Track average for all products, 17% seems a solid score.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">Nissan hopes to sell 75,000 to 80,000 of the new Quests in its first model year but are a "little behind" schedule, Suckow acknowledges. However, he says, Nissan will ramp up marketing in the spring, a strong selling season for minivans, as families prepare for vacations. Nissan will continue to target "new" moms to reach the goal.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">"Most minivans cause women to cringe," Suckow says. "We've landed on the consumer who is the modern mom. She likes to be known by her first name rather than just being Mom." </p>
