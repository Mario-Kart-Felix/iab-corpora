    <p>Renault unveiled a 3.0-liter diesel V-6 in Frankfurt that Nissan has confirmed will indeed make its way to the U.S. under the hoods of Nissan <a href="/carmodel/nissan-maxima.html" target="_self">Maximas</a>, with 2010 as the target sale date. Although specifics such as power and torque are not yet available, Nissan tells us the engine will meet stringent Tier 2 Bin 5 emissions standards and will be certified for sale in all 50 states.</p><p>When <i>Car and Driver</i> editor-in-chief Csaba Csere spoke with Nissan boss <a href="/columns/a-pair-of-industry-bigwigs-talks-technology-at-the-detroit-auto-show" target="_self">Carlos Ghosn</a> at the 2007 Detroit auto show about Nissan's plans for fuel-efficient cars, Ghosn stated that he saw diesels and continuously variable transmissions leading the way in efficiency in the immediate future.</p><p>"Diesel makes more sense in bigger cars than in smaller ones, and diesel is easier to adapt to a wide variety of vehicles than a hybrid is," he said.</p><p>Through July of this year, Nissan had sold 3919 <a href="/carmodel/nissan-altima.html" target="_self">Altima</a> hybrids in the U.S., which officials say is in line with expectations (the car is on sale in only eight states), and there are no plans to expand at this time.</p>
