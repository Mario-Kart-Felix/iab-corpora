Is it true that I can increase my gas mileage by adding 4-5 oz. of acetone per 16 gallons of gas? If so, are there any risks of damage to the engine? KEVIN<br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic;"><span style="font-weight: bold;">A:</span> No, it's not true. Yes, you can damage the engine.</span><br /><br /><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">Q:</span> I have a 2000 Dodge Grand Caravan Sport. I was told by an auto parts store that I need to change the cabin air filter but I can not find any information on any web sites where the cabin air filter is or even is there is one on my car. Would you please let me know. My care has a 3.3 engine. Please help. I am getting frustrated. LINDA<br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic;"><span style="font-weight: bold;">A:</span> I bet. Your minivan does not have a cabin air filter.</span><br /><br /><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">Q:</span> I am looking for advice about throttle body spacers that I have read about in several advertisements. I would like to know if they are a wise investment, and what I can really expect from them. I don&rsquo;t want to dish out the money and not get increased mileage in return.<br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic;"><span style="font-weight: bold;">A:</span> I haven't seen any throttle body spacers advertised as improving fuel economy. Which is fine, because they don't.</span><br /><br /><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">Q:</span> With the USA going to more alcohol based gasoline, it will cause problems for 2-cycle engine owners. Years back I mistakenly purchased gasoline that contained gasohol and used in in my chain saw.&nbsp; Although I added the proper oil mixture to the fuel, my engine siezed. The shop mechanic asked where I bought my gas and he said that the purchased gas contained alcohol and caused the engine failure. Is there or will there be an additive or a different type of oil that will allow consumers to continue using their outboard motors, chain saws, brush cutters, etc.? RON<br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic;"><span style="font-weight: bold;">A:</span> Your chainsaw should be ale to burn 10% ethanol mixtures with a minor carburetor adjustment. E85 (85% alcohol), would probably melt the piston from running too lean. 2-strokes are pretty sensitive about mixture ratios, as you've found out.</span><br /><br /><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">Q:</span> What should have been a routine timing belt and tensioner, camshaft seals, crankshaft seal and water pump replacement on a 1990 Plymouth Voyager 3.0 engine has me, an auto mechanics teacher and certified auto mechanic, mystified. After replacing all the parts mentioned,I manually turned the crankshaft two revolutions to make sure everything turned smoothly and that the all timing marks are in position. This is a non-interference engine. I turned the crankshaft clockwise 3/4 of the way around using the crankshaft bolt and a ratchet, when #3 and #6 pistons near TDC, it comes to a dead stop. I tried to turn the crankshaft counterclockwise and it stopped very close to the same position. I have done a number of tests including rotating the camshafts individually, a visual test of all valve springs and valves and a pressure test of all cylinders to check for leaky valves. I only found one intake valve at #6 cylinder that was leaking.&nbsp; So I brought #6 piston up near TDC, pulled the spark plug and probed the cylinder with a magnet, and then a vacuum attachment, trying to locate <span style="font-style: italic;">something</span>, but came up empty handed. Assuming now that the cylinders are clear, is it possible that the oil pump rotors or something in the torque converter or transmission somehow got jammed? Everthing was working fine before the job started. Do you have any suggestions on what to check next before pulling the heads? ANTHONY J. DELIA<br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic;"><span style="font-weight: bold;">A:</span> I'm guessing the camshaft is out of time.</span><br /><br /><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic;">Q:</span> It is my understanding the air filter on my 2004 Ford Focus with 2.3 four cylinder is non serviceable. Is this correct? BEN PRICE<br /><br /><span style="font-style: italic;"><span style="font-weight: bold;">A:</span> There's a pollen filter and a cabin air filter. There's no procedure in the shop manual for changing the cabin air filter, bu the pollen filter is under the hood near the base of the windshield. There is a part number for the cabin air filter, however, so it must be possible to change it, even though it's not supposed to be necessary.</span></p>
