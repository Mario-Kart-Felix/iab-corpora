<h3 class="entry-title"><a href="http://wheels.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/08/14/could-outmoded-phone-booths-become-e-v-charging-stations/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Could Outmoded Phone Booths Become E.V. 	<address class="byline author vcard">By <a href="/author/jim-motavalli/" class="url fn" title="See all posts by JIM MOTAVALLI">JIM MOTAVALLI</a></address></header>
<!-- The Content -->
<div class="entry-content"><div class="w480"><a href="http://wheels.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/08/14/could-outmoded-phone-booths-become-e-v-charging-stations/"><img src="http://graphics8.nytimes.com/images/2013/08/13/automobiles/wheels/phone-booth-1/phone-booth-1-blog480.jpg" id="100000002387462" width="480" height="355" alt="Although the telephones in this phone booth on the corner of 43rd Street and Seventh Avenue are no longer in use, some E.V. advocates want to repurpose them as charging stations."/></a><span class="credit">Benjamin Preston</span> <span class="caption">Although the telephones in this phone booth on the corner of 43rd Street and Seventh Avenue are no longer in use, some E.V. advocates want to repurpose them as charging stations.</span></div>
<p>Consider New York’s public pay telephones. They may be outdated in the digital age, but they have telephone wiring and are often supplied with electricity. More important, they already occupy precious city real estate — and already have the permits necessary to stay there. Is it possible to imagine some of them seamlessly replaced with electric vehicle charging stations, with vital dedicated parking spaces?</p>
<p>New York City still has about 8,000 active public telephone kiosks, and their slow fade has led to discussions about repurposing the space they occupy for another public use. The franchise contracts that the city awarded for the operation of public phones will expire in October 2014, and looking for inspiration, the city sponsored a design contest that in the words of one winning team “was an opportunity to reimagine a piece of outdated city infrastructure in the age of mobile.” <a href="http://wheels.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/08/14/could-outmoded-phone-booths-become-e-v-charging-stations/#more-188274" class="more-link">Read more&#8230;</a></p>

<h3 class="entry-title"><a href="http://wheels.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/04/04/a-new-electric-car-with-an-old-name/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to A New Electric Car With an Old Name">A New Electric Car With an Old Name</a></h3>
	<address class="byline author vcard">By <a href="/author/paul-stenquist/" class="url fn" title="See all posts by PAUL STENQUIST">PAUL STENQUIST</a></address></header>
<!-- The Content -->
<div class="entry-content"><div class="w480"><a href="http://wheels.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/04/04/a-new-electric-car-with-an-old-name/"><img src="http://graphics8.nytimes.com/images/2013/04/04/automobiles/wheels-detroitelec/wheels-detroitelec-blog480.jpg" id="100000002154454" width="480" height="320" alt="Detroit Electric's SP:01, a limited-edition electric sports car."/></a><span class="credit">Detroit Electric, via Associated Press</span> <span class="caption">Detroit Electric&#8217;s SP:01, a limited-edition electric sports car.</span></div>
<p>The fledgling Detroit <a class="tickerized" title="More articles about electric vehicles." href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/subjects/e/electric_vehicles/index.html?inline=nyt-classifier">Electric car</a> company introduced its first product, the fully electric SP:01 two-seat roadster, on Wednesday. Like the <a class="tickerized" href="http://autos.nytimes.com/2010/Tesla/Roadster/340/4137/321944/researchOverview.aspx?inline=nyt-classifier">Tesla Roadster</a>, the SP:01 will be built on a <a class="tickerized" href="http://autos.nytimes.com/2010/Lotus/Elise/260/2977/323752/researchOverview.aspx?inline=nyt-classifier">Lotus Elise</a> chassis. Detroit Electric will handle only final assembly of an initial production run of 999 vehicles.</p>
<p>The Detroit Electric name is familiar to automotive historians. The early 20th century electric-vehicle manufacturer produced about 13,000 cars in its nearly 30-year history. This new venture, which has its headquarters in Detroit’s Fisher Building, acquired the brand name from the United States Patent and Trademark Office.</p>
<p>Detroit Electric is assembling SP:01 prototypes in Europe. The car&#8217;s top speed is 155 miles per hour and its time from zero to 62 m.p.h. is 3.7 seconds, the company said.</p>
<p>The SP:01 is powered by a 201 horsepower (150 kW) electric motor mounted behind the passenger cabin. The Lotus Elise donor car’s manual gearbox delivers the power to the rear wheels. In the Lotus, the manual gearbox has six forward speeds, but in the torque-rich electric car only four are used, with fifth and sixth gears blocked off. However, to achieve that 155 m.p.h. top speed, fifth gear must be reinstated. That fifth ratio can be added as an option at the time of purchase. A two-speed automatic will also be available. Steering and suspension components are similar to those of the Lotus, but have been modified to better suit the SP:01’s weight distribution.</p>
<p>Two lithium-polymer batteries provide electricity. According to a Detroit Electric media release, the SP:01 achieved a range of 180 miles on the European driving cycle. The company said a 7.7 kWh home charging unit could fully charge the car in about four hours.</p>
<p>The car’s body is carbon fiber, which sits on Lotus’s aluminum tub-style chassis. The company said the car weighed 2,400 pounds.</p>
<p>Detroit Electric said the SP:01 would go on sale in several markets in the United States by the end of August. Pricing will start at $135,000, but will vary depending on vehicle specifications and local taxes.</p>

<h3 class="entry-title"><a href="http://wheels.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/03/12/tesla-model-x-production-wont-start-until-late-2014/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Tesla Model X Production Won&#8217;t Start Until Late 2014">Tesla Model X Production Won&#8217;t Start Until Late 2014</a></h3>
	<address class="byline author vcard">By <a href="/author/bradley-berman/" class="url fn" title="See all posts by BRADLEY BERMAN">BRADLEY BERMAN</a></address></header>
<!-- The Content -->
<div class="entry-content"><div class="w480"><a href="http://wheels.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/03/12/tesla-model-x-production-wont-start-until-late-2014/"><img src="http://graphics8.nytimes.com/images/2013/03/12/automobiles/wheels-tesla/wheels-tesla-blog480.jpg" id="100000002113412" width="480" height="313" alt="The Tesla Model X at the 2013 Detroit auto show."/></a><span class="credit">Paul Sancya/Associated Press</span> <span class="caption">The Tesla Model X at the 2013 Detroit auto show.</span></div>
<p>Tesla Motors has confirmed that production of its all-wheel-drive Model X electric crossover will begin in late 2014, a year later than the company had originally announced. The revised timing was described in the company’s Form 10-K filed with the <a class="tickerized" title="More articles about the U.S. Securities And Exchange Commission." href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/organizations/s/securities_and_exchange_commission/index.html?inline=nyt-org">Securities and Exchange Commission</a> last Thursday.</p>
<p>When Tesla first revealed the Model X in February 2012, the company said production would begin at the end of 2013, with deliveries following in 2014. Nearly a year later, at the Detroit auto show last January, <a class="tickerized" title="More articles about Elon Musk." href="http://topics.nytimes.com/top/reference/timestopics/people/m/elon_musk/index.html?inline=nyt-per">Elon Musk</a>, Tesla’s chief executive, made the first public statement about the shift in the schedule. Mr. Musk said production of the Model X would begin in the second half of 2014, <a href="http://www.reuters.com/article/2013/01/14/autoshow-tesla-profit-idUSL2N0AJ0GX20130114">according to Reuters</a>.</p>
<p>While Tesla has been saying since the Detroit auto show in January that Model X production would begin in 2014, “our recent 10-K was the first written verification of that,” Shanna Hendricks, a Tesla spokeswoman, said in an e-mail.</p>
<p>“I almost have trouble calling this a delay in Model X, because that makes it sound like we’ve run into problems with Model X, and it’s taking longer than we thought, and it’s out of our control,” Ms. Hendricks wrote. “When, really, we’re consciously pushing back timing to allow ourselves to focus on its production and product enhancements in Model S.” <a href="http://wheels.blogs.nytimes.com/2013/03/12/tesla-model-x-production-wont-start-until-late-2014/#more-181685" class="more-link">Read more&#8230;</a></p>
