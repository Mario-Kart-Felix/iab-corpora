<p>At this year&rsquo;s New England International Auto Show, hybrids will turn up the heat on a class of cars known more for efficiency than excitement.</p>
<p>While gas-electric models from Toyota and Honda continue to dominate, new American and German hybrids are promising more power, and in some cases, even greater fuel savings.</p>
<p>At least 25 alternative-fuel cars, ranging from diesels to full electrics, will be on display at the Boston Convention &amp; Exhibition Center, starting Thursday afternoon as the show gets underway.
</p>
<p>One car, Ford&rsquo;s Fusion Energi, a plug-in hybrid, is estimated to deliver 21 miles of traveling on electricity before its gas engine has to kick in &mdash; nearly double the range of the Prius plug-in that debuted last year.</p>

<div data-adname="CENTRAL" class="ad aside"></div>
<p id="skip-target">It will compete with the Chevrolet Volt, last year&rsquo;s most popular plug-in, with more than 23,000 sold.</p>
<p>At least 16 hybrids are new for the 2013 model year, and market share, including all-electric vehicles, is expected to increase to 5 percent from 3.3 percent in 2012, according to WardsAuto, an industry tracking firm. But given the volatility of gasoline prices &mdash; last year they hit a record average of $3.62 per gallon nationwide, according to the Energy Information Administration &mdash; that number could rise significantly in coming years.</p>
<p>Ford has four more hybrids on display in Boston this year, including a brand-new Lincoln MKZ Hybrid the company hopes will reignite its luxury division. The Ford Fusion and C-MAX &shy;hybrids, rated at 47 miles per gallon in both city and highway tests, trump Toyota Camry Hybrid and Prius v mileage by a significant margin. But the EPA is investigating those claims following a lawsuit and complaints from owners that the EPA estimates were too high.</p>
<div class="aside nr-graphic">
<div class="figure-static">
<img alt="
" data-fullsrc="//c.o0bg.com/rf/pdf_371w/Boston/2011-2020/2013/01/17/BostonGlobe.com/Business/Graphics/17AutoShow-114.jpg" src="//c.o0bg.com/rf/pdf_r/Boston/2011-2020/2013/01/17/BostonGlobe.com/Business/Graphics/17AutoShow-114.r.jpg"></div>
</div>
<p>German automakers, once reluctant to sell hybrids alongside their diesel models, are enthusiastically adding them to the lineups.</p>
<p>Volkswagen&rsquo;s Jetta Hybrid, a relative value starting at under $26,000, promises sharp handling, a turbocharged engine, and up to 45 combined miles per gallon.</p>
<p>The BMW ActiveHybrid 3 and Mercedes E400 Hybrid serve up speed and luxury and save some fuel along the way.</p>
<p>Improving hybrid technology isn&rsquo;t limited to batteries.</p>
<p>Toyota&rsquo;s new luxury models, the Avalon Hybrid and Lexus ES 300h, have touch-sensitive climate controls and a wireless charging dock for smartphones. The most striking hybrid, the plug-in Cadillac ELR, which debuted Tuesday at the North American International Auto Show in Detroit, has a driver&rsquo;s seat that vibrates if the car senses a collision.</p>
<p>The swell of new hybrids marks a resurgent auto industry, one on track to sell 15.4 million cars in the United States this year, industry watcher R.L. Polk said.</p>
<p>In 2012, sales reached a five-year high of 14.4 million, with seven automakers posting record numbers and all but two &mdash; Mitsubishi and Suzuki, which is exiting the US market &mdash; posting year-over-year increases. By contrast, nearly 17 million cars were sold in 2005.</p>
<p>In Massachusetts, sales increased about 5 percent last year to roughly 260,000 cars, said Robert O&rsquo;Koniewski, executive vice president of the Massachusetts State Automobile Dealers Association. Chrysler was the only domestic automaker to sell more cars in Massachusetts; Toyota and Honda rose by 19 and 10 percent, respectively. Toyota posted record sales in New England, excluding Connecticut, despite issuing more recalls and agreeing to a $1.1 billion settlement related to its widely publicized 2009 and 2010 recalls.</p>
<p>&ldquo;The bond between our dealers and our customers is pretty tight,&rdquo; said Cooper Ericksen, Toyota&rsquo;s general manager in the Boston region. &ldquo;A lot of the things that you hear about I don&rsquo;t think play out at the dealership-to-customer level.&rdquo;</p>
<p>Even as Americans are holding on to cars longer than ever &mdash; the average age is 11 years old &mdash; major technological strides may make drivers reconsider their rides, said John Paul, public affairs manager for AAA Southern New England.</p>
<p>&ldquo;People are starting to look at those [new] cars and look at the combination of improved safety and fuel economy and low interest loans, and think maybe it is time to shop for a new car,&rdquo; Paul said.</p>
<p>The auto show isn&rsquo;t all about practical decisions. As usual, there will be plenty of room in the exhibition hall for fantasy drives. For instance, the exotic Fisker Karma, a $100,000 plug-in hybrid with 22-inch wheels, is being displayed for the first time, as are several gas-only &shy;supercars, such as the McLaren MP4-12C. Gleaming Italian and British luxury cars are in Boston this week, too.</p>
<p>But don&rsquo;t plan on getting too close: The high-end vehicles will be kept behind velvet ropes.</p>
