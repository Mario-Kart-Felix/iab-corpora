    <h3 class="post-title">
	 
	 Hybrids and the end of the American car
	 
    </h3>
    

	         <div class="post-body">
	<div>
      <div style="clear:both;"></div><a onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}" href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/uploaded_images/fisker_plug_in_hybrid_vehicle_at_naias_2008-760581.jpg"><img style="margin: 0pt 10px 10px 0pt; float: left; cursor: pointer; width: 250px; height: 188px;" src="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/uploaded_images/fisker_plug_in_hybrid_vehicle_at_naias_2008-760569.jpg" alt="Battery-powered autos might be the key to the future auto industry, however, cost-effective efficiency - whether it includes the battery or not - is certain to be the path towards future auto profits." border="0" /></a><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic; color: rgb(102, 0, 0);">Karma: Is this really the path towards efficiency for the masses?</span><br /><br />For decades the US has driven the world's auto industry. If an automaker could make it here, they could make it anywhere. But, is America still that important?<br /><br />Recently, China topped the US in auto sales, yet the Chinese auto market has barely been scratched. Likewise, add in the potential of all 4 BRIC countries, let alone the rest of the emerging markets, and the importance of the US market could be significantly marginalized in just a decade or two.<br /><br />Even more interesting, by 2030, according to a plethora of research, <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_cars.htm">hybrid cars</a> and EVs - the proverbially future of the US market - will achieve, in a best case scenario, a combined 30 percent share in the US. Yet, in the developing world, share will probably be significantly lower as most research suggests emerging markets will embrace far cheaper forms of transportation than US-like products.<br /><br />Thus, just how important is the battery to auto industry success?<br /><br />When Henry Ford, for example, revolutionized the US auto industry, his success was driven by efficiency. Efficient scalability, or cost-effective solutions for the greatest numbers, powered the US auto industry for decades. Then, of course, the market saturated and maximized quarterly profits, rather than increased efficiency, took over the US auto market.<br /><br />But the automotive world is quickly changing. New and huge, yet totally un-American markets are quickly emerging. While American autos might be cost-effective for some early adopters in some emerging markets, will they be cost-effective for the masses? Will adding batteries to American autos make them more cost-effective, or less cost-effective, for the masses?<br /><br />If Henry Ford's efficient scalability is the key to the future of auto industry success, doesn't the US market have to become more worldly? Moreover, can the world market, ultimately, be based on anything other than the cheapest and safest efficiency possible, whether that includes batteries or not?<div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Hybrid%20Vehicles.html">Hybrid Vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/plug-in%20hybrid%20vehicles.html">plug-in hybrid vehicles</a></p>

Moreover, as battery technology evolves, hybrids and plug-ins could be upgraded to even more efficient batteries, negating the legacy effects caused by conventional vehicles upon energy dependence.<br /><br />Instead, the Forbe's plan to energy independence is better highway fuel economy, smaller cars and diesel fuel. Apparently, that's the cheapest way to burn through the rest of the world's oil, which naturally ends our foreign oil dependence of course.<div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Foreign%20Oil%20Dependency.html">Foreign Oil Dependency</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Hybrid%20Vehicles.html">Hybrid Vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/plug-in%20hybrid%20vehicles.html">plug-in hybrid vehicles</a></p>

    <h3 class="post-title">
	 
	 Forget hybrids and EVs: Just make cars smaller and lighter?
	 
    </h3>
    

	         <div class="post-body">
	<div>
      <div style="clear:both;"></div><a onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}" href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/uploaded_images/vw_gti-756650.JPG"><img style="margin: 0pt 10px 10px 0pt; float: left; cursor: pointer; width: 250px; height: 188px;" src="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/uploaded_images/vw_gti-756648.JPG" alt="Smaller, lighter cars and hybrids offer the best immediate and medium path to emissions reductions." border="0" /></a><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic; color: rgb(102, 0, 0);">Could it be this easy?</span><br /><br /><a href="http://www.greencarcongress.com/2010/01/oxford-university-study-finds-downscaling-of-vehicle-size-and-weight-the-best-way-to-reduce-transpor.html#more">According to an Oxford Study</a> the best path to decreasing emissions in autos, in the short term, requires a serious decrease in weight and size.<br /><br />Over the medium term, however, the study finds that <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_cars.htm">hybrid cars</a>, such as the <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_car_types/toyota_prius_hybrid.htm">Toyota Prius</a>, offer "significant savings" while helping electric drive trains evolve.<br /><br />While EVs, plug-in hybrids and fuel cell vehicles offer interesting potential long term, all have serious issues to overcome in the short to medium term, such as raw material availability.<br /><br />Finally, first generation biofuels offer some localized uses. Second generation biofuels show more promise, but will still probably be constrained by land availability. Algae shows some probability of overcoming the land availability issue, but massive innovations and breakthroughs are still required.<div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/biofuels.html">biofuels</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/electric%20cars.html">electric cars</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Hybrid%20Vehicles.html">Hybrid Vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/plug-in%20hybrid%20vehicles.html">plug-in hybrid vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/toyota%20prius.html">toyota prius</a></p>

    <h3 class="post-title">
	 
	 Are the plug-in studies wrong?
	 
    </h3>
    

	         <div class="post-body">
	<div>
      <div style="clear:both;"></div><a onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}" href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/uploaded_images/2011_chevy_volt_silver-736914.jpg"><img style="margin: 0pt 0pt 10px 10px; float: right; cursor: pointer; width: 277px; height: 154px;" src="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/uploaded_images/2011_chevy_volt_silver-736288.jpg" alt="Are plug-in vehicles, such as plug-in hybrid cars, a real and timely solution to foreign oil dependency and reduced CO2 emissions, or are they more of a distraction?" border="0" /></a><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic; color: rgb(102, 0, 0);">Can the Volt revolutionize the auto industry?</span><br /><br />I've followed <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_cars.htm">hybrid cars</a>, intently, for a long time, and 5 years ago I would have predicted much greater hybrid penetration than exists today.  Thus, when Toyota launched the second generation <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_car_types/toyota_prius_hybrid.htm">Prius</a>, I believed auto analysts, such as Anthony Pratt of JD Power, for instance, were crazy when they claimed hybrid market share would barely reach 3 percent by 2010.<br /><br />Unfortunately, however, Mr. Pratt was right.<br /><br />Thus, as study after study - and analyst after analyst -  has concluded that, barring something extraordinary, plug-in vehicle marketshare is going to grow slowly over the next few decades, I've carefully listened. If correct, these studies and analysts demonstrate that it will be decades before plug-in vehicles have any real impact on reducing either foreign oil dependency or CO2 emissions.<br /><br />Based on the last 3 decades of US dependence on foreign oil, another 3 decades seems a scary proposition to this hybrid fan.<br /><br />Therefore, perhaps alternative fuels, such as natural gas, should be considered more seriously in the interim. Likewise, maybe there should be a greater focus on incentives for conventional hybrids, especially ones that can be converted to plug-in hybrids down the road. Or, maybe just a simple tax credit for any car that achieves 50 mpg on the EPA's city cycle is the key.<br /><br />Then again, maybe all the studies and analysts are wrong. Maybe a major breakthrough in lithium-ion chemistry isn't so far-fetched? Or, maybe gas prices are destined to double in the next decade?<br /><br />Why might the bulk of the data piling up against mass plug-in adoption in the next decade or two be wrong?<div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Hybrid%20Vehicles.html">Hybrid Vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/plug-in%20hybrid%20vehicles.html">plug-in hybrid vehicles</a></p>

	 10 percent green equals 100 percent greenwashed
	 
    </h3>
    

	         <div class="post-body">
	<div>

It's more about greenwashing than sales and profits, at least for the next decade or more." border="0" /></a><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic; color: rgb(102, 0, 0);">A meaningless hybrid?</span><br /><br />If you're at the Detroit Auto Show, or following the coverage, you might think the automotive world has really changed. All our troubles are behind and new green vehicles, particularly hybrids and plug-in vehicles, are set to make everything better.<br /><br />Yet, in ten years less than 10 percent of America's automotive fleet will be hybrid or plug-in - at best - according to almost every automotive analyst and researcher.<br /><br />Anyway, this year in Detroit the <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_car_types/Ford_Fusion_Hybrid.htm">Ford Fusion hybrid</a> was awarded The North American Car of the Year. And it's deserving. Everything about the Fusion hybrid is great, except for sales. Sure, almost 30,000 Ford consumers bought a Fusion hybrid last year, however, over 400,000 Ford consumers bought a gas-guzzling Ford F150.<br /><br />And next year in Detroit, the <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_car_types/gm_chevrolet_volt_electric_concept_vehicle.htm">Chevy Volt</a> will probably win The North American Car of the Year. Yet, the Volt will probably move even less units than did the Fusion hybrid in its first year. Even more alarming, by 2020, according to GM executives like Bob Lutz, plug-ins like the Volt will still only appeal to about 5 - 10 percent of consumers.<br /><br />Today, even hybrid king Toyota only offers the third most fuel efficient fleet in America - despite selling 200,000 hybrids - thanks to its many gas-guzzler sales.<br /><br />Sadly, in tens years the US auto fleet won't be much more fuel efficient than it is today, and green vehicles will still be just a niche, but at least we'll get to enjoy a decade of  superb greenwashing along the way.<div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Chevy%20Volt%20electric%20vehicle%20concept.html">Chevy Volt electric vehicle concept</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Ford%20fusion%20hybrid.html">Ford fusion hybrid</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Hybrid%20Vehicles.html">Hybrid Vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/plug-in%20hybrid%20vehicles.html">plug-in hybrid vehicles</a></p>

    </h3>

Thereafter, cost-savings will probably slow. Coincidentally, Toyota knows a little something about the cost-effectiveness of new technologies.<br /><br />Today, about 70 - 80 percent of new car customers are interested in purchasing <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_cars.htm">hybrid cars</a>, a segment dominated by Toyota. With such high interest, why is market share still only about 3 percent? According to consumer surveys it all boils down to costs, and consumer surveys on plug-in hybrids tell a similar story.<br /><br />So, why is Toyota bashed for stating the obvious?<br /><br />Finish: <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/toyota_plug_in_hybrid_vehicles_when_did_toyota_pull_the_plug.htm">When did Toyota pull the plug on plug-in hybrids?</a><div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Chevy%20Volt%20electric%20vehicle%20concept.html">Chevy Volt electric vehicle concept</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/plug-in%20hybrid%20vehicles.html">plug-in hybrid vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/toyota%20prius.html">toyota prius</a></p>
