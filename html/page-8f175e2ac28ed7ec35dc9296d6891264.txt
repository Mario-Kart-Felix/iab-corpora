Or possibly Robert Anderson of Scotland (between 1832 and 1839). Using non-rechargable batteries. Electric vehicles would hold all vehicle land speed records until about 1900.<BR></B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1859: Gaston Plante invented rechargeable lead-acid batteries.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1889: Thomas Edison built an EV using nickel-alkaline batteries.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1895: First auto race in America , won by an EV.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1896: First car dealer  sells only EVs.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1897: First vehicle with power steering  an EV. Electric self-starters 20 years before appearing in gas-powered cars.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1898: NYC blizzard, only EVs were capable of transport on the roads. First woman to buy a car  it was an EV.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1899: Pope Manufacturing Company forms the Electric Vehicle Company, the first large-scale operation in the US automobile industry.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1900: NYCs huge pollution problem  horses. 2.5 million pounds of manure, 60,000 gallons of urine daily on the streets; 15,000 dead horses removed from the streets each year. All US cars produced: 33% steam cars, 33% EV, and 33% gasoline cars. Poll at the National Automobile Show in NYC showed people's first choice for automobiles was electric followed closely by steam.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1901: Oldsmobile EV (Walt Disney's). William McKinley, 25th US President, takes his final ride in an electric ambulance.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1903: First speeding ticket  it was earned in an EV. Krieger company makes a hybrid vehicle  using a gasoline engine to supplement a battery pack.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1904: America has only 7% of the 2 million miles of roads better than dirt  only 141 miles, or less than one mile in 10,000 was paved. Here's a 1904 Curved Dash Olds (replica). Henry Ford begins assembly line production of low-priced gas-powered vehicles.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1908: Henry Ford buys his wife, Clara Ford, an EV. Many socialites of that time gave this rousing endorsement for EVs, It never fails me.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1910: Motorized assembly produces gas-powered cars in volume; reducing cost per vehicle.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1912: 38,842 EVs on the road. Horse drawn tankers deliver gasoline to gas stations. EVs perform well in snow.</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1913: Ford creates experimental EVs [1, 2] . Self starter for gas cars (10 years later for the Model-T).</B></DIV>
<DIV><B><BR>1915: The Detroit Electric Automobile.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1921: Federal Highway Act. By 1922, federal match (50%) for highway construction and repair (for mail delivery). Before this, roads were considered only feeders to railroads, and left to the local jurisdiction to fund.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1956: National System of Interstate and Defense Highways. Funded 90% by states, and 90% by the federal government.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1957: Sputnik is launched. The US space program initiates advanced battery R&amp;D.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1966: Gallup poll: 36 million really interested in EVs. At the time EVs had a top speed of 40 mph, and typical range less than 50 miles.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1967: Walter Laski founds the Electric Auto Association.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1968-1978: Congress passes more regulatory statues than ever before due to health risks associated with cars: collisions, dirty air.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1972: First Annual EAA EV rally.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1974: CitiCar debut at Electric Vehicle Symposium in Washington , DC. Full production also ramps up. </B></DIV>
<DIV><B>By 1975, Vanguard-Sebring, maker of the CitiCar is the 6th largest auto maker in the US. EAA member Roger Hedlund sets first world speed record for EVs at Bonneville Salt Flats.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1976: EAA members assist US Congress in creating the Electric and Hybrid Vehicle Research, Development, and Demonstration Act of 1976.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1977: EAA member Frank Willey developed a transistorized speed controller and earned the IEEE Outstanding Engineering Award. First named the Willey-9 controller, later became the Curtis 1221C.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1983: A fleet of EVs drove from San Jose, CA to San Francisco, CA, 100 mile round trip, on a single charge.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1985: Saied Motai drove 230 miles on a single charge.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1990: California establishes the Zero Emission Vehicle (ZEV) Mandate; requires 2% of vehicles to be ZEVs by 1998, 10% ZEVs by 2003. GM shows their production EV initially named, Impact; later it was re-named the EV-1. (US government spent $194 million on all energy efficient research. Much less than the $1 billion for a single day of Desert Storm, or the $1 billion per week of 2003 Iraq conflict.)</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1991: First Phoenix Solar and Electric 500 race.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1992: EAA supports California $1,000 tax credit for EVs.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1993: EAA member Bob Schneeveis races over 100 mph in a custom-built electric car named "Snow White". The EAA's EV Showcase exhibit is featured at WESCON Electronics Trade Show in San Francisco. GM estimated that it would take 3 months to collect names of 5,000 people interested in the EV-1  it only took one week!</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1994: Twelve additional states adopt the California ZEV mandates. The GM Impact EV (later to be named the EV-1) sets a 187 mph speed record.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1995: Renaissance Cars, Inc begins production of the Tropica.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1996: EAA helps to hatch CALSTART incubator (for EV research) in Alameda , CA. Solectria Sunrise breaks the 300 mile range at the NESEA Tour de Sol. GM begins production of the EV-1 (formerly called the Impact).</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>1997: Toyota Prius hybrid gas-electric vehicle unveiled at the Tokyo Auto Show as the first production hybrid vehicle. First National Electric Drag Racing Association (NEDRA) event in Woodburn, Oregon.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>2000: Ford offers the Th!nk City EV, it's version of the Pivco, in California.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>2001: CARB upholds the ZEV Mandate of between 4,000 and 15,000 EVs starting in 2003. Dr. Andy Frank and his UC Davis Team Fate produce demonstration plug-in hybrid vehicles.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>2002: EAA launches the 1st annual Chapter's meeting in Washington, D.C. Toyota RAV4-EV retail sales begins; their estimated 2-year supply sold out in 8 months. Ford sells the Th!nk City Group.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>2003: ZEV Mandate weakened to allow ZEV credits for non-ZEVs. Only requires 250 fuel-cell vehicles by 2009. Toyota stops production of the RAV4-EV; Honda stops lease renewals of the EV-Plus; GM does the same for the EV-1.</B></DIV><BR>
<DIV><B>2003: AC Propulsions tZero earns highest grade at the Michelin Challenge Bibendum; tZero specs: 300 miles per charge, 0-60mph in 3.6 seconds, 100 mph top speed.</B></DIV><BR>
