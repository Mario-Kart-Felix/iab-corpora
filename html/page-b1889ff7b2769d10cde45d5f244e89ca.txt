</font><font FACE="Arial"><strong><font color="#FF0000">Things To Consider When Wiring Accessories...</font></strong></p>
<p>                   
    
<font FACE="Times New Roman">  
  
<img border="0" src="mess-a-wires.jpg" width="125" height="150" align="right"></font>When wiring anything in your vehicle that draws heavy current such as high powered        
  offroad lights or audio amplifiers, there are a few things to consider.&nbsp; Number one, make sure you use        
  wire that is rated for the amperage that the accessories is going to pull.&nbsp;        
  It is        
  always better to have wire that is OVER rated rather than wire that is not rated high        
  enough.&nbsp; If wire is used that is not rated to handle the current that your accessory        
  will pull, the result could be overheated wires that could melt the insulation 
or the electrical plugs found throughout your vehicle (see image below), causing a        
  short or worse yet it could result in a fire.&nbsp; If you know how much current your        
  accessory will draw you can determine what gauge wire is appropriate for your application.</p></font>

I like to use wire that far exceeds the current draw of my accessory.&nbsp;&nbsp; 
It&#39;s overkill but in a few applications I&#39;ve used heavy gauge stranded 
industrial wire with water and chemical resistant insulation. That way there is 
no question as to whether the wire is rated high enough or not.&nbsp; If this 
approach is taken, it is very wise to place a fuse at the battery end as close 
to the battery as possible.&nbsp; Most wire in a vehicle, if shorted out, will 
burn up before the battery overheats and possibly explodes.&nbsp; If wire that 
is over-rated for vehicle use is used and a short occurs, a short will most 
likely result in damage to the vehicle of some sort unless a fuse is put in line 
as close to the battery as possible.&nbsp; With the fuse there, in the case of a 
dead short, the fuse will burn out first before any damage could occur.&nbsp; </font>
            </td>

<font FACE="Arial">  
  
<p><b>Where to Get your Power</b></p>                   
    
</font>                   
    
<font FACE="Times New Roman">  
  
<p><font FACE="Arial">The second important thing to consider when adding 
accessories is to determine where you will get your power.&nbsp; Many people 
will consider tapping into the hot wire of an existing circuit using something 
called a T-tap or quick slide connector, which are designed specifically for </font>  
  </font><font FACE="Arial">  
  
tapping into an existing wire.&nbsp; This is not always a good idea for several 
reasons.&nbsp; First and foremost, tapping into an existing circuit puts 
additional load on that circuit.&nbsp; The additional load placed on a circuit 
or wire may exceed the limits of those wires, connectors and fuse.&nbsp; 
Overloading a circuit will do one of two things.&nbsp; First, it may blow a 
fuse.&nbsp; So people will sometimes place a larger fuse in the fuse block to 
handle the extra load on that circuit.&nbsp; BAD IDEA!&nbsp; The fuse may hold but the resulting 
extra current may be more that the wire is rated for creating heat that can melt wires and connectors and start fires!&nbsp; 
The connector to the right was overloaded and fused two adjacent terminals 
together, causing a short the blew the vehicles main fuse.&nbsp; Before the fuse 
blew the connector actually started to burn.&nbsp; The wire that carried the 
extra load also had melted its insulation and in places was exposed.&nbsp; 
Luckily for the owner of this vehicle, it did not result in a fire but in many 
cases it does.</font></p>
<p><b><font face="Arial">A better idea....</font></b></p>
    
<font FACE="Times New Roman">  
  
<p><font FACE="Arial">With accessories that pull a lot of power it is always better to get your power        
  directly from the batteries positive terminal rather than tapping into the existing fuse        
  block or wiring harness.&nbsp; In most cases the vehicles existing fuse blocks, 
wires and connectors are not rated        
  to handle the additional load of high powered accessories such as offroad lights 
and amplifiers.&nbsp;        
  If        
  you are the kind of person that likes to add all kind of goodies to your vehicle it might        
  be worth installing an additional fuse block that handles non-critical items like offroad        
  lights, CB radios, power inverters, audio equipment, etc.&nbsp; This additional block can then be powered        
  by a heavy duty wire capable of carrying the current required of all the accessories on the block.&nbsp; Be sure to fuse the block at the battery.</font></td>
    </tr>
  </table>
</div>

<table border="0" cellpadding="5" cellspacing="0" id="table16">  
  <tr>  
    <td width="100%" valign="top" align="left">
    <img border="0" src="r-shack_30a_relay_275-226.jpg" width="191" height="214" align="right"></font><font FACE="Arial">Another 
    important thing to consider is how you control the switching on/off of the 
    power to your accessories.&nbsp;  
  </font><font FACE="Times New Roman">  
  
    <font face="Arial">In almost every case where high current is        
    required, the switch you will use to turn on the power should not handle the load 
    because most switches are not designed to handle higher amps.&nbsp;        
      The job of switching the power is        
    better left to a relay. &nbsp; What is a relay?&nbsp; A relay is a device that, through a        
    magnetic induction coil, turns on the power for you.&nbsp; The switch that is installed in        
    the cab of your 4x4 actually only powers the relay itself which draws very little        
    current.&nbsp; In most&nbsp; installations a 30 AMP relay from Radio Shack (Auto Relay        
    Cat. Number 275-226) will do unless your amperage demands exceed 30 amps.&nbsp;        
      I like to        
    used a lighted switch in the cab to let me know if driving lights are on.<br>

    above.&nbsp; As in the diagram a wire is run from a 12 volt power source to the switch

      <font size="5"><b><a name="Ohms-Law">Ohms Law</a></b></font><p align="left">                   
    
<font FACE="Times New Roman">  
  
    <img border="0" src="../../../images/misc/ohmlaw.gif" width="245" height="239" align="right"></font><b><font size="4">Ohm&#39;s 
    Law Defined<br>
    <br>
    </font><font size="2">Ohm&#39;s Law defines the relationships between (P) power, 
    (E) voltage, (I) current, and (R) resistance. One ohm is the resistance 
    value through which one volt will maintain a current of one ampere. <br>
    ( I ) Current is what flows on a wire or conductor. Current is measured in 
    (A) amperes or amps. </font></b>
    <p align="left"><b><font size="2">( E ) Voltage is the difference in 
    electrical potential between two points in a circuit. It&#39;s the push or 
    pressure behind current flow through a circuit, and is measured in (V) 
    volts. </font></b>
    <p align="left"><b><font size="2">( R ) Resistance determines how much 
    current will flow through a component. Resistors are used to control voltage 
    and current levels. The higher the resistance, the smaller the amount of 
    current is allowed to flow. Resistance is measured in ohms. </font></b>
    <p align="left"><b><font size="2">( P ) Power is the amount of current times 
    the voltage level at a given point measured in wattage or watts.</font></b><p align="left">
    <b><font size="4">V = I x R <br>
    I = V / R <br>
    R = V / I <br>
