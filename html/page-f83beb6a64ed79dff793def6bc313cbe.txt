	 <h3 class="storytitle">
			Switch Cabling And&nbsp;Auto-MDIX		 </h3>
	<div class="meta">Filed under:  <a href="http://cciepursuit.wordpress.com/category/cabling/" title="View all posts in Cabling" rel="category tag">Cabling</a>,<a href="http://cciepursuit.wordpress.com/category/home-lab/" title="View all posts in Home Lab" rel="category tag">Home Lab</a>,<a href="http://cciepursuit.wordpress.com/category/ios/" title="View all posts in IOS" rel="category tag">IOS</a>,<a href="http://cciepursuit.wordpress.com/category/switching/" title="View all posts in Switching" rel="category tag">Switching</a> &#8212; cciepursuit @ 6:28 am <br /></div>

	<div class="storycontent">
		<p><a target="_blank" href="http://www.groupstudy.com/archives/ccielab/200701/msg00718.html">This thread in GroupStudy about using Auto-MDIX to enable the use of straight-through cables between switches</a> reminded me of a real world issue involving the same technology.  At my previous job, there was a &#8220;senior&#8221; engineer who supported a large site.  She was having an issue with hard-setting the speed and duplex of an uplink port (I think it was a &#8220;stack&#8221; of 2950s) on a Cisco switch.  While most of our uplinks were fiber, this one was copper (it was a small office that just needed to connect to the MDF on the floor directly above it).  Our standard was to use a cross-over cable for any switch-to-switch connections and to hard-set the speed and duplex on both sides of the connection.  She was having a problem with setting the speed and duplex.  If she hard-set these settings on both sides of the link, then the link would go down.  If she let one side of the link auto-negotiate, the link would come up, but the connection would not meet our standards.  I eventually got dragged into this issue.</p>
<p>Looking at the switch settings, I noticed something odd: auto-MDIX was enabled (globally) on both switches.  Our standard was to disable this on every switch.  This gave us a little protection from users making unauthorized switch-to-switch connections (accidentally or on purpose) as cross-over cables were not generally available to end users (most wouldn&#8217;t know what a cross-over cable was).  I asked the engineer if she was using a cross-over cable on the uplink. </p>
<p>Her response: &#8220;That&#8217;s not the problem.  You can use a straight-through cable.&#8221; </p>
<p>&#8220;Sure you CAN, but our standard is to use crossover cables because&#8230;.blah, blah, blah.&#8221;</p>
<p>Long story shorter: she refused to adhere to our standards and use a cross-over cable and I refused to waste my time on the issue.  I wasn&#8217;t sure that the cabling was the issue, but if disabling auto-MDIX and using the proper cabling did not fix the issue then we could at least take those elements out of the equation. </p>
<p>She muddled on for a couple more days (she couldn&#8217;t be bothered to open a TAC case) and the problem remained.  Finally our top-level LAN guy looked into the issue.  <a target="_blank" href="http://www.cisco.com/univercd/cc/td/doc/product/lan/cat3560/12225see/scg/swint.htm#wp1193977">He found the answer</a> after a few minutes on Cisco&#8217;s web site (this is specific to the 3560):</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>Configuring Auto-MDIX on an Interface</strong><br />
When automatic medium-dependent interface crossover (auto-MDIX) is enabled on an interface, the interface automatically detects the required cable connection type (straight through or crossover) and configures the connection appropriately. When connecting switches without the auto-MDIX feature, you must use straight-through cables to connect to devices such as servers, workstations, or routers and crossover cables to connect to other switches or repeaters. <strong>With auto-MDIX enabled, you can use either type of cable to connect to other devices, and the interface automatically corrects for any incorrect cabling.</strong> For more information about cabling requirements, see the hardware installation guide.</p>
<p><strong>Auto-MDIX is enabled by default. When you enable auto-MDIX, you must also set the interface speed and duplex to auto so that the feature operates correctly.</strong> Auto-MDIX is supported on all 10/100 and 10/100/1000-Mbps interfaces and on 10/100/1000BASE-TX small form-factor pluggable (SFP)-module interfaces. It is not supported on 1000BASE-SX or -LX SFP module interfaces.</p></blockquote>
<p>So you can use a straight through cable to connect two switches, but you then <strong>cannot </strong>explicitly set the speed and duplex.  The engineer was told that the solution to her problem was to disable auto-MDIX and use a cross-over cable.  Where had I heard that before?  <img src='http://s0.wp.com/wp-includes/images/smilies/icon_smile.gif?m=1129645325g' alt=':-)' class='wp-smiley' /> </p>
<p>To be fair, I wasn&#8217;t aware of the auto-MDIX needing auto-negotiation issue, BUT whenever you are troubleshooting a problem you want to minimize any deviations from known working configurations to eliminate unnecessary variables.  I think that I remember reading that auto-MDIX uses the same protocol as auto-negotiation and that&#8217;s why both need to be enabled.  I&#8217;m not positive about that though.</p>
<p>This table is pretty interesting.  I assume that &#8220;correct cabling&#8221; means a cross-over cable and &#8220;incorrect cabling&#8221; means a straight-through cable.</p>

<p>This makes sense because you need at least one side to be able to logically switch the pinouts of a straight-through cable to emulate a cross-over cable.  Hard-setting the speed or duplex disables the auto-negotiation protocol (which auto-MDIX must utilize as well) which effectively disables auto-MDIX:</p>
<p><strong>Note:</strong> The only command that I know of that will show the auto-MDIX state of an interface (other than looking at the running-configuration of the interface) is the rather verbose &#8220;show controllers ethernet-controller fax/x phy | include MDIX&#8221; command.</p>
<p><strong>Note:</strong> The default setting for switch ports is to have auto-MDIX enabled.  This is a pretty recent change though.  IOS versions prior to 12.2(20)SE will use the default of &#8220;no mdix auto&#8221;.</p>
<blockquote><p><strong>&#8220;mdix auto&#8221; is the default, so it does not show in the running-configuration:</strong><br />
sw2(config)#do sh run int fa0/32<br />
Building configuration&#8230;<br />
Current configuration : 34 bytes<br />
!<br />
interface FastEthernet0/32<br />
end</p>
<p><strong>We can verify that auto-MDIX is on for this interface:<br />
</strong>sw2(config)#<strong>do sh controll eth fa0/32 phy | i MD<br />
</strong> <strong>Auto-MDIX                             :  On</strong>   [AdminState=1   Flags=0x00052248]</p>
<p><strong>Let&#8217;s hard-set the speed and see what happens to auto-MDIX:<br />
</strong>sw2(config)#int fa0/32<br />
sw2(config-if)#<strong>speed 100<br />
</strong>sw2(config-if)#do sh control eth fa0/32 phy | i MD<br />
 <strong>Auto-MDIX                             :  Off</strong>   [AdminState=1   Flags=0x00010A48]</p>
<p><strong>Notice that our configuration does not state that auto-MDIX has been disabled:<br />
</strong>sw2(config-if)#do sh run int fa0/32<br />
Building configuration&#8230;</p>
<p>Current configuration : 45 bytes<br />
!<br />
interface FastEthernet0/32 <br />
 speed 100<br />
end</p></blockquote>
<p>This verifies that hard-setting the speed and/or duplex turns off auto-MDIX for the interface.</p>
<p><strong>Note:</strong> I did test to see if DTP was effected by auto-MDIX.  It was not.  As long as the link was up, DTP could work it&#8217;s trunking magic.</p>
<p>So the long and the short of it is: you can use straight-through cables to connect two Cisco switches as long as you are willing to sacrifice the ability to hard-set the speed and/or duplex on both sides of the link.</p>

<li class="comment byuser comment-author-cciepursuit bypostauthor even thread-even depth-1 highlander-comment" id="comment-6009">
	<div id="div-comment-6009">
		<div class="comment-author vcard">
			<img alt='' src='http://0.gravatar.com/avatar/38975619408ca2a3a410360da532c29c?s=32&amp;d=monsterid' class='avatar avatar-32' height='32' width='32' />			<p>You&#8217;re right about auto-negotiation working most of the time, and that one of the reasons why we leave all of our end user switch ports set to auto/auto. But there are two important reasons for hard-setting server switch ports and requiring that the server owner do the same with their NIC:</p>
<p>1) Auto negotiation failures.  If auto-negotiation of duplex fails, it will default to half-duplex mode.  A server connected at 100/half is not a happy box.</p>
<p>2) Billing. In the companies I have worked for, the network team bills a &#8220;port charge&#8221;.  We require that the server owners fill out business requests to specify the speed and duplex of their devices.  We then hard-set the server port to that setting and require the server owners to do the same on their NIC. The port charge increases as the port speed increases.  All of our datacenter switches have ports capable of 1000Mbps.  We&#8217;ll often get trouble tickets about server problems only to find out that server owner has changed their NIC to 1000Mbps without requesting the port change.  If we ran auto negotiation, then the port would most likely auto negotiate to 1000Mbps.</p>
<p>A bit off topic: The server teams figured out a way around this process by calling the NOC and having them either hard set the port to a different speed or to set the port to auto/auto.  We eventually wrote a script that runs every Wednesday at midnight that goes though all of the ports and verifies that they are set to the speed that is in the business request.  If the speed is different then the script sets the port to the correct speed.  This will usually &#8220;break&#8221; the server resulting in the server owner getting paged out in the wee hours of the morning.  The next day we check the logging server to see who changed the speed/duplex on the switch and notify their manager.  Needless to say, we&#8217;ve had a sharp decrease in NOC port reconfigurations.</p>
<p>Anyhoo&#8230;I agree that there are going to be more issues with mismatches if you are not running auto/auto on every port, but implementing a corporate standard to hard set server ports is useful for the reasons above, PLUS it ends finger-pointing pretty quickly during troubleshooting.</p>
