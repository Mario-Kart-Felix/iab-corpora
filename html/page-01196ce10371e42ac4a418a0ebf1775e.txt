						<h3 id="section---WhyShouldIEarnAnAutomotiveAndDieselTechnologyDegree">Why Should I Earn an Automotive and Diesel Technology Degree?</h3>
<p>According to the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), you may be able to receive training in automotive and diesel technology at the high school level (<i>www.bls.gov</i>). However, the BLS further states that while secondary school programs may be helpful, they can also vary a great deal in their content and focus. Increasingly, employers prefer to hire someone who has completed a formal, postsecondary education program.
</p>
<p>The National Center for Education Statistics (NCES) lists over 500 community colleges and technical institutes that offer an associate's degree in automotive or diesel technology. In addition, the National Automotive Technicians Education Foundation (NATEF) maintains an online directory of schools offering programs that have been certified by the National Institute for Automotive Service Excellence (ASE).
</p>
<h3 id="section---WhatSortOfCertificationOrLicensingIsAvailable">What Sort of Certification or Licensing is Available?</h3>
<p>Following your completion of an ASE-certified program and 1-2 years of relevant work experience, you may be eligible to sit for one or more of the ASE certification examinations. As mentioned by the BLS, ASE certification is the industry standard for automotive and diesel technicians. Though certification isn't legally required, you may find that it enhances your chances of employment and advancement.
</p>
<p>You must pass all the tests in a given series to be certified as a master technician. Every five years, you will need to be re-tested in order to maintain your certification.
</p>
<h3 id="section---WhatCanIExpectInAProgram">What Can I Expect in a Program?</h3>
<p>A program usually takes two years to complete and can consist of 64-136 credits. The difference may be explained in part by the fact that some schools offer a degree program that combines both automotive and diesel technology, while others offer them as individual programs. Completion of a program can lead to an Associate in Occupational Studies (A.O.S.) or Associate of Applied Science (A.A.S.) degree.
</p>
<p>Typical courses you might encounter include suspension and steering, electrical and electronics systems, welding and metal repair, powertrains, computerized engine management systems, transportation HVAC systems, hydraulics and preventative maintenance. You'll find that most technical courses consist of classroom and lab work. You may have the opportunity to serve an internship at a school-partnered, independent shop, garage or repair facility.
</p>
<p>Because of the large number of hands-on training requirements, there are no completely online associate's degree programs in automotive and diesel technology. You may find cases in which schools allow you to complete some of your didactic or general education requirements online.
</p>
<h3 id="section---WhatAreSomeEmploymentAndWageProjections">What Are Some Employment and Wage Projections?</h3>
<p>The BLS projected that from 2008-2018, employment opportunities for automotive and diesel technicians would increase five percent and six percent, respectively. This may be due to several factors, including the growing number of cars and trucks in operation, the improved longevity of vehicles and the increasing use of diesel engines in automobiles.
</p>
<p>By becoming certified in a number of areas, you may be able to advance to a supervisory or management position. In 2010, the BLS determined the mean annual wage for automotive mechanics to be $38,200, while the salary for diesel mechanics was determined to be $42,250.</p>
