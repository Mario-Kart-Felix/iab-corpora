		<div id="section-0" class="section summary">
			<p>Minivans and crossover SUVs are vehicles that, in terms of their appearance and uses, blur the lines between trucks and cars. Both classes of vehicle are based on car platforms, and generally offer a more car-like ride and handling than full-size vans or truck-based SUVs. While crossovers and minivans are aimed at different sectors of the market, they both place an emphasis on utility, passenger room and cargo space.</p>
		</div>

		<div id="section-1" class="section"><h2>Platforms</h2>
		<div id="section-1" class="section">
				<p>Crossovers and minivans are generally constructed with a unibody design in which the vehicle's body provides strength and stability, as opposed to the design used in most large trucks and vans, in which the vehicle's body is bolted to a separate frame. Many early minivans and crossovers were based on existing sedan platforms in their manufacturers' lineups. Some current minivans and crossovers share a platform; the original Honda Odyssey minivan, for example, was based on the Accord platform, and the current Odyssey shares a platform with the Pilot SUV.</p>
				
				
					</div>
	</div><div id="section-2" class="section"><h2>Body Design</h2>
		<div id="section-2" class="section">
				<p>The body design of both minivans and crossovers is typically a &quot;two-box&quot; configuration, in which the body is divided into two primary volumes: an engine compartment, and a combined passenger/cargo space. This is in contrast to a typical passenger car &quot;three-box&quot; design, in which the body is divided into separate engine, passenger and cargo volumes. Minivans usually provide access to their second- and third-row seats via one or two side sliding doors, while crossovers are typically equipped with four hinged doors like a passenger car; this design difference makes a minivan better suited to uses that require easy, frequent access to the passenger compartment. Most minivans and crossovers are equipped with a liftgate for rear access to cargo space.</p>
				
				
					</div>
	</div><div id="section-3" class="section"><h2>Seating</h2>
		<div id="section-3" class="section">
				<p>Most minivans feature seating for eight passengers in three rows of seats, but some models, such as the Nissan Quest, provide seating for just two passengers in the second row, for a total capacity of only seven passengers. Smaller crossovers are likely to have seating for five passengers in two rows of seats, although some roomier crossovers, such as the Honda Pilot, Toyota Highlander, Mazda CX-9 and Chevrolet Traverse, are equipped with a third row of seats and can accommodate seven or eight passengers.</p>
				
				
					</div>
	</div><div id="section-4" class="section"><h2>Fuel Economy</h2>
		<div id="section-4" class="section">
				<p>Minivans typically get better gas mileage than full-size SUVs; the 2012 Honda Odyssey, for example, achieves 21 mpg combined city/highway, while the 2012 Chevrolet Suburban manages only 17 mpg. However, comparably sized crossovers get mileage comparable to that of minivans; the 2012 Pilot gets the same 21 mpg that its sibling, the Odyssey, gets. Smaller compact crossovers are often even more economical; in the Honda lineup, the 2012 CR-V is rated at 26 mpg, which is significantly better than either the Pilot or the Odyssey.</p>
				
				
					</div>
