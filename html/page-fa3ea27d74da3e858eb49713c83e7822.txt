            <p>
<p>On paper, the updated-for-2013 <a href="http://www.autoweek.com/section/Hyundai&template=newcars">Hyundai</a> Genesis R-Spec, with its 348-hp V6, should've been the quickest of the seven less-than-$30,000 sports cars we assembled for the test at MIS. As far as top speed goes, it was, easily eclipsing 135 mph down the main straightaway at the track.</p><p>But illustrating that horsepower is just one element of speed, it was the 2013 <a href="http://www.autoweek.com/section/Mustang&template=newcar&make=Ford">V6-powered Ford Mustang</a>, with its 305 hp, that took the overall title of quickest of the field&#8212;despite being electronically speed-limited to 112 mph. That top speed was hit about halfway down the nearly one-mile-long straightaway, right about at the bus-stop chicane.</p><p>The Mustang is a better-handling car, too, especially when fitted with the optional V6 Performance package as on our tester. The package (code 67B) includes a strut-tower brace, larger antiroll front bar and spec SVT rear antiroll bar, stiffer front springs, bigger front and rear brake calipers, 19-inch-by-8.5-inch Foundry Black-painted machined-aluminum wheels fitted with Pirelli 255/40R-19 performance tires and revised stability-control calibration. </p><p>The Mustang suspension handled the rough transition from the banked oval onto the flat road course and vice versa better than any car in the test.</p><p>The Pirellis were especially helpful on track, providing excellent grip over the two days of testing and allowing the 'Stang to lap the course in 1:35.96, edging the Genesis' best time of 1:36.61 despite being speed limited. </p><p>The brakes held up well, too, only starting to fade after two or three consecutive hot laps; the Genesis' brakes got mushy after just one. In our 60-to-0-mph tests, the Mustang needed 117 feet, while the Genesis took 125 feet.</p><p>The 3.31 limited-slip rear differential helped with the Mustang's 5.75-second 0-to-60-mph time; the Genesis' best was 5.93. The Mustang also beat the Genesis in the quarter-mile, turning a 14.4-second time to the Hyundai's 14.57. Our test 'Stang had the optional Recaro leather sport seats, and even at $1,995 for the pair, we'd check that box on the order sheet if a Mustang was headed to our garage. </p><p>The Genesis is a fun car to drive, with all of that horsepower making it easy to hang the rear end out. Sometimes, it's too easy. But a clunky shifter, relatively flat-bottomed seats and tires that didn't provide as much grip as the Mustang's made driving the car at the track a handful. While we are big fans of the mongo-horsepower versions of the Mustang GT, the V6 car with the Performance package opened our eyes to the fact that you don't need 500 hp to have a lot of fun.
</p>
