	<div class="article cms_clear restore postcontainer"><div align="center">
  <table border="3" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" width="99%">
    <tr>
      <td><table border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" width="100%">
      	<tr>
      		<td width="25%" bordercolor="#F0DBC7" bgcolor="#F0DBC7">&nbsp;</td>
      		<td width="50%" bordercolor="#F0DBC7" bgcolor="#F0DBC7" valign="top"><br>
        	<P align="center"><STRONG><font size="4">If a 4x4 Crashes in the Woods&hellip;</font></STRONG><br><font size="1">By Sean Cundall</font></P>
					<P><BR><BR></P>
					<P><STRONG>The National Highway Traffic Safety Association</STRONG> doesn't keep 
					<A HREF="http://www.carinsurancelist.com/car-accident-statistics.htm" target="_blank">car	accident statistics</A>
					for rollovers that happen in the woods, or the times cars get stuck
					in the sand, but it's safe to assume that off-road insurance wouldn't
					even exist if there weren't a significant number of incidents either
					with off-road vehicles (ORV), or adventurous drivers with 4x4s.</P>
					<P><STRONG>If you don't have</STRONG> a special off-road policy already, you should resist
					the urge to take your four-wheel drive vehicle off the beaten track
					until you've read your existing insurance policy, and checked for
					coverage. Some insurers do include extremely limited off-road
					coverage, but most don't cover all four-wheeling activities. If your
					policy does not explicitly mention off-road activities, you probably
					aren't covered.</FONT></P>
					<P><STRONG>If you go off-roading</STRONG> under a standard policy, and get into an accident
					you will very likely have trouble getting your existing insurer to
					pay out a claim, and if they do cover repairs, you should expect your
					premium to be increased significantly.</P>
					<P><STRONG>There Are Options</STRONG></P>
					<P><STRONG>The only truly </STRONG><A HREF="http://www.carinsurancelist.com/" target="_blank">cheap
					auto insurance</A>for off-roading is designed specifically for ORVs, but if you're
					determined to go off-road in the same 4x4 you drive every day, you
					should know that there are options for coverage. They are:</P>
					<UL>
					<LI><STRONG>Add a recreational use rider</STRONG> to your regular insurance policy. This will
					increase your policy, but not as much as a non-covered accident
					would, and it will include many of the extra features as an ORV
					policy.
					<LI><STRONG>Contact small carriers</STRONG>, or specialty carriers, to see if they can cover a
					daily driver for off-road use. You may be able to do this as
					supplemental insurance, or you may have to move your policy
					entirely, but there are ways to get this coverage.
					</UL>
					<P><STRONG>Whichever option you select</STRONG>, you'll want to make sure that the quote you
					receive includes the following:</P>
					<UL>
					<LI><Strong>Coverage for safety gear</strong>,
					including protective clothing, helmets and goggles
					<LI><strong>Replacement and repair</strong> of aftermarket parts.
					<LI><strong>Increased liability coverage limits</strong>
					<LI><strong>Coverage for non-collision accidents</strong>, including medical care if you flip your truck.
					</UL>
					<P><strong>All of these items</strong> are generally
					excluded from traditional policies, but standard with ORV
					policies.&nbsp;Recreational use riders may not cover gear or
					aftermarket parts.</P>
					<P><strong>Another alternative</strong> is to invest
					in a dedicated ORV. This can be a dune buggy or ATC, but it's
					possible to simply designate a truck that is no longer street-legal
					as off-road only and cover it as a rEcreational vehicle. In that
					case, however, you'd have to acquire a tow-trailer or sled, because
					such vehicles are not allowed to roll on county roads.</P>
					<P><strong>Off-roading is becoming
					increasingly popular</strong> as an inexpensive but intensely active family
					hobby, but it should never be considered without insurance coverage.
					Knowing your policy, and knowing your options, will save you money in
					the long run.</P>
