  <div id="text" class="entry-content">
        <p> The world would be a better place, don't we all know, if only that swashbuckling neighbor with the red Ferrari could be taken down a peg or two.</p><p> There is hope, it turns out, of at least an embarrassing pratfall for Mr. <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=news&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Fancy+Pants%22">Fancy Pants</a>, thanks to your friendly neighborhood tax collector. </p><p> The <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=news&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22California+Franchise+Tax+Board%22">California Franchise Tax Board</a> has been scrutinizing the car registrations<strong />of the people who purchase luxury vehicles and comparing them with tax records. Lo and behold, quite a few of them are tax cheats. </p><p> The pilot program, which began in 2008, has nabbed 6,000 luxury-car owners who did not file tax returns. As a result, 1,400 additional returns have been filed, and $3 million has been collected. More than $14 million has also been recovered from the owners of high-priced <a href="http://www.sfgate.com/autos/">automobiles</a> who had previously pleaded for relief, saying they could no longer afford to pay their taxes. That's $17 million total from the high-rolling tax evaders. </p><h3 class="subhead" style="subhead">DMV shares records</h3><p>"The general feeling is that if they can afford a car like that, then they can pay their taxes," said <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=news&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Daniel+Tahara%22">Daniel Tahara</a>, spokesman for the Franchise Tax Board. "We're trying to identify people who are either rich or middle class who say they can't afford to pay taxes, but then they are purchasing these expensive cars. I guess you can consider it lying."</p><p> The luxury-auto program pores over the car registrations of 1.5 million people a year, essentially targeting anyone who pays $40,000 or more for a car. The <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=news&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Department+of+Motor+Vehicles%22">Department of Motor Vehicles</a> provides the registration information to the tax board, which then cross-references the names with its list of people who have filed tax returns. About 3 percent of the car buyers whose records are pulled turn out to be tax cheats, Tahara said. </p><p> In truth, only a fraction of the tax-evading car owners shelled out movie-star money for their wheels, but they didn't peruse Craigslist for dented Buicks either. </p><p>"We see a Lamborghini or a Ferrari here and there," Tahara said, "but the majority are BMWs, Mercedes, <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=news&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Lexus%22">Lexus</a> and things like that." </p><p> The state's beleaguered budget can use every penny the board squeezes out of the taxpayers, Tahara said. But the important thing about the program, he said, is that it restores a sense of fairness to the plain folk who dip into their children's college funds to pay taxes while wheezing around in beat-up cars with dog hair on the seats.<strong /></p><p><strong /></p><h3 class="subhead" style="subhead">The tax gap</h3><p>The luxury-auto program is one of many efforts by the tax board to close what officials call the tax gap - the difference between what taxpayers owe and what they pay. California's tax gap has been estimated by economists to be a whopping $6.5 billion a year - a sum that could surely put a dent in the state's $26.6 billion deficit. </p><p> California residents who make more than $14,754 a year are required by law to file tax returns. Married people or registered domestic partners making $29,508 or more have to file, according to California law. </p><p>"So you already see that if you are purchasing a vehicle that's $40,000 and above, you are already over the threshold for filing taxes," Tahara said. "If we didn't have this program, we would be leaving a lot of money on the table."</p><p> Tax gap enforcement efforts have been greatly expanded in recent years. Businesses, people who claim false withholdings or dependents, and others have been targeted and tracked via computer databases and other means.</p><h3 class="subhead" style="subhead">Notice to non-filers</h3><p>Since the 2005-06 fiscal year, $450 million in additional revenue has been collected, not including the luxury-car program. Tahara said 900,000 notices have been sent out so far this fiscal year notifying people who didn't file tax returns that they are required to do so. Those identified through the luxury-vehicle program are notified that they need to file California tax returns or come up with a legitimate reason why they cannot. Tax officials have the authority to seize the cars and sell them at auction, but Tahara said most people pay up before they lose their ride.</p><p> It should be noted, Tahara said, that not every Lamborghini driver who fails to pay taxes is a tax cheat. </p><p> "We are seeing some instances where people originally identified through these records subsequently did have financial problems and then couldn't pay taxes," he said. "They've been hit by the economy and really do need our help in relaxing some of the collection activities."</p><p>Still, there is a certain element of schadenfreude among taxpayers, including, perhaps, envious neighbors, when the big shots in the Maseratis get nailed. </p><p> "Some people try to avoid paying taxes or delay paying taxes any way they can," Tahara said. "It's our job to get them to pay their fair share."</p><p /><p /><p class="dtlcomment">E-mail <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=news&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Peter+Fimrite%22">Peter Fimrite</a> at <a href="mailto:pfimrite@sfchronicle.com">pfimrite@sfchronicle.com</a>.</p>
