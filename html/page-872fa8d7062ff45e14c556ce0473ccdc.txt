  <div class="article-body clearfix">

              <p>Electric vehicles have earned a place at the <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22San+Antonio+International+Auto%22">San Antonio International Auto</a> &amp; Truck Show for the first time this year.</p>
<p>They're presence wasn't overwhelming, but the vehicles were drawing plenty of lookers. The <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Alamo+Area+Council+of+Government%22">Alamo Area Council of Government</a>'s second annual electric car conference also was held as part of the show.</p>
<p>The electric vehicles at the show range from the all-electric Nissan Leaf to the gas-electric Chevrolet Volt and the sleek Fisker EV Karma — priced at $112,850, it's clearly a dream vehicle except for those with plenty of disposable income.</p>
<p>Richard and <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Jeanne+Garcia%22">Jeanne Garcia</a> checked out the Nissan Leaf at the 43rd annual auto show, which continues through Sunday at the Convention Center.</p>
<p>“There are no studies on the longevity of the battery,” <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Richard+Garcia%22">Richard Garcia</a> said, adding that he would be concerned about the high cost of replacing a battery.</p>
<p>And both wondered if the Texas heat would shorten the life of a battery, and whether an all-electric car like the Leaf would give them the range they need in sprawling San Antonio.</p>
<p>Still, if gas prices were to jump sharply higher, some of their hesitation about buying a hybrid or all-electric car would “fade away,” Richard said.</p>
<p>The Leaf also drew the attention of <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Glenn+Thigpen%22">Glenn Thigpen</a>.</p>
<p>“I would consider it, but I would prefer a hybrid above an all-electric for the greater range,” he said.</p>
<p>And he said he doesn't think many people are prepared to pay as much as $15,000 more for a gas-electric hybrid or all-electric car compared with gas-powered autos.</p>
<p>“When they get the price point down, I think people will jump in,” he said.</p>
<p><a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Anthony+Perl%22">Anthony Perl</a>, a transportation expert and author, most recently, of “Transport Revolutions: Moving People and Freight without Oil,” says electric cars will be important, but electric rail should come first because it's a mature technology that's already used in many countries. It's also incredibly safe: Japan has had no fatalities related to its bullet train since it was introduced in 1964.</p>
<p>Perl spoke at the AACOG conference at the auto show.</p>
<p>He said conventional wisdom is that roughly half of the world's oil still is in the ground, so humans have plenty of time to figure out what a post-oil world will look like — a risky stance to take.</p>
<p>Much of the remaining oil will be more expensive to remove, refine and transport, meaning costs will continue to rise, he said.</p>
<p>And because oil products power 95 percent of the transportation sector, it's imperative that the U.S. focus now on creating the infrastructure for a post-carbon transportation system, he added.</p>
<p>He sees three paths toward that system: electric vehicles, rail and water transportation, and “collectively managed travel” such as car sharing or light rail.</p>
<p>The last time the U.S. focused on car sharing was during World War II. Perl showed an old poster of a lone man in a car with this slogan: “When you ride alone, you ride with Hitler!”</p>
<p>He suggests creating a national agency charged with redeveloping the country's transportation infrastructure that would include the secretaries of defense, energy and transportation.</p>
<p>“You have to have a target for where you want to go,” he said. “The transportation revolution will arrive whether we're ready or not.”</p>
<p>San Antonio is preparing for more electric vehicles by adding several charging stations in town, said <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Julia+Jones%22">Julia Jones</a>, CPS Energy research manager in charge of the utility's electric vehicle unit.</p>
<p>CPS Energy, the city, the <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Texas+Sustainable+Energy+Research+Institute%22">Texas Sustainable Energy Research Institute</a> and other partners plan to install up to 100 fast-charge, 220-volt stations around town by year's end, Jones said.</p>
<p>Some of the money to add the stations comes from an <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Energy+Department%22">Energy Department</a> grant, while some businesses and private-property owners have agreed to take on part of the cost.</p>
<p>Adding the charging stations isn't cheap, Jones said, with stations costing an average $4,500 nationally. And in some San Antonio locations the price tag actually may be higher because the city's rocky terrain drives up the cost in some locations, she said.</p>
<p>Even so, San Antonio is “well on its way” in preparation for electric vehicles, said <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Mike+Tinskey%22">Mike Tinskey</a>, Ford's director of vehicle electrification and infrastructure, who also spoke at the AACOG conference Thursday.</p>
<p>At Ford, 2 percent of its vehicles are hybrids, but he expects that percentage to increase.</p>
<p>“At Ford, we believe the plug-in hybrid will be the most accepted,” Tinskey said. “We think that will be the volume leader.”</p>
<p>By 2015, sales of hybrids, such as the gas-electric Toyota Prius, are expected to rise to 900,000 vehicles and hold a 5.5 percent market share, the global market-research firm J.D. Power and Associates has estimated.</p>
<p>Gas and battery-powered hybrids such as the Chevrolet Volt and the luxury Fisker Karma, as well as the all-electric Nissan Leaf and the Tesla Roadster, will make up less than 1.6 percent of the market by 2015, J.D. Power said.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Staff Writer <a href="/?controllerName=search&action=search&channel=business&search=1&inlineLink=1&query=%22Tracy+Hamilton%22">Tracy Hamilton</a> contributed to this report.</p>
