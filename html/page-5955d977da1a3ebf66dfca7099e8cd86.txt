        <h1>2013 Nissan Quest LE Minivan</h1>        

                <iframe class='fb_like_btn' src="http://www.facebook.com/plugins/like.php?href=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.richmondautomall.com%2Freviews%2F2013-nissan-quest-le-minivan&amp;layout=standard&amp;show_faces=false&amp;width=450&amp;action=like&amp;font=arial&amp;colorscheme=light&amp;height=35" scrolling="no" frameborder="0" style="border:none; overflow:hidden; width:310px; height:35px;" allowTransparency="true"></iframe> 

        <p>Although people were saying that minivans were “on the wane” a few years ago and that SUVs and crossovers would corner all of the market for practical family vehicles, this hasn’t really been the case.</p>

<p>The reason this never happened is that minivans are simply far too useful for them ever to completely disappear. While SUVs and crossovers have their place in the general automotive picture in this country, minivans remain in the eyes of many the ultimate family vehicle. While there has been some attrition and several automakers have stopped making minivans, there are still lots of great choices out there and most of them are available at Richmond Auto Mall.</p>

<p>My most recent minivan drive was in a 2013 Nissan Quest, a vehicle that’s been through several generations now and remains a solid contender in its market segment. One thing the Quest brings home to me is “why are we still calling them minivans?” There’s nothing very “mini” about the Quest - it’s a big vehicle and has outstanding people and cargo-hauling capability as a result. Obviously, a very small family with, perhaps, one child would find a van like this is overkill, but with two or more kids, there simply isn’t a better choice. And there’s always the fact that kids tend to have lots of friends and they’ll often want to come along on trips too. For families into sports, it’s really the only way to go, unless they opt for a seriously big SUV.</p>

<p>The Quest is now in its fourth generation and the current one arrived in 2011, so there won’t be any major changes for quite a while yet. Some earlier Quest generations were quite controversial as far as their design went. <a href="http://www.richmondautomall.com/dealers/pan-pacific-nissan-richmond"> Nissan </a> targeted “design-conscious people” with one rather daringly-styled model and many critics thought they had gone too far. At the time, I praised Nissan for being bold enough to depart from the boxy lines which made most minivans look too much like one another. The current Quest is more conservatively styled, though is a very bold design and looks very solid on the road. As the years have passed, these vans have become increasingly packed with convenience items of every kind and the latest models are very sophisticated technically.</p>

<p>Early minivan styling efforts often involved raked rear bodywork for a streamlined look, but today, manufacturers like Nissan have opted for an almost vertical rear end which creates more space inside. The Quest has a ground-hugging look with what almost look like racecar style side skirts and this softens the bulk of the vehicle a little. Like all minivans now, the Quest boasts sliding doors on both sides and, of course, there’s a huge rear hatch. All three access points are powered, either from the key fob or from inside the vehicle. This is an enormous asset if you’re standing in the rain outside the supermarket with armfuls of bags. </p>

<p>You can also fold the rear seatbacks down electrically using buttons in the rear cargo area. This is handy when some extra space is needed quickly - for example, if that large Ikea-type carton you’re grappling with doesn’t quite fit. Going back to grocery bags, the Quest has a generous number of fold-down hooks for such bags that help prevent their contents spilling all over the floor when driving back from the shops.</p>

<p>Quest power comes from one of Nissan’s highly-praised V-6 engines - a 3.5-litre. 24-valve unit developing 260-horsepower. It doesn’t seem too long ago that minivans reached the 200-horsepower level and now they’re knocking on the door of 300. This gives the van plenty of pickup and great ability to climb long hills at altitude, where most engines lose power. The Quest uses a continuously-variable (CVT) automatic transmission and it matches the engine well. Some first-time CVT users find the absence of “shift steps” disconcerting, but frankly, I don’t know why the whole auto industry hasn’t gone over to transmissions like this.</p>

<p>Like some other minivans on the market right now, the Quest LE comes very lavishly equipped, even in basic form, with every imaginable luxury convenience and safety item. The only option my test van had was a “dual moonroof” package, which included powered sunroofs front and back. It also had a three-coat paint job (in brilliant pearl white) which costs an extra $300. An entry-level Quest carries a sticker price of just under $30,000, MSRP.</p>

<p>The Quest is a great van to drive with lots of power, excellent handling and, of course, vast amounts of passenger and cargo space. The LE version is just about the “ultimate minivan” and it’s durable enough to give a family many years of reliable service.</p>
