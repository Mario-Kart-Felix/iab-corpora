        <h1>2011 Nissan Quest Minivan</h1>        

                <iframe class='fb_like_btn' src="http://www.facebook.com/plugins/like.php?href=http%3A%2F%2Fwww.richmondautomall.com%2Freviews%2F2011-nissan-quest-minivan&amp;layout=standard&amp;show_faces=false&amp;width=450&amp;action=like&amp;font=arial&amp;colorscheme=light&amp;height=35" scrolling="no" frameborder="0" style="border:none; overflow:hidden; width:310px; height:35px;" allowTransparency="true"></iframe> 

        <p>Although many buyers have moved to SUVs and crossovers, minivans remain the ultimate family vehicles. Nothing has ever quite matched the combination of passenger capacity, roominess, cargo capability, economy and (in recent years) brisk performance. Given all this, it&rsquo;s surprising that some automakers have gotten out of the minivan field to concentrate on the aforementioned SUVs and CUVs. Others, however, have kept the faith and we&rsquo;ve had two all-new minivans this year, one from Toyota and the other from Honda. As 2011 begins, we&rsquo;ll be getting another contender in the form of the all-new Nissan Quest. Perhaps, after all, savvy buyers are not abandoning minivans altogether and maybe the interest in this kind of vehicle is undergoing a &ldquo;reawakening.&rdquo; </p>

<p>Whatever the reason, it&rsquo;s good to see all this activity involving vehicles like the Toyota Sienna, Honda Odyssey and Nissan Quest. The biggest dilemma buyers face is deciding which to choose - and there are some fine vans from other automakers too, of course, including the market leading Chrysler products. Interestingly, every automaker but one involved in the minivan market is represented at the Richmond Auto Mall.</p>

<p>The Quest was only just announced at the Los Angeles auto show, so this feature is more of a preview than a road test. A full test will follow when vehicles are released for media evaluation.</p>

<p>Images just released by Nissan give a good indication of what the new vehicle looks like. The earlier Quest was a very radical piece of styling and drew some criticism for this boldness - thought not from me. I liked this adventurous approach because the key feature of any van is practicality and designers don&rsquo;t have as much scope as they do with a sedan or coupe. The new Quest seems to take a slightly more conservative stance, but it will probably be more competitive with its rivals and it&rsquo;s a very nicely sculptured piece of styling. Looking at the Quest from the rear reveals that cargo space was a priority, rather than swoopy looks. The nose is very clean and has a &ldquo;Nissan family look&rdquo; about it and the interior seems like a great effort to combine high style with utility.</p>

<p>Nissan is claiming &ldquo;bold, segment-leading design&rdquo; and judging from the initial images, it looks like they got the job done pretty well. The overall aim was to provide consumers with &ldquo;all the size, safety, functionality and comfort that a minivan has to offer.&rdquo; Nissan believes that this was achieved without sacrificing style or driving pleasure.</p>

<p>Said Mark McDade, Director, Marketing, Nissan Canada: &ldquo;Nissan recognized it was time to provide the market with more than just a people-mover and with the new Quest, we&rsquo;ve done just that.&rdquo; The new Quest is aimed at both families and empty-nesters - confirming that many grandparents buy minivans to haul the kids around and also to go travelling in. Added McDade: &ldquo;The new Quest will be the answer for those who need the space of a traditional minivan, but don&rsquo;t want to feel that they&rsquo;re driving one.&rdquo; </p>

<p>In my experience, there&rsquo;s nothing better than a minivan for a long-distance road trip and I&rsquo;ve driven these vehicles many times from Vancouver to Southern California and back. You just can&rsquo;t beat having all that space to play with and the high seating positions are a great help when viewing the passing countryside.</p>

<p>The 2011 Quest uses a 3.5-litre V-6 developing a healthy 253-horsepower and it&rsquo;s mated to an advanced continuously variable transmission (CVT). There are four basic models to choose from - S, SV, SL and LE. The basic vehicle is very lavishly equipped and as you move up to the LE, the spec sheet just keeps growing. The Quest is built on the same platform as the Maxima and Altima sedans, so it promises to have a very car-like feel to it. It&rsquo;s also related to the Murano luxury crossover.</p>

<p>Among the various &ldquo;goodies&rdquo; you get, according to model choice, are one-touch power sliding doors, dual opening moonroofs, second row power windows, fold-flat second and third row seats, removable second row console, rear storage well, blind spot warning, tire pressure monitoring and a long list of other standard or available features. A full navigation system with backup camera is available, as would be expected these days.</p>

<p>The new Quest should arrive at the dealerships early next spring and first indications are that it&rsquo;ll be well worth waiting for.</p>
