</div><div id="va" style="float:left; z-index:-1"><!-- ContentCoreElement ID="751ababb-6710-4e5b-afad-9d2e331255dc", DateTime="5/12/2010 3:01:47 PM" --><table width="245" border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0" class="vaOuter" xmlns:fo="http://www.w3.org/1999/XSL/Format"><tr><td height="20" colspan="2"><img width="1" height="20" src="http://images.usatoday.com/_common/_images/clear.gif"></td></tr><tr><td width="12"><img width="12" height="1" src="http://images.usatoday.com/_common/_images/clear.gif"></td><td><table width="100%" border="0" cellpadding="0" cellspacing="0"><tr><td valign="top" colspan="2"><span class="va_main_header"><img width="10" height="10" src="http://images.usatoday.com/_common/_images/clear.gif" class="sectionBullet">&nbsp;HOW THE SHOOTOUT WORKED </span></td></tr><tr><td valign="top" colspan="2"><img width="1" height="15" src="http://images.usatoday.com/_common/_images/clear.gif"></td></tr><tr><td class="vaText" colspan="2"><P>The $25,000 Family Sedan Shootout was sponsored by USA TODAY, auto shopping and information site Cars.com and PBS' <I>MotorWeek</I> TV auto magazine to have a real family and media experts evaluate what an American family can get in a new sedan at a family-friendly price.</P>
<P>Shootout sedans were rated in 10 categories by the family and six journalists. The family rating was adjusted to count for 40% of each final score.</P>
<P>Half the categories had to do with the package: styling, interior trim, space, features. The other half were based on how the cars drove: acceleration, braking, shifting and handling.</P>
<P>Eight automakers participated in the Shootout, providing family sedans with sticker prices of $25,000 or less, before destination charges. Each car was equipped with what the company thought was its best configuration for that price. </P>
<P>All had four-cylinder engines and automatic transmissions. </P>
<P>The cars were driven on prescribed routes covering California freeways and suburban streets in and around Pasadena and Irwindale. </P>
<P>Common-sense shoppers would call all the entries midsize, though the Hyundai Sonata and the Honda Accord edge -- by tenths of a cubic foot -- into the range of interior volume the government classifies as full-size. </P>
<P>Not among the contenders is Volkswagen's Passat, which starts at about $2,000 above the Shootout's price ceiling. <div class="byLine" id="byLineTag">By James R. Healey, USA TODAY</div>
<div class="inside-copy">IRWINDALE, Calif. &#151; The average price of a new car exceeds $27,000 today &#8212; and it doesn't take long on dealers' lots to see $35,000 small SUVs and $30,000-plus mainstream sedans.</div>
<p class="inside-copy">Makes you wonder if a family can get a decent new vehicle &#8212; adequate room, sufficient power, nice accessories &#8212; for an affordable price.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">The answer is yes, as it turns out.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">
<div class="inside-copy"><b>SHOOTOUT RESULTS: </b><a href="#chart">How the the cars did in the tests</a></div>
<div class="inside-copy"><b>SPOTLIGHT: </b><a href="http://www.usatoday.com/money/autos/2010-05-03-sedan-shootout-tiernan_N.htm">Tiernan family checks out the cars</a></div>
<div class="inside-copy"><b>CARS.COM: </b><a href="http://www.cars.com/go/crp/buyingGuides/Story.jsp?section=Passenger&amp;story=bestFamilysedan&amp;subject=stories&amp;referer=&amp;year=New&amp;3=h">For longer reviews, more photos and other goodies </a></div>
</p>
<p class="inside-copy">That was the heartening finding of The $25,000 Family Sedan Shootout, a comparison test judged by a California family and a panel of auto journalists from USA TODAY, research site Cars.com and PBS TV show <i>MotorWeek.</i></p>
<div id="tagCrumbs"></div>
<p class="inside-copy">Top scorer in the eight-way faceoff: A 2011 <a href="http://content.usatoday.com/topics/topic/Organizations/Companies/Manufacturing,+Construction/Hyundai+Motor" title="More news, photos about Hyundai">Hyundai</a> Sonata, priced at just $22,745.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">But the score spread was fairly tight &#8212; 125 points out of a possible 1,000 separated first from last. So all the sub-$25,000 midsize sedans were sufficiently appealing to make a family's short list. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">David Champion, <i>Consumer Reports</i> magazine's auto testing chief, says $25,000 should, indeed, buy "a very good four-cylinder, or low-end V-6, family sedan." The Shootout reinforced that. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">Two contestants &#8212; Sonata and <a href="http://content.usatoday.com/topics/topic/Suzuki+Motor+Corporation" title="More news, photos about Suzuki">Suzuki</a> Kizashi &#8212; had navigation systems. Kizashi also had all-wheel drive. Ford Fusion had leather upholstery. Three cars had automatic climate control. Four had sunroofs. All had power seats. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">The array of features typical in this size and price class will, in fact, seem remarkable if you haven't browsed window stickers lately: </p>
<p class="inside-copy">&#8226; Power steering, brakes, windows, locks and mirrors. Traction and stability controls. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">&#8226; Anti-lock brakes with electronic brake-force distribution. It sends more braking force to the front wheels in harder stops, as the car nosedives and shifts more weight onto the front wheels. That was only on luxury cars just a few years ago.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">&#8226; Air conditioning &#8212; climate control in autospeak &#8212; is all but standard, of course. But the bar is being raised as dual-zone climate control shows up more often. That lets drivers and front passengers control their temperatures separately. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">And automatic climate control is working its way mainstream. That automatically heats or cools, as needed, to maintain a set temperature. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">&#8226; AM/FM/CD stereo is nearly standard, as are jacks for iPods and other portable players. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">&#8226; Nearly all in this class have trip computers displaying fuel economy and miles to empty. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">And it's rare that a midsize sedan can't boast a government rating of 30 or more miles per gallon in the federal highway test cycle. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">Competition has forced automakers to put more of those features into their cars, to make them stand out or at least stay even with rivals.</p>
<p class="inside-copy"><b>More features push up average prices </b></p>
<p class="inside-copy">The flood of features isn't limited to midsize cars, as automakers scrap for any sales advantage. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">That widespread feature-inflation, combined with buyers re-entering the market as the recession eases and credit loosens, worked to push up the average transaction price of a new car in the U.S. to $27,368 in the first quarter this year, from its recent low of $25,453 in 2008, according to <a href="http://content.usatoday.com/topics/topic/Organizations/Companies/J.+D.+Power+and+Associates" title="More news, photos about J.D. Power and Associates">J.D. Power and Associates</a>' Power Information Network.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">But the average in the intensely competitive midsize sedan segment &#8212; cars such as those in the Shootout &#8212; has been more stable: $21,500 now, PIN data show, vs. $21,217 in 2008. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">Midsize cars make up nearly half of U.S. new-car sales &#8212; 46.2% in 2009, according to Autodata &#8212; so they are key to an automaker's financial health. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">Five of the midsize sedans competing in the Shootout routinely are among the 10 best-selling vehicles in the U.S.: <a href="http://content.usatoday.com/topics/topic/Organizations/Companies/Manufacturing,+Construction/Honda" title="More news, photos about Honda">Honda</a> Accord, <a href="http://content.usatoday.com/topics/topic/Organizations/Companies/Manufacturing,+Construction/Toyota+Motor+Corporation" title="More news, photos about Toyota">Toyota</a> Camry, Ford Fusion, <a href="http://content.usatoday.com/topics/topic/Brands/Automotive/Chevrolet" title="More news, photos about Chevrolet">Chevrolet</a> Malibu, <a href="http://content.usatoday.com/topics/topic/Nissan+Motor+Co.,+Ltd" title="More news, photos about Nissan">Nissan</a> Altima. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">Others competing: Hyundai Sonata, Suzuki Kizashi and Mazda6. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">The Shootout proved it should be easy to find a price tag less than $25,000 on a sedan roomy enough to seat a family of four or five, satisfying to drive and with enough features that you won't feel deprived. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">The driving tests, on freeways and suburban streets in and around the commerical and industrial areas of Irwindale, Calif. and more genteel environs of Pasadena, proved something else: real-world fuel economy near 30 miles per gallon was common.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">The redesigned Sonata hit the market shortly before the Shootout. It was, not surprisingly, seen as the freshest-looking entry, considered the most attractive and feature-laden by some judges. Mainly, it seemed the best overall package: premium look and furnishings for a mainstream price. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">But being brand new wasn't required. The No. 2 spot went to Toyota Camry, now in its fourth model year since the last redesign. It stickered at $22,879, about even with the Sonata. </p>
<p class="inside-copy">Here's a look at the contenders, in the order they finished, and some comments from the judges' impressions. The scores are out of a possible 1,000.</p>
<p class="inside-copy">Also noted are some features that were a pleasant surprise for the price, regardless of whether they were standard or optional. </p>

<p class="inside-copy">Shootout sedans were rated in 10 categories by the family and six journalists. The family rating was adjusted to count for 40% of each final score.</p>
