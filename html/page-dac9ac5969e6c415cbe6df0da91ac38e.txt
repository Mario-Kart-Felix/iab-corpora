It is one of the most common <a href="/wiki/Car_body_style" title="Car body style" class="mw-redirect">car body styles</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_sedans">Types of sedans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of sedans">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of sedans" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Several versions of the body style exist, including four-door, two-door and <a href="/wiki/Fastback" title="Fastback">fastback</a> variants of both.</p>
<p>A sedan seats four or more people and has a fixed roof that is full-height up to the rear <a href="/wiki/Window" title="Window">window</a>. The roof structure will typically have a fixed B-pillar on sedan models. Most commonly it is a four-door; two-door models are rare, but they do occur (more so historically). In the U.S., the term sedan has been used to denote a car with fixed window frames, as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">hardtop</a> style without a "B" pillar and where the sash, if any, winds down with the glass. However, true hardtops have become increasingly rare.</p>
<p>Four-door sedans can have either two or three side windows: One in each door (vent windows not counted), and the possible third integrated in the "C"-pillar.</p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Notchback" title="Notchback">notchback</a> sedan is a <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">three-box</a> sedan, where the passenger volume is clearly distinct from the trunk volume of the vehicle (when seen from the side). The roof is on one plane, generally parallel to the ground, the rear window at a sharp angle to the roof, and the trunk lid is also parallel to the ground.</p>

<p>A <a href="/wiki/Fastback" title="Fastback">fastback</a> sedan is a <b>two-box</b> sedan, with continuous slope from the roof to the base of the <a href="/wiki/Decklid" title="Decklid" class="mw-redirect">decklid</a>, but excludes the <a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">hatchback</a> feature. <a href="/wiki/Marketing" title="Marketing">Marketing</a> terminology is often misleading in this area - for example, <a href="/wiki/Daimler_AG" title="Daimler AG">Daimler AG</a> calls the <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_CLS-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz CLS-Class">Mercedes-Benz CLS-Class</a> sedan a <i>four-door coupé</i> because its semi-fastback design tries to give the impression of a coupé.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Certain sedans are edging close<sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words" class="mw-redirect"><span title="Who says this? (May 2013)">weasel&#160;words</span></a></i>]</sup> to being <i>one-box</i> vehicles, where the windshield is steeply raked from the hood/bonnet and the rear window slopes toward almost the end of the car, leaving just a short rear deck that is part of the trunk lid — the 2006 4-door JDM <a href="/wiki/Honda_Civic_(eighth_generation)" title="Honda Civic (eighth generation)">Honda Civic</a> is an example of this.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> They are not fastbacks because their bodyline changes from the roof to the rear deck. Their steeply raked rear windows end with a <a href="/wiki/Decklid" title="Decklid" class="mw-redirect">decklid</a> that does not continue down to the bumper. Instead, their rear ends are tall — sometimes in a <a href="/wiki/Kammback" title="Kammback">Kammback</a> style — to increase trunk space.</p>
<p>Typically this design is chosen for its <a href="/wiki/Aerodynamic" title="Aerodynamic" class="mw-redirect">aerodynamic</a> advantages. Automakers can no longer afford the penalty in <a href="/wiki/Fuel_consumption" title="Fuel consumption" class="mw-redirect">fuel consumption</a> produced by the traditional <a href="/wiki/Notchback" title="Notchback">notchback</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">three box</a></i> form.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hardtop_sedans">Hardtop sedans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hardtop sedans">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hardtop sedans" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">Hardtop</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:1958_Ambassador_4-d_hardtop.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/1958_Ambassador_4-d_hardtop.jpg/220px-1958_Ambassador_4-d_hardtop.jpg" width="220" height="105" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/1958_Ambassador_4-d_hardtop.jpg/330px-1958_Ambassador_4-d_hardtop.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/1958_Ambassador_4-d_hardtop.jpg/440px-1958_Ambassador_4-d_hardtop.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:1958_Ambassador_4-d_hardtop.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf12/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
1958 <a href="/wiki/AMC_Ambassador" title="AMC Ambassador">AMC Ambassador</a> hardtop sedan</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In historic terminology, a sedan will have a frame around the door windows, while the <a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">hardtop</a> has frameless door glass. A true <a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">hardtop</a> sedan design also has no center or "B" pillar for roof support behind the front doors).<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> This pillarless body style offers greater visibility.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> However, it requires extra underbody strengthening for structural rigidity. The hardtop design can be considered separately (i.e., a vehicle can be simply called a four-door hardtop), or it can be called a hardtop sedan. During the 1960s and 1970s, hardtop sedans were often sold as <i>sport sedans</i> by several American manufacturers and they were among the top selling body styles. During the 1980s, automakers in the U.S. focused on removing weight and increasing strength, and their new four-door sedans with B-pillars were called <i>pillared hardtops</i> or <i>pillared sedans</i>. The <i>sport sedan</i> term has since been appropriated for other uses. In Japan the hardtop design was used for several luxury-type sedans during the 1990s.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Hatchback_sedans">Hatchback sedans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hatchback sedans">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hatchback sedans" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:2004-2005_Chevrolet_Malibu_MAXX_LS.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/2004-2005_Chevrolet_Malibu_MAXX_LS.jpg/220px-2004-2005_Chevrolet_Malibu_MAXX_LS.jpg" width="220" height="116" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/2004-2005_Chevrolet_Malibu_MAXX_LS.jpg/330px-2004-2005_Chevrolet_Malibu_MAXX_LS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/2004-2005_Chevrolet_Malibu_MAXX_LS.jpg/440px-2004-2005_Chevrolet_Malibu_MAXX_LS.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:2004-2005_Chevrolet_Malibu_MAXX_LS.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf12/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Chevy_Malibu" title="Chevy Malibu" class="mw-redirect">Chevy Malibu</a> Maxx hatchback sedan</div>
</div>

<p><a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">Hatchback</a> (a.k.a. liftback) sedans typically have the <a href="/wiki/Fastback" title="Fastback">fastback</a> profile, but instead of a trunk lid, the entire back of the vehicle lifts up (using a liftgate or hatch). A vehicle with four passenger doors and a liftgate at the rear can be called a four-door hatchback, four-door hatchback sedan, or five-door sedan. An example of such is the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Malibu_Maxx" title="Chevrolet Malibu Maxx" class="mw-redirect">Chevrolet Malibu Maxx</a> and <a href="/wiki/Audi_A5" title="Audi A5">Audi A5 Sportback</a>. There can also be two-door hatchback sedans (three-door sedans), by the same technical explanation for two-door sedans. Examples of this design are the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Golf" title="Volkswagen Golf">Volkswagen Golf</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chauffeured_sedans">Chauffeured sedans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chauffeured sedans">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chauffeured sedans" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">Limousine</a></div>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Luxury_Car_2.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Luxury_Car_2.jpg/220px-Luxury_Car_2.jpg" width="220" height="132" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Luxury_Car_2.jpg/330px-Luxury_Car_2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Luxury_Car_2.jpg/440px-Luxury_Car_2.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Luxury_Car_2.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf12/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
The <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Town_Car" title="Lincoln Town Car">Lincoln Town Car</a> is often used as a chauffeured car in the U.S.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Strictly speaking <a href="/wiki/Limousine" title="Limousine">limousine</a> sedans have a separate compartment for the driver and the passenger compartment is long enough to contain at least two comfortable, forward-facing <a href="/wiki/Bench_seat" title="Bench seat">bench seats</a>. Vehicles used for these means are usually <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Town_Car" title="Lincoln Town Car">Lincoln Town Car</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cadillac" title="Cadillac">Cadillac</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Motor_Cars" title="Rolls-Royce Motor Cars">Rolls-Royce</a> or <a href="/wiki/Maybach" title="Maybach">Maybach</a> for the more wealthy. The term limousine can refer to a large sedan, especially if hired from a service. Chauffeured limousines are primarily used by individuals for weddings, businesses for meetings, as well as for airport and sightseeing transportation. <a href="/wiki/Chauffeur" title="Chauffeur">Chauffeurs</a> are professional drivers, usually with experience in the <a href="/wiki/Transportation_industry" title="Transportation industry" class="mw-redirect">transportation industry</a> or <a href="/wiki/Tourism_industry" title="Tourism industry" class="mw-redirect">tourism industry</a>. Chauffeured sedans are owned either by private owners, livery services, or corporations. Large corporations as well as governments commonly provide luxury sedans to top executives, as well as VIP guests. Chauffeured sedans, such as the Lincoln Town Car, may also be stretched into limousines that are capable of seating up to twenty people.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">Origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Origin">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Origin" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Litter_(vehicle)" title="Litter (vehicle)">Litter (vehicle)</a></div>
<p>The word <i>sedan</i> is possibly derived from a southern <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> dialect derivative of Italian <i>sedia</i> "chair" (the first sedan was said to have been introduced from <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">Naples</a>). However, <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_people" title="Portuguese people">Portuguese</a> and <a href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">Spanish</a> navigators and colonists encountered <a href="/wiki/Litter_(vehicle)" title="Litter (vehicle)">litters</a> of various sorts in <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a>, <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">Japan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">Mexico</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">Peru</a>. They were imported into <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spain</a> in the late sixteenth century. Soon the fashion spread into <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> and then <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. All the names for these derived from the root "sed-" from the <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> "sella" - the traditional name for a carried chair.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The first automobile to use the configuration was the 1899 <a href="/wiki/Renault_Voiturette" title="Renault Voiturette">Renault Voiturette Type B</a>. The first closed car, for at least 4 persons, which used the word <i>sedan</i> was the 1911 Speedwell sedan, which was manufactured by the Speedwell Motor Co in Dayton, Ohio.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> But even before that time completely closed cars were called saloons or limousines, like the 1905 Rational 4-door limousine<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> or the 1907 Renault 4-door limousine<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> or the 1910 Stella 2-door saloon.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The words saloon or limousine do not exclusively mean a fully closed car.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Cars which are called sedans are almost always fully closed. The term "convertible sedan" was used in the 1930s to describe a car with a soft, foldable top and roll-up windows, very much like a convertible coupe.</p>
<p>The derivation from the town of <a href="/wiki/Sedan,_Ardennes" title="Sedan, Ardennes">Sedan, Ardennes</a> in France, where it was said to have been made or first used, lacks historical evidence, according to <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">Oxford English Dictionary</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International_terminology">International terminology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International terminology">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Sedan_(automobile)&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International terminology" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American English</a> and American Spanish, the term <b>sedan</b> is used (accented as "sedán" in Spanish). The engine compartment, at the front, is covered by the <a href="/wiki/Hood_(vehicle)" title="Hood (vehicle)"><i>hood</i></a>; the cargo compartment at the rear is called the <a href="/wiki/Trunk_(automobile)" title="Trunk (automobile)"><i>trunk</i></a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">British English</a>, a car of this configuration is called a <b>saloon</b>. The engine compartment cover is the <i>bonnet</i>, the cargo-compartment <i>boot</i> is at the rear. Hatchback sedans are known simply as <i>hatchbacks</i> (not hatchback saloons); long-wheelbase luxury saloons may be referred to as <i>limousines</i>.</p>
<p>The British English term <i>saloon</i> is sometimes used by British car manufacturers in the United States. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_car" title="Rolls-Royce car" class="mw-redirect">Rolls-Royce</a> Park Ward was sold as a saloon in the United States, while the smaller <a href="/wiki/Rolls-Royce_Silver_Seraph" title="Rolls-Royce Silver Seraph">Silver Seraph</a> was called a sedan.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">Australia</a> the American term sedan is used, albeit with the British terms <i>boot</i> and <i>bonnet</i> being retained. In <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">New Zealand</a> the British terms "boot", "bonnet" and "windscreen" are most commonly used, but the American terms are understood by most of the population. However, the American terms "sedan" and "station wagon" are predominantly used, with "saloon" only finding occasional usage, particularly in the field of motor racing. In other languages, sedans are known as <b>berline</b> (<a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>), <b>berlina</b> (<a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">European Spanish</a>, <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">European Portuguese</a>, <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">Romanian</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>); although these terms also may include hatchbacks. These terms, besides sedan, derive from types of <a href="/wiki/Horse" title="Horse">horse</a>-drawn <a href="/wiki/Carriage" title="Carriage">carriages</a>. In <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>, the term <b>Limousine</b> is used for sedans, and "Stretch-Limousine" for limousines.</p>
