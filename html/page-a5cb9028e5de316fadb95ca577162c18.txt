<h2 class="art-PostHeader">
<a href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/extend-life-vehicle-part-ii-save-interior" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Extend the Life of Your Vehicle Part II: Save the Interior">
Extend the Life of Your Vehicle Part II: Save the Interior</a>
</h2>
<div class="art-PostHeaderIcons art-metadata-icons">
<img src="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/themes/PerformanceAuto/images/PostDateIcon.png" width="17" height="18" alt="" />
September 27th, 2012
</div>

</div>
<div class="art-PostContent">

          <div id="fb_share_1" style="float: right; margin-left: 10px;;width: 55px;" name="fb_share"><div id="fb-root"></div><script src="http://connect.facebook.net/en_US/all.js#appId=125029517579627&amp;xfbml=1"></script><fb:like href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/extend-life-vehicle-part-ii-save-interior" send="false" layout="box_count" width="55" show_faces="false" font="arial"></fb:like></div><p>Last week we shared a few guidelines in our blog <a href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/extend-vehicles-life-quick-tips"  target="_blank">“How to Extend Your Vehicle’s Life: Quick Tips.”</a> This week we want to enlighten you on how to maintain/increase the value and life of your vehicle’s interior. We understand that with work, children, school and life’s other every day stresses, you don’t always remember to grab that soda can left in the cup holder after lunch, or maybe you accidently spilled coffee on your upholstery when you were running late to work, and on the way home, made a wide turn and spilled your left-overs from lunch.</p>
<p>The interior of your vehicle is like your living room; you probably spend more time there than you realize and the cleaner it is, the more you are able to enjoy the atmosphere. That being said, when you decide to trade those four wheels for something bigger and better or, something smaller and more economical, the trade value will be higher if the interior is in excellent condition. Performance Auto Group suggests these helpful maintenance tips to help keep your vehicle’s interior clean and looking its best:</p>
<p><span style="text-decoration: underline;"><a href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/car-shade3.jpg" ><img class="alignleft  wp-image-1408" title="car shade" src="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/car-shade3-300x164.jpg" alt="" width="231" height="147" /></a><strong>Park in the shade.</strong></span> UV rays are not only harmful to your skin, but also can be harmful to your interior. Harsh heat can damage expensive computer systems and fade your dash, so if a garage is not available, do your best to find a shaded parking spot. Also, your vehicle’s interior stays cooler when parked in the shade. It is not a pleasant feeling to open your car door and be slapped in the face with what feels like boiling beads of moisture and proceed to enter. Invest in a portable shade to place in your windshield for times when shady parking spots are limited.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">Clean inside.</span></strong> Dirt and spilled foods/beverages accumulate and the harsh chemicals used to clean up messes can lead to corrosion on vinyl surfaces.<a href="http://www.performanceauto.com/index.htm" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.performanceauto.com']);" target="_blank"> Performance Auto Group</a> recommends vacuuming and wiping down the interior with a mild solution of detergent and water each time the exterior is washed.<a href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/rubber-floor-mats1.jpg" ><img class="wp-image-1404 alignright" title="rubber floor mats" src="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/rubber-floor-mats1-300x225.jpg" alt="" width="235" height="149" /></a></p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong><span style="text-decoration: underline;">Use Floor mats for winter.</span></strong> Rubber floor mats collect the slush, salt and grime that your feet drag in during winter that would otherwise, stain and damage your vehicle’s carpet. It is easier to power wash rubber mats with a hose than put in the unnecessary elbow-grease to remove salt and mud stains left behind.  It’s also easier on your wallet to switch out floor mats than pay for new carpet or a complete detail. Call your local Performance Auto Group or Baxter Auto Group parts department to see what type of floor mats are available for your vehicle.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><span style="text-decoration: underline;"><a href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/weather-stripping2.jpg" ><img class="alignleft  wp-image-1412" title="OLYMPUS DIGITAL CAMERA" src="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/09/weather-stripping2-300x224.jpg" alt="" width="236" height="180" /></a><strong>Fix weather-stripping immediately.</strong></span> Old or torn weather stripping can lead to rain and melted snow leaking into your vehicle’s interior and freeze the doors shut.  Check the weather-stripping for any leaks and determine if it needs to be repaired or replaced. Your service advisor can offer his/her qualified opinion if you are unsure. Smaller leaks can be repaired by seam-sealers, while rips and tears need a special caulking treatment that is available at your local Performance dealership’s parts department. <p>Ever wonder what the letters and numbers on the side of a tire represent? Although the alpha-numeric sequences may seem as like an alien coding system, it can be fairly easy to decipher. The alpha-numeric labels note the tire size, when it was manufactured, speed-rating, load and tire pressure markings, construction type, special weather ratings and the Original Equipment (OE) information.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>We previously mentioned the importance of the OE information in our last blog, <a href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/shop-tires"  target="_blank">“How to Shop for New Tires.”</a> When ordering tires from a local dealership or tire shop you will need to provide the OE information to ensure you are getting the correct tires for your vehicle.  The Performance Auto Group has <a href="http://www.performanceauto.com/locations/list.htm" onclick="javascript:_gaq.push(['_trackEvent','outbound-article','http://www.performanceauto.com']);" target="_blank">16 dealership locations </a>that are conveniently located throughout Nebraska and the Kansas City area.</p>
<p>For more detailed information on side tire codes, continue reading Part II of this blog.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h4><strong>What Do Tire Numbers and Letters Mean: The Details<a href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/Rosettta-of-Rubber-copy3.jpg" ><img class="wp-image-1278 alignright" title="Rosettta of Rubber copy" src="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/08/Rosettta-of-Rubber-copy3-300x300.jpg" alt="" width="237" height="230" /></a></strong></h4>
<p>This section should help explain the main components of side tire coding for both car enthusiasts and those with basic tire knowledge. Collecting information prior to purchasing a new set of tires is a necessary first step.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>The consumer needs to know what they are looking for in order to determine the type of tires that would best match their vehicle’s frame and performance. However, it is always recommended to double check with your local dealership before making any purchases or alterations to any vehicle.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<h4><strong>How to Decode a Tire’s Sidewall</strong></h4>
<p>To determine the size, find a tire code that looks similar to this: 225/40R18. The three-digit number preceding the virgule (more commonly referred to as a slash), represents the tire’s width in millimeters. The rest of the code is an aspect ratio of the sidewall height divided by the width; the letter describes the construction of the tire, while the last two numbers represent the wheel’s diameter in inches.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>As previously mentioned, the OE and tire compound/construction vary from each manufacturer but can determine for which vehicle that tire was specifically produced. For example, “M0” corresponds to a Mercedes-Benz model. Coordinating the recommended OE with the correct vehicle allow for better vehicle performance and uneven wear and tear. This tire code varies for each brand and model, so call a local dealership to compare the OE code with the vehicle’s make and model to confirm which tires are recommended for that vehicle.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>There is also a code that defines the service description and speed-rating. This code will look similar to “93W.”  It defines the tire’s capabilities. The two-to-three digit number stands for the tire’s load rating. A higher number means the more weight the product can withstand. Followed by a corresponding letter, this letter determines the speed-rating (the highest suggested speed) and is marked from low to high as the following:</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">L (75 mph), S (112 mph, suggested for most vehicles) and Z (149 mph). More recently, manufacturers have produced a W (168 mph) and Y speed-rating tire. The “Y” rating indicates the tire can bear up to speeds of 186 mph.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>Finally, special weather ratings may be presented as a pictogram or several variations of the letters “M” and “S” which stand for mud and snow and is correlated with the tire tread. A pictogram that presents a snowflake within a mountain indicates that the tire meets U.S. Rubber Manufacturing Association standards for “packed-snow-traction” requirements.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>

Don&#8217;t Flub Your Flat Tire Fiasco</a>
</h2>
<div class="art-PostHeaderIcons art-metadata-icons">
<img src="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/themes/PerformanceAuto/images/PostDateIcon.png" width="17" height="18" alt="" />

<div class="art-PostContent">

          <div id="fb_share_1" style="float: right; margin-left: 10px;;width: 55px;" name="fb_share"><div id="fb-root"></div><script src="http://connect.facebook.net/en_US/all.js#appId=125029517579627&amp;xfbml=1"></script><fb:like href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/flub-flat-tire-fiasco" send="false" layout="box_count" width="55" show_faces="false" font="arial"></fb:like></div><p>Is it pouring outside? Did you just start a road trip? Running late for an office meeting? Well, you’ll probably get a flat tire – at least that’s what it seems like. Though flat tires always seem to occur at the most inconvenient times, a frustrating situation can go from bad to worse if you lack a solution to your deflated donut predicament.</p>
<p>For your safety (and sanity) it’s important to know the general procedure of changing a tire. Vehicle makes and models will obviously differ in tire-changing tools, but there are fundamental guidelines that everyone can follow.</p>
<p><em>My tip: Avoid this dreaded situation all together by checking the pressure of your tires every now and then. Things like nails, glass and shards of metal are sometimes harder to prepare for, however…</em></p>
<p><a href="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/08/816170_tire_puncture.jpg" ><img class="size-full wp-image-1025 alignright" title="Flat Truck Tire" src="http://www.performanceautoblog.com/wp-content/uploads/2011/08/816170_tire_puncture.jpg" alt="" width="300" height="224" /></a></p>
<p><strong>First things first</strong> – pull over to a safe location. “What was that? Did I hit a squirrel? Did my tire fall off?” No. When your steering starts to pull to one side and the ride gets bumpy, you’ve got a flat. Pull over to a safe (and ironically, flat) location, void of heavy traffic. You will need room to move around the car.</p>
<p>Prolonging the distance driven with a flat could cause further damage to your wheel, the hub and even the axle.</p>
<p><strong>Grab your gear.</strong> After getting out, assessing the damage and probably spouting profanities under your breath, get to business. When you purchase your car, you should become familiar with the location of your spare and the tools that come along with it. You can usually find them around the trunk area. For most cars it’s under the trunk floor, but for SUVs, trucks and minivans, the tire&#8217;s usually mounted on/in the tailgate or underneath the car. Be sure to check your owner&#8217;s manual to be sure. The tire tool trifecta includes the spare, a jack and a lug wrench. You will need all of them. No more, no less.</p>
<p><strong>Loosen the Lug Nuts.</strong>  Loosening these small-but-mighty metal guys can drive you…nuts.  It’s true.  Some mechanics tighten them too much, leaving you stressing and sweating when you are faced with a flat. The lug wrench should suffice, but a little extra elbow grease might be necessary. The key word in this step is “loosen” – don’t remove completely. That would make the next step much more difficult.</p>
<p>A word to the wise: lefty loosey, righty tighty…that means counterclockwise.</p>
<p><strong>Jack it up.</strong> Use the provided jack and place it underneath the car next to the deflated tire. Find the “jack spot” (usually marked with two indentations) and use that as a location for the best leverage. The jack that comes with your car should get the job done. You only need to raise it high enough to allow the wheel to spin freely.</p>
<p><strong>Remove and replace the dud.</strong> Once the bad tire is raised, completely remove the lugs by hand. Be sure to set them somewhere flat and in eyes’-sight so they don’t roll away. Remove the bad tire and set it back in the place of the spare. Affix the spare tire to the wheel studs, and screw on the lugs once again, beginning by hand and finishing the job with the lug wrench. A key note – you don’t want the lugs super tight. A “snug” lug is a safe lug.</p>
<p><strong>Lower the wheel and clean u</strong>p. Lower the vehicle back down completely with the jack and replace the tools where you found them. Make sure you didn’t overlook any small parts. When you get back on the road, be cautious – usually there is a maximum speed for spares. It’s usually written on the inside of the tire wall. Take a look at that before your drag race to the closest service shop.</p>
<p>You did it. It’s like it never even happened, right? Oh wait, there’s grease on your work shirt…</p>
