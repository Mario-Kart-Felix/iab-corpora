<div><h3> <a href="http://www.timelessrides.com/blog/preservation/winterizing-your-classic-car">Winterizing your Classic Car</a> </h3></div>
<div style="float: left; margin: 5px 5px 5px 5px; border: 2px solid;">
<a href="/blog/preservation/winterizing-your-classic-car" class="imagecache imagecache-blog_image_leader imagecache-linked imagecache-blog_image_leader_linked"><img src="http://www.timelessrides.com/sites/default/files/imagecache/blog_image_leader/winterizing-your-classic-car.jpg" alt="" title="" width="225" height="131" class="imagecache imagecache-blog_image_leader"/></a></div>
<div style="float: left; margin: 5px 5px 5px 5px;">
<fb:like href="http://www.timelessrides.com/blog/preservation/winterizing-your-classic-car" send="false" layout="box_count" width="46" show_faces="false" colorscheme="dark"></fb:like>
</div>
<div class="nicetext"><p>Cars and trucks are meant to be driven regularly, so when collector vehicles are stored away for the winter months, they are more susceptible to problems.  Trapped moisture can lead to mold and mildew in the interior and rust out metal.  The corrosive elements of the fluids sitting in your fuel, brake, engine, transmission, and cooling systems for months can also wreak havoc.  Because your collector car is likely old, systems may be even more fragile than a conventional daily driven vehicle. This detailed article will guide you through the steps necessary to properly store your classic car through its winter hibernation.</p>
