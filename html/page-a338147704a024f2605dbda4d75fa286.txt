<p>A <b>station wagon</b>, also called an <b>estate car</b> and an <b>estate</b>, is an automotive <a href="/wiki/Car_body_style" title="Car body style" class="mw-redirect">body-style</a> variant of a <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">sedan/saloon</a> with its roof extended rearward<sup id="cite_ref-Hilliers_1-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilliers-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> over a shared passenger/cargo volume with access at the back via a third or fifth door (the <a href="/wiki/Liftgate" title="Liftgate" class="mw-redirect">liftgate</a> or <a href="#Tailgate_evolution">tailgate</a>), instead of a trunk lid. The body style transforms a standard <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">three-box</a> design into a <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling#One-box_and_Two-Box_design" title="Three-box styling">two-box</a> design — to include an <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">A, B, and C-pillar</a>, as well as a D-pillar. Station wagons can flexibly reconfigure their interior volume via fold-down rear seats to prioritize either passenger or cargo volume.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/American_Heritage_Dictionary" title="American Heritage Dictionary" class="mw-redirect">American Heritage Dictionary</a> defines a station wagon as "an automobile with one or more rows of folding or removable seats behind the driver and no luggage compartment but an area behind the seats into which suitcases, parcels, etc., can be loaded through a tailgate."<sup id="cite_ref-ahd_2-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ahd-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When a <a href="/wiki/Model_range" title="Model range" class="mw-redirect">model range</a> includes multiple body styles, such as sedan, hatchback and station wagon, the models typically <a href="/wiki/Automotive_platform_sharing" title="Automotive platform sharing" class="mw-redirect">share their platform</a>, <a href="/wiki/Powertrain" title="Powertrain">drivetrain</a> and bodywork forward of the A-pillar. In 1969, <i><a href="/wiki/Popular_Mechanics" title="Popular Mechanics">Popular Mechanics</a></i> said, "Station wagon-style&#160;... follows that of the production sedan of which it is the counterpart. Most are on the same wheelbase, offer the same transmission and engine options, and the same comfort and convenience options."<sup id="cite_ref-pm_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pm-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Station wagons have evolved from their early use as specialized vehicles to carry people and luggage to and from a train <i>station</i>, and have been marketed worldwide.</p>

<p><i>Station wagon</i> and <i>wagon</i> are the common names in <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">American</a> and <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">Canadian</a> English, while <i>estate car</i> and <i>estate</i> are common in the rest of the English world. Both names harken to the car's role as a shuttle, with storage space for baggage, between country <i>estates</i> and train <i>stations</i>.</p>
<p>Having shared antecedents with the British <i><a href="/wiki/Shooting-brake" title="Shooting-brake">shooting-brake</a></i> (originally a wooden-bodied vehicle used to carry shooting parties with their equipment and game), station wagons have been marketed as <b>breaks</b>, using the <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> term (which is sometimes given fully as <b>break de chasse</b>, literally "hunting break." Early U.S. models often had exposed wooden bodies and were therefore called <i><a href="/wiki/Woodie" title="Woodie">woodies</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Manufacturers may designate station wagons across various model lines with a proprietary nameplate. Examples include "Estate" (<a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a>), "Avant" (<a href="/wiki/Audi" title="Audi">Audi</a>), "Touring" (<a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a>), "Break" (<a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn" title="Citroën">Citroën</a>), Kombi or Variant (<a href="/wiki/Volkswagen" title="Volkswagen">Volkswagen</a> and <a href="/wiki/Saab_Automobile" title="Saab Automobile">Saab</a>) and "Sports Tourer" or Caravan (<a href="/wiki/Opel" title="Opel">Opel</a>).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_from_hatchbacks">Distinction from hatchbacks</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distinction from hatchbacks">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distinction from hatchbacks" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Both station wagons and <a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">hatchbacks</a> typically share a <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">two-box design</a> configuration, with one shared, flexible, interior volume for passengers and cargo<sup id="cite_ref-Vehicle_Dynamics_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vehicle_Dynamics-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-AutomotiveTech_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AutomotiveTech-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> — and a rear door for cargo access.<sup id="cite_ref-cdn2_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-cdn2-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Further distinctions are highly variable:</p>
<p><b>Pillars:</b> Both configurations typically feature <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">A, B &amp; C-pillars</a>, station wagons feature a <a href="/wiki/Pillar_(car)" title="Pillar (car)" class="mw-redirect">D-pillar</a> and hatchbacks may feature a D pillar.</p>
<p><b>Cargo Volume:</b> Station wagons prioritize passenger and cargo volume — with windows aside the cargo volume. Of the two body styles, a station wagon roof (viewed in profile) more likely extends to the very rearmost of the vehicle, enclosing a full-height cargo volume<sup id="cite_ref-AutomotiveTech_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-AutomotiveTech-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> — a hatchback roof (especially a <a href="/wiki/Liftback" title="Liftback" class="mw-redirect">liftback</a> roof) might more likely rake down steeply behind the C-Pillar, prioritizing style<sup id="cite_ref-Hilliers_1-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilliers-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> over interior volume, with shorter rear overhang and with smaller windows (or no windows) aside the cargo volume.</p>
<p><b>Cargo floor contour:</b> Favoring cargo capacity, a station wagon may prioritize a fold-flat floor, where a hatchback would more likely allow a cargo floor with pronounced contour (e.g. the <a href="/wiki/Mini_(BMW)" title="Mini (BMW)" class="mw-redirect">new Mini</a> or the sixth generation <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fiesta#Fiesta_Mark_VI_.282008.E2.80.93.29:The_sixth_generation" title="Ford Fiesta">Ford Fiesta</a>).</p>
<p><b>Seating:</b> Station wagons may have two or three rows of seats (e.g., the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Taurus" title="Ford Taurus">Ford Taurus</a> wagons) while hatchbacks may only have one or two.<sup id="cite_ref-Vehicle_Dynamics_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vehicle_Dynamics-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><b>Rear suspension:</b> A station wagon may include reconfigured rear suspension for additional load capacity<sup id="cite_ref-Hilliers_1-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Hilliers-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and to minimize intrusion in the cargo volume, (e.g., worldwide versions of the first generation <a href="/wiki/Ford_Focus_(international)" title="Ford Focus (international)" class="mw-redirect">Ford Focus</a>).</p>
<p><b>Rear Door:</b> Hatchbacks typically feature a top-hinged <a href="/wiki/Liftgate" title="Liftgate" class="mw-redirect">liftgate</a> for cargo access, with variations ranging from a two-part liftgate/tailgates (e.g., the 1958 <a href="/wiki/Austin_A40_Farina" title="Austin A40 Farina">A40 Countryman</a>) to a complex tailgate that can function either as a full tailgate or as a trunk lid (e.g., the 2008 <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0koda_Superb#B6_Superb_.28Typ_3T.29_.282008-present.29" title="Škoda Superb">Škoda Superb</a>'s TwinDoor). Station wagons have also enjoyed numerous <a href="#Tailgate_evolution">tailgate configurations</a>. Hatchbacks may be called Liftbacks when the opening area is very sloped and the door is lifted up to open.<sup id="cite_ref-Vehicle_Dynamics_5-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Vehicle_Dynamics-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Automotive journalist <a href="/wiki/Dan_Neil" title="Dan Neil">Dan Neil</a>, in a 2002 <i><a href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times" class="mw-redirect">New York Times</a></i> report described verticality of the rear cargo door as the prime distinction between a hatchback and a station wagon: "Where you break the roofline, at what angle, defines the spirit of the vehicle", he said. "You could have a 90-degree break in the back and have a station wagon."<sup id="cite_ref-nyt1_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A model range may include multiple configurations, as with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Focus_(North_America)#Body_styles_and_trims" title="Ford Focus (North America)" class="mw-redirect">2005–2007 Ford Focus</a> which offered sedan (ZX4), wagon (ZXW) and three and five-door hatchback (ZX3 and ZX5) models.</p>

<p>The first station wagons were a product of the age of <a href="/wiki/Train" title="Train">train</a> travel. They were originally called "depot hacks" because they worked around train <i>depots</i> as <i>hacks</i> (short for <a href="/wiki/Hackney_carriage" title="Hackney carriage">hackney carriage</a>, an old name for <a href="/wiki/Taxicab" title="Taxicab">taxis</a>). They also came to be known as "carryalls" and "suburbans".</p>
<p>Before the 1930s, manufacturers assembled the framing of passenger compartments of passenger vehicles in hardwood. In automobiles, the framing was sheathed in steel and coated with colored lacquer for protection. Eventually, all-steel bodies were adopted because of their strength, cost, and durability.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Early station wagons evolved from trucks and were viewed as <a href="/wiki/Commercial_vehicle" title="Commercial vehicle">commercial vehicles</a> (along with <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">vans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">pickup trucks</a>), not consumer automobiles—with the framing of the early station wagons left unsheathed because of the commercial nature of the vehicles. Early station wagons were fixed roof vehicles, but lacked the glass that would normally enclose the passenger compartment, and had only bench seats.<sup id="cite_ref-georgano_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-georgano-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> In lieu of glass, side curtains of canvas could be unrolled. More rigid curtains could be snapped in place to protect passengers from the elements outside.</p>
<p>In 1922 <a href="/wiki/Essex_(automobile)" title="Essex (automobile)">Essex</a> introduced the first affordable enclosed automobile (<a href="/wiki/Sedan_(automobile)" title="Sedan (automobile)">sedan</a>), which shifted the auto industry away from open vehicles to meet consumer demand for enclosed automobiles.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Initially, manufacture of the wagon's passenger compartments was outsourced to custom body builders because the production of the all-wood bodies was very time consuming. Major producers of wood-bodied station wagons included Mitchell Bentley, Hercules, USB&amp;F, Cantrell, and other custom builders. The roofs of "woodie" wagons were usually made of stretched canvas that was treated with a water proofing dressing.</p>
<p>Eventually, the car companies themselves began building their own station wagons. <a href="/wiki/Star_(automobile)" title="Star (automobile)">Star</a> (a division of <a href="/wiki/Durant_Motors" title="Durant Motors">Durant Motors</a>) was the first car company to offer a factory-built station wagon, in 1923. In 1919, the Stoughton Wagon Company of Stoughton, Wisconsin, had begun putting custom wagon bodies on Model T chassis.<sup id="cite_ref-georgano_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-georgano-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1929 <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> was by far the biggest seller of station wagons. Since Ford owned its own hardwood forest and mills, it began supplying the wood components for the Model A wagon (although initially some final assembly still took place away from the factory, by <a href="/wiki/Briggs_Automotive_Company" title="Briggs Automotive Company">Briggs</a>, in Detroit, with wood from the Mengel Company in <a href="/wiki/Louisville,_Kentucky" title="Louisville, Kentucky">Louisville, Kentucky</a>).<sup id="cite_ref-georgano_11-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-georgano-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The same year, J. T. Cantrell provided woodie bodies for <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> vehicles until 1931.<sup id="cite_ref-georgano_11-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-georgano-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>By the mid-1930s, wood-bodied station wagons had some prestige. They were priced higher than regular cars and were popular in affluent communities. By 1941, the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Town_and_Country_(pre-1990)" title="Chrysler Town and Country (pre-1990)" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler Town and Country</a> was the most expensive car in the company's lineup.</p>
<p>Woodie wagons required constant maintenance: bodies were finished in varnishes that required recoating; bolts and screws required periodic tightening as wood expanded and contracted through the seasons. In 1935, <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> introduced a steel-bodied eight-seat <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Suburban" title="Chevrolet Suburban">Suburban</a> wagon, based on the Chevrolet truck.<sup id="cite_ref-georgano_11-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-georgano-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Woodies popularity was renewed, in the surfing culture, during the 1950s and 1960s.</p>

<p>After World War II, automobile production resumed, with prewar tooling. New advances in production techniques made all-steel station wagon bodies more practical, eliminating the cost, noise, and maintenance associated with wood bodies.<sup id="cite_ref-lastwood_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-lastwood-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The first factory-built all-steel station wagon in North America was the 1946 Jeep Station Wagon, based on the Jeep produced by <a href="/wiki/Willys-Overland" title="Willys-Overland" class="mw-redirect">Willys-Overland</a> during World War II.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Willys offered a trim level, evoking earlier wood bodywork, rendered instead in paint and trim work. In 1935, Chevrolet introduced the Chevy Suburban, with an all-steel station-wagon body on a commercial-truck chassis. In 1947, <a href="/wiki/Crosley" title="Crosley">Crosley</a> introduced an all-steel, car-based wagon in Europe.</p>
<p>In 1949, <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_(automobile)" title="Plymouth (automobile)">Plymouth</a> introduced the first all-steel station wagon in the U.S., the two-door Suburban, based on an <a href="/wiki/Automobile_platform" title="Automobile platform">automobile platform</a>. In 1950 Plymouth discontinued the <a href="/wiki/Woodie" title="Woodie">woodie</a> station wagon, converting to all steel bodywork. <a href="/wiki/Buick" title="Buick">Buick</a> discontinued production of the last North American wood bodywork after the 1953 <a href="/wiki/Model_year" title="Model year">model year</a>. Morris of England continued to market its <a href="/wiki/Morris_Minor" title="Morris Minor">Morris Minor Traveller</a> until the late 1960s.</p>
<p>By the late 1950s, wood body construction was replaced with wood accents on all-steel bodies. Ford introduced the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Country_Squire" title="Ford Country Squire">Country Squire</a> with <a href="/wiki/Woodie#Simulated_woodgrain" title="Woodie">simulated woodgrain</a> paneling.<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1955, only <a href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford" class="mw-redirect">Ford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(automobile)" title="Mercury (automobile)">Mercury</a> offered a woodie model,<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> accomplishing the simulation of wood with other materials, e.g., steel, plastics and DiNoc (a <a href="/wiki/Vinyl" title="Vinyl">vinyl</a> product).</p>
<p>Ford was undoubtedly the most known for the use of woodgrain appliques on its vehicles. In addition to the well-known <a href="/wiki/Ford_Country_Squire" title="Ford Country Squire">Country Squire</a>, the "Squire" trim level was an available option in a few different <a href="/wiki/Model_range" title="Model range" class="mw-redirect">model ranges</a>. These include the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Falcon_(Americas)" title="Ford Falcon (Americas)" class="mw-redirect">Falcon Squire</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fairlane_(Americas)" title="Ford Fairlane (Americas)">Fairlane Squire</a>, and in the 1970s the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Pinto" title="Ford Pinto">Pinto Squire</a> Surviving examples of these smaller "Squire" wagons are much rarer than the more popular full-size Country Squire, which was ordered and produced in much higher quantities. The Squire was always the highest trim level of any Ford Wagon and included the signature woodgrain applique, and usually additional exterior chrome, nicer interior trim, special emblems, etc. Other manufacturers marketed wagons and minivans with simulated wood off and on from the mid-1950s through the early 1990s. The most notable of these are the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Town_and_Country" title="Chrysler Town and Country" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler Town and Country</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Buick_Estate_Wagon" title="Buick Estate Wagon" class="mw-redirect">Buick Estate Wagon</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile_Custom_Cruiser" title="Oldsmobile Custom Cruiser">Oldsmobile Custom Cruiser</a>, The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Kingswood_Estate" title="Chevrolet Kingswood Estate">Chevrolet Kingswood Estate</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Colony_Park" title="Mercury Colony Park">Mercury Colony Park</a>. Simulated wood appliqués became less common in the 1980s. With the introduction of the retro-styled <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_PT_Cruiser" title="Chrysler PT Cruiser">Chrysler PT Cruiser</a> wagon-like model, aftermarket firms began selling simulated woodgrain kits.</p>
<p>Station wagons experienced highest production levels in the United States from the 1950s through the 1970s. The late 1950s through the mid-1960s was also the period of greatest variation in body styles, with pillared two- and four-door models marketed alongside <a href="/wiki/Hardtop" title="Hardtop">hardtop</a> (no B-pillar) four-door models (e.g., <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">American Motors</a>' Rambler Cross-Country wagons).<sup id="cite_ref-mom.27s_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mom.27s-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Rambler offered a four-door of this body style in 1956, followed by Mercury, Oldsmobile, and Buick in 1957; Chrysler entered the market in 1960. The pillarless designs could be expensive to produce, added wind noise, and created structural issues with body torque.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> GM eliminated the hardtop wagon from its lineup in 1959, and AMC and Ford exited the field beginning with their 1960 and 1961 vehicles, leaving Chrysler and Dodge with the body style through the 1964 model year.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Full-size_wagons">Full-size wagons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Full-size wagons">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Full-size wagons" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>Traditionally, full-sized American station wagons were configured for six or nine passengers. The basic arrangement for seating six was three passengers in the front and three passengers in the rear, all on bench-type seats; to accommodate nine, a third bench seat was installed in the rear cargo area, over the rear axle. Through 1956, all wagons had the third row facing forward, but Chrysler's 1957 models had a roof too low to permit a forward-facing seat installed over the axle, so it was turned around and placed behind the axle. GM wagons would adopt the rear-facing third row with 1959 models until 1971, and again in 1977.</p>
<p>In full-size Ford and Mercury wagons built after 1964, the configuration was two seats facing each other, placed behind the rear axle. According to Ford, each seat would accommodate two people, raising the total <a href="/wiki/Seating_capacity" title="Seating capacity">seating capacity</a> to ten passengers; however, these seats were quite narrow in later models and could accommodate only one passenger, limiting the total capacity to eight passengers.</p>
<p>The 1964–1972 <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile_Vista_Cruiser" title="Oldsmobile Vista Cruiser">Oldsmobile Vista Cruiser</a> and 1964–1969 <a href="/wiki/Buick_Sport_Wagon" title="Buick Sport Wagon">Buick Sport Wagon</a> featured raised rooflines beginning above the second-row seat and continuing all the way to the rear tailgate. Above the second seat were <a href="/wiki/Acrylic_glass" title="Acrylic glass" class="mw-redirect">acrylic glass</a> skylights in which passengers could view the outside from overhead. On the three-seat models of these wagons, the third seat faced forward as did the first and second seats. Later, the 1971-1976 full-size "clamshell" wagons used a similar raised roof to provide adequate headroom for a forward-facing third row, but without the skylights.</p>
<p>Recent models of station wagons are usually built on smaller platforms and accommodate five or six passengers (depending on whether bucket or bench seats are fitted in front) in two rows of seating. The <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_E-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz E-Class">Mercedes-Benz E-Class</a> Estate, a mid-sized station wagon, has an optional rear-facing jump seat for two passengers in the cargo area.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.motortrend.com/roadtests/overview/1301_2013_mercedes_benz_gl450_vs_2012_e350_4matic_wagon/">[1]</a></p>
<p>Full-size station wagons have been replaced by medium or full-size SUVs (both light truck and crossover types) with three-row seating such as the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Suburban" title="Chevrolet Suburban">Chevrolet Suburban</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ford_Expedition" title="Ford Expedition">Ford Expedition</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_GL-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz GL-Class">Mercedes-Benz GL-Class</a>.<a rel="nofollow" class="external autonumber" href="http://www.motortrend.com/roadtests/overview/1301_2013_mercedes_benz_gl450_vs_2012_e350_4matic_wagon/">[2]</a></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Two-door_wagons">Two-door wagons</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Two-door wagons">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Two-door wagons" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>In 1951, the <a href="/wiki/Compact_car" title="Compact car">compact</a> 100-inch (2,540&#160;mm) wheelbase <a href="/wiki/Nash_Rambler" title="Nash Rambler">Nash Rambler</a> line included a two-door station wagon design whose production continued through 1955. After the merger of <a href="/wiki/Nash-Kelvinator" title="Nash-Kelvinator">Nash</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Motor_Car_Company" title="Hudson Motor Car Company">Hudson</a>, the new company, <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">American Motors</a> (AMC) reintroduced the two-door wagon in the "new" <a href="/wiki/Rambler_American" title="Rambler American">Rambler American</a> line in 1959 with only a few modifications from the original version. This was a car targeting buyers looking for economy and load space, as well as a strategy of reintroducing an old design. This two distinct model run was a successful business decision that is almost unheard of in automobile history.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The 1955–1957 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Nomad" title="Chevrolet Nomad">Chevrolet Nomad</a> and sibling <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Safari" title="Pontiac Safari">Pontiac Safari</a> were two-door station wagons. <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(automobile)" title="Mercury (automobile)">Mercury</a> produced a two-door hardtop (no "B-pillar") station wagon from 1957 to 1960, named the Commuter. Chevrolet produced the 1964–1965 <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Chevelle" title="Chevrolet Chevelle">Chevelle</a> 300 series two-door station wagon.</p>
<p>In 1961 Volkswagen introduced the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Type_3" title="Volkswagen Type 3">Type 3</a> (also known in various markets as the Variant and the Volkswagen 1500 (later the Volkswagen 1600)), available as a two-door sedan and as a two-door station wagon, which was commonly called the Squareback. VW's then-typical rear-engine layout was retained for the Type 3, but the engine profile was flattened, resulting in a small car offering interior room, as well as trunk space in the front. The model was offered through the 1973 model year.</p>
<p>The 1970s were a high point for two-door wagons in the U.S. as <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">GM</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford" class="mw-redirect">Ford</a>, and AMC fielded examples in their <a href="/wiki/Subcompact_car" title="Subcompact car">subcompact car</a> lines.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Vega" title="Chevrolet Vega">Chevrolet Vega</a> Kammback, introduced in September 1970, was the first U.S.-made four-passenger wagon and the first two-door wagon from GM in six years. It shares its wheelbase and length with Vega coupe versions and was produced in the 1971–1977 model years. The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Vega#Pontiac_Astre" title="Chevrolet Vega">Pontiac Astre</a> Safari wagon is a Pontiac <a href="/wiki/Badge_engineering" title="Badge engineering">rebadged</a> Vega that was introduced in the U.S for the 1975 <a href="/wiki/Model_year" title="Model year">model year</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Vega#1978-1979" title="Chevrolet Vega">Chevrolet Monza</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Sunbird#1976.E2.80.931980" title="Pontiac Sunbird">Pontiac Sunbird</a> Safari wagons replaced the Vega and Astre respectively. Retaining the Vega wagon body, they were produced for the 1978 and 1979 models years with Pontiac and Buick engines.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ford_Pinto" title="Ford Pinto">Ford Pinto</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Bobcat" title="Mercury Bobcat" class="mw-redirect">Mercury Bobcat</a> 2-door wagons were produced between 1972 and 1980. American Motors also entered the market with a wagon version of the <a href="/wiki/AMC_Pacer" title="AMC Pacer">AMC Pacer</a>, produced between 1977 and 1980.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> The last two-door wagon available in America, the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Fox" title="Volkswagen Fox">Volkswagen Fox</a>, was discontinued in 1990.</p>
<p>In the United Kingdom, estate car versions of small and middle sized models were more common. The estate ("Traveller") versions of the <a href="/wiki/Morris_1000" title="Morris 1000" class="mw-redirect">Morris 1000</a> ("Minor") and <a href="/wiki/Mini" title="Mini">Mini</a>, with external ash wood frames (structural on the 1000); had two vertically divided van-type rear doors in the style of older shooting-brakes (see "<a href="/wiki/Station_wagon#Station_wagons_around_the_world" title="Station wagon">station wagons around the world</a>", below). The <a href="/wiki/Hillman_Husky" title="Hillman Husky">Hillman Husky</a> estate version of the <a href="/wiki/Hillman_Imp" title="Hillman Imp">Hillman Imp</a> was unusual in being a rear-engined estate. Other two-door station wagons in Europe included the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort_(Europe)" title="Ford Escort (Europe)">Ford Escort</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morris_1100" title="Morris 1100" class="mw-redirect">Morris 1100</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Viva" title="Vauxhall Viva">Vauxhall Viva</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Chevette" title="Vauxhall Chevette">Vauxhall Chevette</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiat_127" title="Fiat 127">Fiat 127</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Saab_95" title="Saab 95">Saab 95</a>.</p>
<p>A former East German car fleet made uniformly of <a href="/wiki/Trabant" title="Trabant">Trabants</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wartburg_(car)" title="Wartburg (car)">Wartburgs</a> had a distinct luxurious version of 2-doors <a href="/w/index.php?title=Estate_coupe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Estate coupe (page does not exist)">estate coupe</a> Trabant 601 Estate with an extra C-column far back to extend the rear compartment of the car. This version had a full scale liftgate allowing to access the entire rear room of nearly 1000 l with rear seats folded.</p>

<p>Since the 1970s, sales of station wagons in the United States and Canada dropped for several reasons. The <a href="/wiki/1973_oil_crisis" title="1973 oil crisis">1973 oil crisis</a> was a turning point against the "traditional classic American station wagon—with its acres of fake woodgrain siding, sticky vinyl bench seats and lazy-revving V-8 engine", which have been described as "wallowing land arks".<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1984 the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler Corporation</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_minivans" title="Chrysler minivans" class="mw-redirect">first minivans</a> derived from the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_K_platform" title="Chrysler K platform">K platform</a>. While the K platform was also used for the <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Reliant" title="Plymouth Reliant">Plymouth Reliant</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Aries" title="Dodge Aries" class="mw-redirect">Dodge Aries</a> station wagon models, the minivan would soon eclipse them in popularity. Since minivans and SUVs are classified as light trucks under US <a href="/wiki/CAFE_standards" title="CAFE standards" class="mw-redirect">CAFE standards</a>, manufacturers had a strong incentive to market those vehicles over station wagons, which are classified as cars. Station wagons have remained popular in Europe<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> and other locations whose emissions and efficiency regulations do not distinguish between cars and light trucks.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The emergence and popularity of <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">sport utility vehicles</a> which closely approximate the traditional wagon bodystyle was a further blow. After struggling sales, the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Caprice" title="Chevrolet Caprice">Chevrolet Caprice</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Buick_Roadmaster" title="Buick Roadmaster">Buick Roadmaster</a>, the last American full size wagons, were discontinued in 1996. The <a href="/wiki/Ford_Taurus" title="Ford Taurus">Ford Taurus</a> wagon was discontinued after the 2005 model year. The <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Magnum" title="Dodge Magnum">Dodge Magnum</a> was marketed during the 2005–2008 model years.</p>
<p>Since then, smaller wagons have been sold in the U.S. as less expensive alternatives to SUVs and minivans. Domestic wagons also remained in the Ford, Mercury, and Saturn lines until 2004 when the bodies began a phase-out, replaced by car-based <a href="/wiki/Crossover_SUV" title="Crossover SUV" class="mw-redirect">crossover SUVs</a> and minivans designed to look like station wagons.</p>
<p>The last <a href="/wiki/Subcompact" title="Subcompact" class="mw-redirect">subcompact</a> station wagon produced in the United States and Canada was the 1992 <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Corolla" title="Toyota Corolla">Toyota Corolla</a>. <a href="/wiki/Compact_car" title="Compact car">Compact</a> station wagons have been declining since the 2000s. Ford dropped the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Focus_(North_America)" title="Ford Focus (North America)" class="mw-redirect">Ford Focus wagon</a> for 2008, and <a href="/wiki/Subaru" title="Subaru">Subaru</a> replaced the <a href="/wiki/Subaru_Impreza" title="Subaru Impreza">Impreza wagon</a> with a 5-door hatchback model. <a href="/wiki/Volvo" title="Volvo">Volvo</a> announced that they will withdraw their compact station wagon, the <a href="/wiki/Volvo_V50" title="Volvo V50" class="mw-redirect">V50</a>, from the U.S market by 2012 due to poor sales figures. In Europe the V50 remains popular.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>European luxury carmakers such as Audi, <a href="/wiki/BMW" title="BMW">BMW</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz" title="Mercedes-Benz">Mercedes-Benz</a> still offered wagons in their North American lineup, using the labels "Avant", "Touring", and "Estate" instead of wagon. However, these wagons had fewer trim and powertrain levels than their sedan counterparts,<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_22-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> for instance the wagon styles of high-performance trims such as the <a href="/wiki/BMW_M5" title="BMW M5">BMW M5</a>, <a href="/wiki/Audi_RS6" title="Audi RS6" class="mw-redirect">Audi RS6</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-AMG" title="Mercedes-AMG">Mercedes-Benz E63 AMG</a> were never imported to North America. The <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_W204#W204_.282007.E2.80.93present.29" title="Mercedes-Benz W204" class="mw-redirect">Mercedes-Benz W204 C-Class</a> wagon was not offered in the United States and Canada unlike the previous generation. The <a href="/wiki/BMW_5_Series" title="BMW 5 Series">BMW 5 Series</a> Touring was dropped from BMW's North American lineup after the conclusion of the E60 generation, due to slow sales in the United States with only 400 wagons sold in 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Due to the popularity of SUVs in North America, these European manufacturers have been supplanting their wagons with car-based crossovers such as the <a href="/wiki/BMW_Sports_Activity_Series" title="BMW Sports Activity Series" class="mw-redirect">BMW Sports Activity Series</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Audi_Q5" title="Audi Q5">Audi Q5</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_M-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz M-Class">Mercedes-Benz M-Class</a>, these offer a wider range of options and engines than wagons.</p>
<p>However, the <a href="/wiki/Cadillac_CTS" title="Cadillac CTS">Cadillac CTS</a> gave rise to its wagon counterpart, the 2010 CTS Sportwagon. Unlike European luxury wagons sold in North America, the CTS Sportwagon has almost as many trim levels as its sedan counterpart.<sup id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_22-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although station wagons have declined in North America, they offer several advantages over car-based crossovers. Wagons offer cargo space without compromising driving dynamics or radically increasing weight. The high fuel prices in Europe and Japan have led to nearly half of all production vehicles being wagons, and a similar result is expected for the United States. This trend is already being seen with an increase in wagons being sold in the United States. Cadillac's 2009 CTS Sport Wagon, Acura's 2011 TSX Sport Wagon, and Lexus’ CT200h Hybrid wagon are examples of this increased proliferation.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>European manufacturers often built two-door station wagons in the post-war period for the compact class, and not four-door models, a practice that continued at Ford (amongst others) with its <a href="/wiki/Ford_Escort_(Europe)" title="Ford Escort (Europe)">Escort</a> Mark III, for example, well into the 1980s. Usually, by that time, manufacturers created four-door models. In Europe, Australia and New Zealand, these vehicles remain popular and in volume production, although minivans (known in Europe as MPVs—multi-purpose vehicles) and the like have had some impact. As in North America, early station wagons were aftermarket conversions and had their new bodywork built with a wooden frame, sometimes with wooden panels, sometimes steel. Station wagons were the originators of fold down seats to accommodate passengers or cargo.</p>
<p>Station wagons are generally called <i>estate cars</i> or simply <i>estates</i> in the United Kingdom. The term <a href="/wiki/Shooting-brake" title="Shooting-brake">shooting-brake</a>, a term for an original hunting vehicle, came to be known synonymously with <i>station wagon</i> and were also custom built as modified luxury coupés with an estate car-like back. They generally retain two side doors. Until the early 1960s many of them were built with structural wooden rear frames, making them some of the most expensive and luxurious "woodies" ever built. A smaller Estate car was the very popular <a href="/wiki/Morris_Minor" title="Morris Minor">Morris Minor</a> Traveller Estate which copied the wooden side panel frames of larger designs. Most small cars produced in the UK from the 1950s until the 1980s had Estate versions, some of which were also used as small delivery vans minus the rear windows.</p>

<p>The British companies Rover and Austin produced 4×4 vehicles (the <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover" title="Land Rover">Land Rover</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Austin_Gipsy" title="Austin Gipsy">Gipsy</a>, respectively) in the 1950s. Apart from the standard canvas-topped utility vehicles, these 4×4s were available in estate car body styles that were sold as "Station Wagons". They incorporated more comfortable seating and trim than standard editions (which were typically aimed at agricultural and military buyers) and together with options such as heaters made the Station Wagon vehicles more attractive to private buyers. The name was alien in the UK, but was probably chosen because of the high number of these vehicles that went to export markets such as Africa and Australia, where the name was understood. Early advertising for the Land Rover version took the name literally, showing the vehicle collecting people and goods from a <a href="/wiki/Railway_station" title="Railway station" class="mw-redirect">railway station</a>. Land Rover still calls the passenger-carrying variations of its <a href="/wiki/Land_Rover_Defender" title="Land Rover Defender">Defender</a> model "Station Wagons".</p>
<p>In France almost all station wagon models are called the <b><a href="/wiki/Brake_(carriage)" title="Brake (carriage)">Break</a></b> (note the different spelling from the English shooting brake). French breaks from <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a> and <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn" title="Citroën">Citroën</a> in particular were available in seven- or eight-seater "family" versions long before MPVs became known in Europe.</p>
<p>The German <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Polo" title="Volkswagen Polo">Volkswagen Polo</a> crossed type divisions by offering a two-door station wagon shape (not <i>named</i> as a wagon) as the main model in its range in some markets in the 1980s—despite the existence of two-door sedan and hatchback ("<a href="/wiki/Coup%C3%A9" title="Coupé">coupé</a>") variants.</p>
<p>Japanese manufacturers did not value station wagons highly until very recently. For many years, models sold as well-appointed station wagons in export markets were sold as utilitarian "van" models in the home market for many years. This explains why station wagons were not updated for consecutive generations in a model's life in Japan: for instance, while a sedan might have a model life of four years, the wagon was expected to serve eight — the 1979 Toyota Corolla (built until 1987) and the 1987 <a href="/wiki/Mazda_Capella" title="Mazda Capella">Mazda Capella</a> (built until 1996) are examples. The <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Avenir" title="Nissan Avenir">Nissan Avenir</a> is an example of a model that began its life as a utility vehicle and became a well-equipped passenger car in the 1990s. <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a> no longer offers a wagon version of the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Camry" title="Toyota Camry">Camry</a>, but still offers wagon versions of Corolla or Auris.</p>
<p>In Australia and New Zealand the most popular station wagons are the large <a href="/wiki/Ford_Falcon_(Australia)" title="Ford Falcon (Australia)">Ford Falcon</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holden_Commodore" title="Holden Commodore">Holden Commodore</a> models.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> These are usually built on a longer <a href="/wiki/Wheelbase" title="Wheelbase">wheelbase</a> than their sedan counterparts, though they share the same door skins, leading to a slightly unusual appearance with the rear door not reaching all the way to the rear wheel arch. <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Motors" title="Mitsubishi Motors">Mitsubishi</a>'s Australian subsidiary designed wagon versions of its <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Magna" title="Mitsubishi Magna">Magna</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi_Magna" title="Mitsubishi Magna">Verada</a> for the local market, although it no longer offers a large wagon. Smaller wagons have declined in popularity, in comparison with Europe, although they have traditionally been more popular in New Zealand than in Australia. For example, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Telstar" title="Ford Telstar">Ford Telstar</a> was offered as a wagon in New Zealand, but not Australia, even though the mechanically identical <a href="/wiki/Mazda_626" title="Mazda 626" class="mw-redirect">Mazda 626</a> was sold in both countries.<br style="clear:both;" /></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tailgate_evolution">Tailgate evolution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tailgate evolution">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Tailgate evolution" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Many modern station wagons have an upward-swinging, full-width, full-height rear door supported on <a href="/wiki/Gas_spring" title="Gas spring">gas springs</a>—often where the rear window can swing up independently. Historically, wagons have employed numerous designs:</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Split gate:</b> The earliest common style was an upward-swinging window combined with a downward swinging tailgate. Both were manually operated. This configuration generally prevailed from the earliest origins of the wagon bodystyle in the 1920s through the 1940s. It remained in use through 1960 on several models offered by Ford. This style was later adopted on aftermarket camper shells for <a href="/wiki/Pickup_truck" title="Pickup truck">pickup trucks</a>, seeing that pickup trucks already had a bottom half tailgate as an OEM feature.</li>
<li><b>Retractable window:</b> In the early 1950s, tailgates with hand-cranked roll-down rear windows began to appear. This was another innovation first seen on <a href="/wiki/Rambler_(automobile)" title="Rambler (automobile)">Rambler</a> wagons.<sup id="cite_ref-mom.27s_18-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-mom.27s-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Later in the decade, electric power was applied to the tailgate window—it could be operated from the driver's seat, as well as by the keyhole in the rear door. By the early 1960s, this arrangement was common on both full-size and compact wagons.</li>
<li><b>Side hinge:</b> A side hinged tailgate that opened like a door was offered on three-seat wagons by <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">American Motors</a> to make it easier for the back row passengers to enter and exit their rear-facing seats. This was later supplanted by the dual-hinged tailgate.</li>
<li><b>Retractable roof:</b> The <a href="/wiki/Studebaker" title="Studebaker">Studebaker</a> <a href="/wiki/Studebaker_Wagonaire" title="Studebaker Wagonaire">Wagonaire</a> station wagon had a unique retractable rear roof section as well as a conventional rear tailgate which folded down. This allowed it to carry tall objects that would not fit otherwise. Water leaks, <a href="/wiki/Body_flex" title="Body flex">body flex</a> and noise prevented the innovation from being adopted by other manufacturers. The concept was reintroduced in 2003 on <a href="/wiki/GMC_(automobile)" title="GMC (automobile)">GMC</a>'s mid-size Envoy XUV <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">SUV</a>.</li>
<li><b>Dual and tri-operating gates:</b> <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a>'s full-size wagons for 1966 introduced a system marketed as "Magic Doorgate"—a conventional tailgate with retracting rear glass, where the tailgate could either fold down or pivot open on a side hinge—with the rear window retracted in either case. Competitors marketed their versions as a <i>Drop and Swing</i> or <i>Dual Action Tailgate.</i><sup id="cite_ref-pm_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-pm-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> For 1969, Ford incorporated a design that allowed the rear glass to remain up or down when the door pivoted open on its side hinge—marketing the system, which had been engineered by <a href="/wiki/Donald_N._Frey" title="Donald N. Frey">Donald N. Frey</a><sup id="cite_ref-time1_28-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-time1-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> ) as the "Three-Way Magic Doorgate". Similar configurations became the standard on full-size and intermediate wagons from GM, Ford, and Chrysler. GM added a notch in the rear bumper that acted as a step plate; to fill the gap, a small portion of bumper was attached to the doorgate. When opened as a swinging door, this part of the bumper moved away, allowing the depression in the bumper to provide a "step" to ease entry; when the gate was opened by being lowered or raised to a closed position, the chrome section remained in place making the bumper "whole".</li>
<li><b>Clamshell:</b> Full-size GM 1971–1976 wagons (Buick, <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet" title="Chevrolet">Chevrolet</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile" title="Oldsmobile">Oldsmobile</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pontiac" title="Pontiac">Pontiac</a>) featured a 'clamshell' design where the rear power-operated glass slid up into the roof as the tailgate (manually or with power assist), dropped below the load floor. The power tailgate ultimately supplanted the manual tailgate, which required marked effort to lift from storage. Subsequently, GM reverted to the doorgate style for its full-size wagons.</li>
<li><b>Liftgate:</b> A simplified, one-piece liftgate on smaller wagons. The last generation of GM's full-size wagons returned to the upward-lifting rear window as had been used in the 1940s.</li>
<li><b>Swing-up window:</b> An upward-lifting, full-height, full-width rear door, where the window on the rear door can be opened independently from the rear door itself. The window is also opened upwards and is held on gas struts. The <a href="/wiki/Renault_Laguna" title="Renault Laguna">Renault Laguna</a> II estate featured this arrangement.</li>
<li><b>Fold-up license plate:</b> Wagons (including the <a href="/wiki/Volvo_Amazon" title="Volvo Amazon">Volvo Amazon</a> wagon and early models of the Range-Rover) had an upward folding hinged plate attached to the lower tailgate of the split rear door. When the tailgate was folded down, the plate hung down and remained readable.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_equipment">Safety equipment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Safety equipment">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Station_wagon&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Safety equipment" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Some station wagons are fitted with additional front-facing or rear-facing seats, along with safety belts, to enable passengers to be carried safely in the cargo area.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Cargo_barriers" title="Cargo barriers" class="mw-redirect">Cargo barriers</a> may be used to prevent unsecured cargo from causing injuries in the event of sudden deceleration, collision, or a <a href="/wiki/Rollover" title="Rollover">rollover</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
