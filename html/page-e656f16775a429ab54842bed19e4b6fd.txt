							<h2 class="slideTitle">Here Come the EVs</h2>
							<div class="imageContent">
								<p>They're finally here! After years of <a href="http://www.thedailygreen.com/environmental-news/latest/new-electric-cars-460210">anticipation</a>, plans, promises and the occasional <a href="http://www.thedailygreen.com/living-green/blogs/cars-transportation/wheego-whip-life-460310">test drive</a>, electric vehicles (EVs) arrived with a thump this week: Nissan delivered the first Leaf to a customer, Chevy put 350 Volts on trucks for delivery to dealers in several states, Mercedes handed over its first leased F-Cell hydrogen car (to the head of the Producers Guild of America) and Think opened its new Indiana factory with 15 City plug-ins for the state's Department of Natural Resources.</p>
<p>The new EVs are expensive, starting around $32,780 (the Leaf's price) and rising to $87,400 (the Fisker Karma plug-in hybrid). But the purchase price can be offset with a $7,500 federal income tax credit, and state rebates and tax credits. The most lucrative of those is a $5,000 rebate for California residents, and Tennessee (where the Leaf will be built) <a href="http://www.thegovmonitor.com/world_news/united_states/first-1000-electric-vehicle-buyers-in-tennessee-get-2500-rebate-38310.html" target="_blank">offers $2,500 for the first 1,000 cars</a>. A list of subsidies by state <a href="http://www.thecarelectric.com/content/electric-car-benefits-by-state.php" target="_blank">is here</a>, but it may be out of date in some instances -- you'll need to check locally. </p>
<p>Also available is a federal tax credit for installing a home charger. This will be 50% of the cost up to $2,000, but only up to December 31, 2010. Congress just extended the credit for a year, but now it's at "pre-stimulus" levels of 30% up to $1,000. </p>
