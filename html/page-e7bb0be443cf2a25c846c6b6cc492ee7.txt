		<div class="date">
			January 28, 2013 &middot; Posted in <a href="http://www.vintageautomotive.net/?cat=8" title="View all posts in News" rel="category">News</a> &middot; <span>Comments Off</span>&nbsp;

		<h3 style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #000000;"><em><strong>Benefits of using an attorney as an escrow agent to protect yourself in a collector car transaction</strong> </em></span></h3>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #000000;">By: Bryan W. Shook, Esquire </span></p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #000000;">Email: <a href="mailto:bshook@dplglaw.com"><span style="color: #000000;">bshook@dplglaw.com</span></a></span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">Using an attorney to act as an escrow agent is one of the most effective ways to safeguard yourself from the pitfalls of buying vehicles sight unseen from far away locations.  In a perfect world collector car transactions would all be “above board” and transparent however as we all know this, unfortunately is not always the case.  That screaming deal on eBay or Craigslist may be a great deal, but it may also be the biggest mistake you make this year.  There are many hazards upon which a prospective buyer may happen, although most happen with the consummation of the sale, especially if the vehicle is not viewed, in person, by the buyer. </span></p>
<p><em><span style="color: #000000;">How can using an attorney as an escrow agent help protect you in a collector car transaction?</span></em></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">An attorney as an escrow agent has more inherent safeties as opposed to using a broker.   Attorneys are regulated by their state’s bar association or their state’s supreme court.  Brokers are usually not regulated at all and only subject to their state’s vehicle board, if regulated at all.  Money placed into an attorney’s escrow account is subject to strict accounting oversight and may only be paid out with the client’s consent and agreement. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">As an attorney familiar with the nuances and pitfalls of collector car transactions I can offer many safeguarding services, professionally, discreetly and unobtrusively to preserve the transaction and yet protect you the client throughout the sale.</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">I offer the following services: </span></p>
<ul>

<li><span style="color: #000000;">Escrow Agent</span></li>

<li><span style="color: #000000;">Independent evaluation of title documents prior to consummation of sale</span></li>
<li><span style="color: #000000;">Vehicle research, including verification of provenance and pedigree</span></li>
<li><span style="color: #000000;">Forensic investigatory services (number &amp; stamp investigation, make and model investigation, production anomalies, etc.) </span></li>
<li><span style="color: #000000;">Powers of Attorney</span></li>
<li><span style="color: #000000;">Import/Export</span></li>
<li><span style="color: #000000;">Competing Claims of Ownership</span></li>
<li><span style="color: #000000;">Title Defense</span></li>
<li><span style="color: #000000;">Help safeguard against misrepresentations and fraud</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">By utilizing my services, you are not only protecting yourself in the transaction, but you are also making a further investment in the value of your purchase. </span></p>

		<h1><a href="http://www.vintageautomotive.net/?p=242" rel="bookmark">Rebodied Cars &#8230; what to do &#8230;</a></h1>
		
		<div class="date">
			June 29, 2012 &middot; Posted in <a href="http://www.vintageautomotive.net/?cat=8" title="View all posts in News" rel="category">News</a> &middot; <span>Comments Off</span>&nbsp; 
		</div>
		
		<p align="center"><strong>Rebodies: </strong><br />
<strong>What&#8217;s the big deal???</strong></p>
<p align="center">(By: Bryan W. Shook, Esquire)</p>
<p align="center"><a href="mailto:BShook@dplglaw.com">BShook@dplglaw.com</a></p>
<p align="center">717-975-9446</p>
<p>I’ve been getting quite a few calls lately seeking information on re-bodied vehicles.  The term &#8220;rebody&#8221; is a term of art used throughout our hobby to denote a vehicle whose original factory body has been replaced with another &#8220;donor&#8221; body.  The donor body is then given the original body’s VIN, serial number, data card, trim tag, cowl tag, etc. and then usually and most unfortunately sold to an unsuspecting buyer as the original, real deal automobile.  This problem is complicated when the rebodied car is an &#8220;air car&#8221; which did not exist prior to the rebody.  Specifically what happens is someone dreams up a car or has the paperwork from a desirable car and makes it from &#8220;thin air&#8221; using the donor body as the starting point.  All of a sudden, the car has pedigree, provenance and history if the builder can dream up a good enough story.  This is problematic as you can plainly see.</p>
<p>There are several legal issues when it comes to a rebodied automobile.  The most important issue is whether or not the rebody was disclosed to you when you purchased the vehicle.  If the rebody was not disclosed to you how can it be said you negotiated with the seller on equal footing.  Another issue comes from the fact that rarely are rebodys done properly.  Were the police notified of the body replacement as required under some state laws?  Did the seller give you two Certificates of Titles?  (Remember the best bodies come from good cars and in today’s day and age, good cars get restored … was the body stolen and the subject vehicle the product of a &#8220;chop shop&#8221;)  Did the seller give you photographs of the original body to evidence the condition of the original body?  Do you have confirmation that the original body has been destroyed? (This is usually where the State Police come in as this is where the stories start about two cars registered under the same VIN)</p>
<p>Without the safeguards outlined above, you can never been shore that the vehicle you purchased truly belongs to you.  Under the law you would have a breach of the warranty of title claim if any third party were to ever come after you claiming you own the body to their car.  The problem is  that if you know the car has been rebodied and you can’t provide the above information to a new purchaser you could be just as liable as the seller who sold the car to you should you not disclose what you know to a prospective purchaser.</p>
<p>If you have a rebodied car or think you do, this is a serious matter.  Rebodied cars can be nearly unsaleable and always have questions.  There was way to rectify the situation and there are ways to unwind the transaction which unknowingly left you with the rebody.  In any event, please call me and we can discuss your options and to what extent your car may have been rebodied.</p>

		<h1><a href="http://www.vintageautomotive.net/?p=231" rel="bookmark">Economics of Car Valuations</a></h1>
		
		<div class="date">
			June 8, 2011 &middot; Posted in <a href="http://www.vintageautomotive.net/?cat=8" title="View all posts in News" rel="category">News</a> &middot; <span>Comments Off</span>&nbsp; 
		</div>
		
		<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial;">The Economics of Vehicle Values</span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span><span style="font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: small;"> </span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: small;">The value of an antique car or a collector vehicle is driven by three key factors: desirability, pedigree/provenance and condition.  These three factors fit into an equation which eventually leads to the value of the vehicle.  The weight to be assigned to any of the factors is subjectively based upon the influence any one factor has over another.</span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial;">Let’s take a look at these three factors:</span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial;"> </span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial;"><strong>Desirability:</strong> Although this is a subjective measure, it is fairly easy to quantify.  A desirable vehicle is one that nearly anyone would love to own.  Also vehicles that were produced in limited quantities or with attributes or options not commonly found on contemporaneous models are desirable.  Exotic vehicles are usually desirable on their name alone.  Finally, some vehicles are desirable simply because they are cool.  While every vehicle is desirable to a collector on some level (yes, even the Yugo is desirable if you can find a complete and somewhat running example), it is the level of desirability which drives the value.  The more people who like the vehicle; the more desirable the vehicle is.  Specific years, specific models, or specific options can make an otherwise undesirable or not so desirable vehicle desirable for the purpose of valuation.   Keep in mind however, that although desirability drives just one aspect of the pricing structure it can sway the equation completely.  A fitting example is a Duesenberg.  In any condition, and even without any pedigree or provenance, a Duesenberg will ALWAYS command a relatively high value based purely upon its highly regarded desirability and relatively low production figures.  The weight to assign to desirability can also be dependent upon the year, model, options or scarcity of a model. </span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: small;"><strong>Pedigree/Provenance:</strong> This is where most vehicles are lacking.  Time has a way of erasing memories.  By and large, information about a vehicle’s origins was not something that was discussed when the vehicles were sold, “back in the day.”  Most vehicle owners do not have much information about the vehicle before they acquired it. Therefore, when you come across a vehicle with paperwork or history, dating from new, you are looking at a vehicle with pedigree.  Merriam-Webster defines “pedigree” as the origin or history of something.  Items that add pedigree are copies of old titles, registration paperwork, original sales forms, window stickers, build sheets, factory documentation, certification, etc.</span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: small;">Provenance is the history of ownership of the vehicle.  Vehicles that were once owned by movie stars or other public figures tend to be worth more than other similar vehicles.  These same vehicles also tend to have more options or unique features which make them more desirable (see above).  Vehicles that have a known, uninterrupted chain of ownership, from new can also be said to have provenance. Nevertheless, the weight assigned to this form of provenance is obviously not the same as the weight assigned to a chain of celebrity ownership or ownership within a large nationally recognized vehicle collection or museum.  For provenance to be given appropriate weight, it must be substantiated with documentation. </span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: small;">A vehicle with either pedigree or provenance is worth more than a similar vehicle without.  A vehicle with these attributes is sometimes referred to as a “no stories” vehicle. </span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial;"><strong>Condition:</strong> This third and final factor is the &#8220;make it or break it&#8221; for most vehicles.  Even if a vehicle has good desirability and pedigree and/or provenance, it likely won’t influence the value of the vehicle as much as condition.  A vehicle with in superb original condition (i.e. extremely well-kept since new) or a vehicle which has been restored or built/rebuilt to an extremely high level is worth more than a similar vehicle needing restoration or a similar vehicle in a deteriorated condition.  In recent years we have seen a push for original vehicles.  As the appreciation for these examples has risen, so has the weight assigned to originality with respect to valuation.  (Note: An overwhelming public appreciation for a particular vehicle or vehicle trait tends to also weigh heavily on desirability).  The more original a vehicle or the better the restoration of the vehicle, the more the vehicle is worth; period. </span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial;">Although the equation into which these three areas are plugged is somewhat objective on its face, the weight assigned to any one area is subjectively based upon the knowledge, expertise, and experience of the person assigning the value.</span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: small;">Any attempt to confidently assign a fixed weight to any of these three areas would be illogical.  There are far too many vehicles with far too many options, characteristics, stories, and degrees of condition to allow for such a rigid valuation method.  Each vehicle must be evaluated first on its own merits and then against similar vehicles with known sales to find a comparable sale.  Then the comparable sales must be evaluated to see how closely they match the subject vehicle.  The closer the comparable sale, the closer your estimate of value will be to the actual value of the subject vehicle.  You must however, keep in mind some principles of economics such as, market saturation versus scarcity, preferences, and rationality (i.e. marginal cost versus marginal benefit).</span></span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Times New Roman; font-size: small;"> </span></p>
<p><span style="font-size: small;"><span style="font-family: Arial;">This theoretical approach to the economics of car valuation should provide you with an understanding of why one vehicle is worth so much more than another. The equation described here should be view as a template which along with the expertise of a seasoned professional will likely allow you to confidently arrive at a value for your particular automobile given the economics discussed herein.</span></span></p>

<h2 style="text-align: center;"><a href="mailto:BShook@dplglaw.com"><span style="color: #000000;">BShook@dplglaw.com</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> </span></h2>
<h2 style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">BUYING A VEHICLE WITHOUT A TITLE</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;"> In the realm of car collecting, one thing can cloud a bargain more than anything else, a vehicle with no title.  The statement “no title” or “sold on bill of sale only” is usually enough to make most potential purchasers turn and run, FAST.  But should it really scare us away? Perhaps, but not without good reason.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;"> A vehicle sold without a certificate of title is a vehicle sold with questions.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Before you purchase any vehicle without a title, you must ask yourself the following. </span></p>
<ol style="text-align: justify;">
<li><span style="color: #000000;">Why doesn’t the seller have a title to transfer to me?</span></li>
<li><span style="color: #000000;">Is the vehicle listed in either the NMVTIS or the NICB VinCheck<sup>SM</sup>?</span></li>
<li><span style="color: #000000;">How am I going to get a certificate of title in my name?</span></li>
</ol>
<h2 style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Why doesn’t the seller have a title to transfer to me?</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">The most obvious question is why does the seller not have a title to the vehicle?  There are many potential answers to this question that should be asked prior to negotiating for the purchase of this vehicle.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">For instance, the vehicle could be missing its title because it was previously salvaged and somehow escaped destruction (i.e. crushing); or worse yet, the vehicle could be stolen.  These are reasons to RUN, FAST!!!</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Often the case, however, is that the vehicle was abandoned, so to speak, by the last owner who held a certificate of title. This usually happened when the vehicle had fallen into such a state of disrepair that the condition and its value at the time made the title and registration of the vehicle a moot point.  Accordingly, whoever acquired the vehicle from the last person may not have requested a transfer of the title for these, or similar reasons.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">The answer to this first question often tells you 90% of what you need to know.  Trust your instinct.  If the story sounds fishy or otherwise unbelievable; it probably is.</span></p>
<h2 style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Is the vehicle listed in either the NMVTIS or the NICB VinCheck<sup>SM</sup>?</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Before you purchase a vehicle without a title you should run the VIN through two sources.  The first is an initiative of the United States Department of Justice located at </span><a href="http://www.vehiclehistory.gov/"><span style="color: #000000;">http://www.vehiclehistory.gov/</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> and known as the National Motor Vehicle Title Information System (NMVTIS).  The NMVTIS is designed to thwart the registration of stolen vehicles and prevent title fraud.  An important note, however, when researching the NMVTIS for collector cars, is that the system is still in its infancy and relies upon information uploaded from individual states.  Results of the NMVTIS include where the vehicle was previously registered and if it has ever been reported stolen or had any other title issues in the past.  With respect to antique and other older collectible vehicles, it is possible that some “older” thefts may have slipped through the cracks. So, don’t rely too heavily on the results of the NMVTIS search if it comes back with no record found.  This is why it is important to also check the second source.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;"> The second source that should be checked is the National Crime Insurance Bureau’s (NCIB) VinCheck<sup>SM</sup>.   This database includes information regarding whether a vehicle has been reported stolen. The link to search the NCIB database is </span><a href="https://www.nicb.org/theft_and_fraud_awareness/vincheck"><span style="color: #000000;">https://www.nicb.org/theft_and_fraud_awareness/vincheck</span></a><span style="color: #000000;"> .</span></p>
<h2 style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;"> Importance of an Adequate Bill of Sale</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;"> If you choose to go ahead with the purchase and buy the vehicle, it is highly recommended that you have the seller give you a bill of sale for the vehicle.  It is important that the bill of sale identify the vehicle by year, make, model and VIN.  The seller should state something to the effect that they are delivering you the absolute rights of possession and ownership of the vehicle.  The seller should also state that to the best of his/her knowledge the vehicle is not stolen and how they acquired the vehicle and from whom.  Of course the bill of sale should also indicate the date of the transaction and full contact information for both you and the seller.  This will ensure compliance with 13 Pa. C.S.A. § 2401 and will assist you in the acquisition of a certificate of title down the road.</span></p>
<h2 style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;"> How am I going to get a certificate of title in my name?</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">The current laws in Pennsylvania contemplate only scenarios in which certificates of title are transferred with every vehicle transaction.  Therefore, by extension, the law fails to contemplate a scenario in which a person or entity would acquire a vehicle or vehicles for which no owner can produce and accordingly transfer a certificate of title. </span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Similarly there appears to be no law, regulation or procedure, formally in place, for the motioning of the Court for a declaration of ownership of a motor vehicle so as to permit Penndot to issue a new certificate of title to memorialize the current owner and allow for registration of the automobile.  Further it is important to note that Penndot has unfortunately purged many of their old motor vehicle records, including ownership and registration records.  As a result, Penndot openly states that “[v]ehicle record information is available for the past 10 years only.”  Penndot Form DL-135 (Rev. 9-10).  Obviously this practice of record retention leaves open many scenarios whereby an owner cannot acquire a certificate title because Penndot has no record of ever issuing one.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">What can be done to acquire a title?  One solution that has developed is to Motion the Court for a Declaration of Ownership.  The Dauphin County Court of Common Pleas has uploaded a sample Motion at </span><a href="http://www.dauphincounty.org/_files/1102.pdf"><span style="color: #000000;">http://www.dauphincounty.org/_files/1102.pdf</span></a><span style="color: #000000;">.</span></p>
<h3 style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Legal History Lesson:</span></h3>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Vehicle titles in Pennsylvania are often the most misunderstood aspect of vehicle ownership.  This is because of historically misinterpreted and misunderstood notions of what exactly constitutes “ownership” of a motor vehicle in Pennsylvania.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Under the Pennsylvania Motor Vehicle Code, an “owner” is defined as:</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="color: #000000;">A person, other than a lienholder, having the property right in or title to a vehicle. The term includes a person entitled to the use and possession of a vehicle subject to a security interest in another person, but excludes a lessee under a lease not intended as security.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">75 Pa. C.S.A. § 102.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">The Superior Court has described titles and ownership this way;</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="color: #000000;">[i]n <em>Braham &amp; Co. v. Steinard-Hannon Motor Co.</em>, 97 Pa.Super. 19, 23 (Pa. Super. Ct. 1929), though it involved the interpretation of earlier legislation concerning motor vehicles, the following language of Judge, later President Judge, Keller is nonetheless pertinent and applicable to The Vehicle Code of the present day: ‘It is clear that the primary purpose of the Act of 1923, supra, was to protect the public against the theft of automobiles and their resale by the thief, and to facilitate the recovery of stolen automobiles. It was a police measure, and was not designed to establish the ownership or proprietorship of the car, * * *.’ And at page 25, of 97 Pa.Super. Judge Keller said: ‘It follows that the act does not provide nor intends to provide that the ‘certificate of title’ shall determine the absolute ownership of the car, or alter or affect in any manner the actual ownership of the vehicle and the relations of the persons interested in it. It only requires registration by the person entitled to its possession and in control of its operation. The certificate is not a warrant of ownership or muniment of title as usually understood in the law. It may be relevant evidence in establishing such title.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;"><em>Weigelt v. Factors Credit Corp.</em>, 174 Pa.Super. 400, 404, 101 A.2d 404, 406 (Pa. Super. Ct. 1954)</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">The idea that a vehicle may have both a legal and an equitable owner was recognized by the Commonwealth Court in <em>Dept. of Trans. v. Walker</em>, 136 Pa.Cmwlth. 704, 584 A.2d 1080 (1990).  The Court in Walker stated that “[i]t follows that Section 102 of the [Vehicle] Code does not provide nor intend to provide that title to a motor vehicle shall determine absolute ownership of such. In fact, our research reveals that in Pennsylvania, the certificate of title constitutes no more than some evidence of ownership.”  <em>Id.</em>, 584 A.2d at 1082, citing Semple v. State Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance Co., 215 F.Supp. 645 (E.D.Pa.1963). <em>See also Aetna Casualty &amp; Surety Co. v. Duncan</em>, 972 F.2d 523 (3d Cir.1992) (under Pennsylvania law, a state-issued certificate of title is in no way controlling on the question of ownership).</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">The courts of Pennsylvania look to see who it is that in fact possesses the attributes commonly associated with ownership.  <em>Aetna Casualty &amp; Surety Co.</em>, at 526.    The elements of ownership that Pennsylvania looks at are the elements of use, benefit, possession, control, responsibility for, and disposition of the automobile.  <em>Wasilko v. Home Mut. Cas. Co.</em>, 210 Pa.Super. 322, 326, 232 A.2d 60, 62 (Pa. Super. Ct. 1967).</span></p>
<h2 style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Conclusion</span></h2>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">In summation, although you may not have received an actual certificate of title when you purchased your vehicle, you still most likely own the vehicle.  This is barring any type of theft in the vehicle’s past or another “owner” stepping in and challenging your interest while asserting that their interest is legally better than yours. </span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">With this in mind, the motioning of the Courts for a declaration that you are the owner of the vehicle, and therefore entitled to a certificate of title from Penndot is often your best bet in getting the vehicle properly titled and on the road.  The declaration of ownership is possible if you have actual legal ownership of the vehicle.  The court will, to the extent that it is able, extinguish the rights and interests of all others, whom you are able to find and notify of the proceedings, in the vehicle.  This will ultimately allow you to register and enjoy your vehicle without having the issue of not having a title hanging over your head and clouding your “ownership” of the vehicle.</span></p>
<p style="text-align: justify;"><span style="color: #000000;">Finally, always remember that a vehicle sold without a certificate of title is a vehicle sold with questions.  This, however, does not absolutely mean that the vehicle can never again be titled.  It does however underscore the need for the exercising of enhanced due diligence throughout the transaction.</span></p>

		<h2 style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-family: Arial;"><strong>Numbers Matching: From a Legal Standpoint</strong><strong></strong></span></h2>
<h2 style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-family: Arial;"><strong>By:  Bryan W. Shook, Esquire</strong><strong></strong></span></h2>
<h2 style="text-align: center;"><span style="font-family: Arial;"><strong>Attorney-at-Law</strong><strong></strong></span></h2>
<h2 style="text-align: center;"><strong><a href="mailto:BShook@dplglaw.com"><span style="font-family: Arial;">BShook@dplglaw.com</span></a></strong></h2>
<p><span style="font-family: Arial;">A seller, an auction house, a broker, etc., all have express duties to not mislead buyers.  It is the presentation that entices and induces a prospective buyer to make an offer or bid on a car.  Often included within this presentation is the phrase “numbers matching”.  Rarely in any field does a single term mean so much; in the field of car collecting, the term “numbers matching” means everything; or does it?</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Arial;">The phrase “numbers matching” was coined an untold number of years ago to describe a vehicle which retained its original driveline (i.e. the driveline that was installed into the vehicle during its initial assembly at its manufacturer’s plant).  Specifically and most important in this definition is the engine; as this is the single most important aspect of a vehicle’s originality.  There are some in the hobby, however that would have you believe that the phrase “numbers matching” has parted ways with its original and understood definition.  These individuals would have you believe that the phrase &#8220;numbers matching&#8221; means that the driveline, has numbers appearing on its components, that look as though they could have been placed on there during the initial assembly process on the manufacturer’s line.  This is where the issue with restamped engines and transmissions becomes ever apparent.</span></p>
<p><strong><span style="font-family: Arial;">This disingenuous play on words is polluting our hobby.</span></strong></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Arial;">Numbers Matching means ORIGINAL; the phrase and the meaning of “numbers matching” have never parted company.  Numbers matching still means, as it always has, that the engine, transmission and rear axle are original to that particular vehicle.  For the phrase to have any other meaning would render it flawed and unnecessary.  The use of the phrase “numbers matching” in a disingenuous fashion opens the seller to a high level of legal exposure.  The buyer who learns after he purchases the vehicle that the vehicle, is not “as advertised”, has the right to revoke his acceptance of the vehicle and enjoys many protections that come along with legal revocation.  For example, these protections could include a statutorily created security interest in the vehicle up to the amount paid for the vehicle, plus certain expenses and other damages.</span></p>
<p><span style="font-family: Arial;">Any misleading use of the phrase &#8220;numbers matching&#8221; blackens the eye of the hobby.  The only reason one would use such a deceitful definition of &#8220;numbers matching&#8221; would be in a cacluated attempt to mask the true nature of the vehicle for self-serving purposes.  This ultimately has a negative impact on the hobby.  </span></p>

			December 31, 2010 &middot; Posted in <a href="http://www.vintageautomotive.net/?cat=8" title="View all posts in News" rel="category">News</a> &middot; <span>Comments Off</span>&nbsp;

		<h1 style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #000000;">2011 Arizona Collector Car Auctions – A Preview</span></h1>
<h1 style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #000000;">By: Bryan W. Shook, Esquire</span></h1>
<h2 style="text-align: center;"><span style="color: #000000;">Email: </span><a href="mailto:bshook@dcdlaw.net"><span style="color: #000000;">bshook@dplglaw.com</span></a></h2>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">December 31, 2010 – Happy New Year!!! – 2011 is sure to be a great year in the collector car market.  The table was set when the fall 2010 collector car auctions finished so strongly. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">The January Arizona collector car auctions are almost here.  Barrett-Jackson, Russo-Steele, RM, Gooding &amp; Co. The Scottsdale Auction and Silver-Fort McDowell Auction are all presenting world class collectible automobiles for sale to the motoring public.  This year’s selection of cars is outstanding. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">RM is selling three Duesenbergs, including a 1926 Duesenberg Model A Dual Windshield Phaeton (Lot # 257), and two 1929s; a 1929 Duesenberg Model J Clear Vision Sedan by Walter M. Murphy Co. (Lot # 272) and an equally desirable 1929 Duesenberg Model J Dual Cowl Phaeton by Derham (Lot # 219).  Lot # 219, the short wheelbase Dual Cowl Phaeton is being offered with reserve. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">Other notable cars to be offered at the RM Sale include Lot # 258, the custom show roadster, “Chromzilla”.  Chromzilla started life as a Brookville steel body which was the foundation for what has to be one of the most show stopping deuces of recent memory.  Chromzilla is being offered from the Estate of John O’Quinn.  Another very interesting car being offered by RM is a 1939 BMW 327/28 Sport Cabriolet.  Given that it is a pre-war BMW, the likes of which this author has rarely seen, this four-seat cabriolet is sure to become a favorite of the collector whose garage it ends up in.  Another notable car being offered by RM is the 1964 Dodge Hemi Charger concept car.  This car was located by legendary concept car collector, Joe Bortz in 1999.  This car has been in the collection of the late John O’Quinn since 2007 and is being offered on behalf of his Estate.</span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">Gooding &amp; Co. is offering several very unique opportunities for collectors.  The first is the offering of an unrestored 1948 Tucker (Car #1010).  Another unique opportunity for collectors is the chance to bid on an extremely rare 1953 Fiat 8V Supersonic which purportedly has less than 18,000 miles from new.  A gorgeous early dark blue Model T Ford (1911) will be offered as well.   For the Supercar lovers, a 2005 Saleen Twin-Turbocharged Competition Package S7 will be offered.  Ladies and Gentlemen, this is a 1000 BHP street-legal rocket ship. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">Russo &amp; Steele have announced they will be offering collectors the chance to bid on very desirable muscle cars through the sale of the Nickey Chicago Collection, the Kevin Hand Collection and the Friedlander Collection. </span></p>
<p><span style="color: #000000;">Bottom line is that this January should prove to be a tremendous opportunity for collectors looking to diversify their collections and add some value to their bottom line with some Blue-Chip collectibles like most of these mentioned today.</span></p>

		<h1><a href="http://www.vintageautomotive.net/?p=103" rel="bookmark">United States Congress sets National Collector Car Appreciation Day as July 9, 2010</a></h1>
		
		<div class="date">
			May 8, 2010 &middot; Posted in <a href="http://www.vintageautomotive.net/?cat=8" title="View all posts in News" rel="category">News</a> &middot; <span>Comments Off</span>&nbsp; 
		</div>
		
		<p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: center; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="color: #000000;"><strong><span style="font-size: 16pt; mso-bidi-font-weight: normal;">NATIONAL COLLECTOR CAR APPRECIATION DAY</span></strong></span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: center; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="color: #000000; font-size: small;">By: Bryan W. Shook, Esquire</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: center; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="color: #000000;">Email: </span><a href="mailto:bshook@dcdlaw.net"><span style="color: #000000;">bshook@dplglaw.com</span></a></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: left; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">May 4, 2010 – At the persuasion of the SEMA Action network with assistance from automotive restoration manufacturers association (ARMO), the United States Senate approved Senate Resolution 213 on May 4, 2010.   The Resolution sets National Collector Car Appreciation Day as July 9, 2010.  Local, regional and national events to celebrate the newly enacted appreciation day are being developed across the country.  This marks a large step forward in collector car hobby. </span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">The resolution, which can be viewed online at <a title="Library of Congress - Thomas.Gov" href="http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/query/z?c111:S.RES.513:" target="_blank">http://thomas.loc.gov/cgi-bin/query/z?c111:S.RES.513:</a> states that:</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;"> </span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">Designating July 9, 2010, as `Collector Car Appreciation Day&#8217; and recognizing that the collection and restoration of historic and classic cars is an important part of preserving the technological achievements and cultural heritage of the United States.</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">Whereas many people in the United States maintain classic automobiles as a pastime and do so with great passion and as a means of individual expression;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">Whereas the Senate recognizes the effect that the more than 100-year history of the automobile has had on the economic progress of the Nation and supports wholeheartedly all activities involved in the restoration and exhibition of classic automobiles;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">Whereas collection, restoration, and preservation of automobiles is an activity shared across generations and across all segments of society;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">Whereas thousands of local car clubs and related businesses have been instrumental in preserving a historic part of the heritage of this Nation by encouraging the restoration and exhibition of such vintage works of art;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">Whereas automotive restoration provides well-paying, high-skilled jobs for people in all 50 States; and</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">Whereas automobiles have provided the inspiration for music, photography, cinema, fashion, and other artistic pursuits that have become part of the popular culture of the United States: Now therefore, be it</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">Resolved, That the Senate&#8211;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">(1) designates July 9, 2010, as `Collector Car Appreciation Day&#8217;;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">(2) recognizes that the collection and restoration of historic and classic cars is an important part of preserving the technological achievements and cultural heritage of the United States;</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">(3) encourages the Department of Education, the Department of Transportation, and other Federal agencies to support events and commemorations of `Collector Car Appreciation Day&#8217;, including exhibitions and educational and cultural activities for young people; and</span></p>
<p class="MsoNormal" style="line-height: normal; margin: 0in 0in 0pt; padding-left: 30px;"><span style="font-family: &amp;amp; COLOR; font-size: 12pt;">(4) encourages the people of the United States to engage in events and commemorations of `Collector Car Appreciation Day&#8217; that create opportunities for collector car owners to educate young people on the importance of preserving the cultural heritage of the United States, including through the collection and restoration of collector cars.</span></p>
