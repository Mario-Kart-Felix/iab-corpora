<p>A <b>vintage car</b> is, in the most general <a href="/wiki/Word_sense" title="Word sense">sense</a>, an old <a href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">automobile</a>, and in the narrower senses of car enthusiasts and <a href="/wiki/Collecting" title="Collecting">collectors</a>, it is a car from the period of 1919 to 1930. Such enthusiasts have categorization schemes for ages of cars that enforce distinctions between <a href="/wiki/Antique_car" title="Antique car">antique cars</a>, vintage cars, <a href="/wiki/Classic_car" title="Classic car">classic cars</a>, and so on. The classification criteria vary, but consensus within any country is often maintained by major <a href="/wiki/Car_club" title="Car club">car clubs</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="rellink">Further information: <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_automobile" title="History of the automobile">History of the automobile</a></div>
<p>The vintage era in the automotive world was a time of transition. The car started off in 1919 as still something of a rarity, and ended up, in 1930, well on the way towards ubiquity. In fact, automobile production at the end of this period was not matched again until the 1950s. In the intervening years, most industrialized states built nationwide <a href="/wiki/Road" title="Road">road</a> systems with the result that, towards the end of the period, the ability to negotiate unpaved roads was no longer a prime consideration of automotive design. In today's terms, a vintage car is defined the same as a <a href="/wiki/Classic_car" title="Classic car">classic</a>.</p>
<p>Cars became much more practical, convenient and comfortable during this period. Car heating was introduced, as was the in-car radio. Four-wheel <a href="/wiki/Brake" title="Brake">braking</a> from a common foot pedal was introduced, as was the use of hydraulically actuated brakes.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Power_steering" title="Power steering">Power steering</a> was also an innovation of this era. Towards the end of the vintage era, the system of <a href="/wiki/Octane_rating" title="Octane rating">octane rating</a> of <a href="/wiki/Fuel" title="Fuel">fuel</a> was introduced, allowing comparison between fuels. In 1923 the gasoline additive Ethyl made its debut at the <a href="/wiki/Indy_500" title="Indy 500" class="mw-redirect">Indy 500</a> that resulted in a boost in octane from the 50's to the 80's<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In the United States <a href="/wiki/Drive-in" title="Drive-in">drive-in</a> <a href="/wiki/Restaurant" title="Restaurant">restaurants</a> were introduced as well as <a href="/wiki/Suburban" title="Suburban" class="mw-redirect">suburban</a> <a href="/wiki/Shopping_center" title="Shopping center" class="mw-redirect">shopping centers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motel" title="Motel">motels</a>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Alfred_P._Sloan" title="Alfred P. Sloan">Alfred P. Sloan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harley_Earl" title="Harley Earl">Harley Earl</a> of General Motors, and <a href="/wiki/Walter_P._Chrysler" title="Walter P. Chrysler" class="mw-redirect">Walter P. Chrysler</a> capitalized on advertising the automobile’s role in the life of the consumer for more than just the utilitarian value compared with the horse. The stock market crash of 1929 started the layoff of automotive workers and many new companies went bankrupt but over two million cars were still produced in 1929 and 1930.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Horatio_Earle" title="Horatio Earle">Horatio Earle</a>, known as the "Father of good roads" had proposed the government create an <a href="/wiki/Interstate_highway_system" title="Interstate highway system" class="mw-redirect">Interstate highway system</a> in 1902 and in 1909 built the World's first mile of concrete road on <a href="/wiki/Woodward_Avenue" title="Woodward Avenue" class="mw-redirect">Woodward Avenue</a> in <a href="/wiki/Detroit" title="Detroit">Detroit</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Federal_road_and_highway_acts">Federal road and highway acts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Federal road and highway acts">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Federal road and highway acts" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aid_Road_Act_of_1916" title="Federal Aid Road Act of 1916">Federal Aid Road Act of 1916</a> was the first federal highway act. War and lack of funding hampered any positive results of this act. The <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aid_Highway_Act_of_1921_(Phipps_Act)" title="Federal Aid Highway Act of 1921 (Phipps Act)">Federal Aid Highway Act of 1921 (Phipps Act)</a> started a 50/50 matching fund to states for road building and resulted in the creation of new and improved roads. During this period as well as the car adapting to society, there were better roads, and society began to adapt to the car. <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight D. Eisenhower</a> participated in the highly publicized <a href="/wiki/Transcontinental_Motor_Convoy" title="Transcontinental Motor Convoy">Transcontinental Motor Convoy</a> in 1919 and after becoming President the experience influenced the <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aid_Highway_Act_of_1956" title="Federal Aid Highway Act of 1956">Federal Aid Highway Act of 1956</a> that included 41,000 miles of highways.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_depression_of_1920-1921">The depression of 1920-1921</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The depression of 1920-1921">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The depression of 1920-1921" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The end of World War I brought about the "<a href="/wiki/Depression_of_1920-21" title="Depression of 1920-21" class="mw-redirect">Depression of 1920-21</a>" with a better than 20 percent inflation rate, a 7 percent <a href="/wiki/Federal_Bank" title="Federal Bank">Fed</a> discount loan rate, and an 11.7 percent unemployment rate, and many companies went bankrupt and the automotive industry was decimated.<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Big_Three">The Big Three</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Big Three">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Big Three" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>From 1920 to 1929, many dramatic changes took place. <a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> went into a financial crisis that lasted until after <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Sloan" title="Alfred Sloan" class="mw-redirect">Alfred Sloan</a> became president in 1923. <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Motor_Car_Company" title="Hudson Motor Car Company">Hudson</a> produced the <a href="/wiki/Essex_(automobile)" title="Essex (automobile)">Essex</a> in 1919 that, by 1925, had propelled the company to third in total sales behind Ford and Chevrolet.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ford_Motor_Company" title="Ford Motor Company">Ford</a> was in the process of building a new plant, buying back stock, and began an 18-month process of tooling-up to replace the <a href="/wiki/Model_T" title="Model T" class="mw-redirect">Model T</a> with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_A_(1927%E2%80%931931)" title="Ford Model A (1927–1931)" class="mw-redirect">Model A</a> in 1927. In 1921 <a href="/wiki/Maxwell_Motor_Company" title="Maxwell Motor Company" class="mw-redirect">Maxwell</a> failed and <a href="/wiki/Walter_P._Chrysler" title="Walter P. Chrysler" class="mw-redirect">Walter P. Chrysler</a>, formerly of General Motors, was brought in to reorganize it and, in 1925, the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Corporation" title="Chrysler Corporation" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler Corporation</a> was formed. With Ford out for a period, Chrysler was able to produce and market the low-priced <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_(automobile)" title="Plymouth (automobile)">Plymouth</a> in 1928, and bought out the <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Brothers" title="Dodge Brothers" class="mw-redirect">Dodge Brothers</a>, also in 1928, resulting in <a href="/wiki/Big_Three_(automobile_manufacturers)" title="Big Three (automobile manufacturers)">"The Big Three"</a> more recently known as the "Detroit Three". During this time <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Britain</a> had six major manufacturers instead of three: <a href="/wiki/Morris_Motors" title="Morris Motors">Morris</a>, <a href="/wiki/Austin_Motor_Company" title="Austin Motor Company">Austin</a>, <a href="/wiki/Standard_Motor_Company" title="Standard Motor Company">Standard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Rootes_Group" title="Rootes Group">Rootes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ford_of_Britain" title="Ford of Britain">Ford of Britain</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Vauxhall_Motors" title="Vauxhall Motors">Vauxhall</a>. There were other automakers that made it past the 1920-1921 depression only to fail during the <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">Great Depression</a>. Among smaller automobile manufacturers was <a href="/wiki/Franklin_(automobile)" title="Franklin (automobile)">Franklin</a> that produced high-quality luxury cars during the 1919 to 1930 vintage era.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety_issues">Safety issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Safety issues">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Safety issues" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Antique automobiles and early to middle era classic cars do not have the safety features that are standard on modern cars. The most rudimentary of safety features, front wheel brakes and <a href="/wiki/Drum_brake" title="Drum brake">hydraulic brakes</a>, began appearing on cars in the 1920s and 1930s respectively. The <a href="/wiki/Seat_belt" title="Seat belt">seat belt</a>, appeared in the sixties and became mandated by <a href="/wiki/Federal_law" title="Federal law">federal</a> seat belt legislation in the United States in 1968.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> However, the merits of adding seat belts to vintage cars is debatable; Chuck Conrad, president of the Des Plaines, Illinois chapter of the Classic Car Club of America stated, "Bolting yourself down to a 70-year-old piece of wood isn't really going to stop anything.".<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Newer mandated safety features, generally apply to production of vehicles after the laws take effect so vehicles built before any new laws are <a href="/wiki/Grandfather_clause" title="Grandfather clause">grandfathered</a>, particularly in the United States.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Collecting">Collecting</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Collecting">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Collecting" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>For the average person car collecting is a hobby. A person usually has a fascination with a certain vehicle or a history with one so seeks a certain make or model. Finding an antique car at an affordable price is not hard but can be relatively expensive depending on the condition or the desired end result. The less work required on a vehicle equates to a higher price. The more work required means a cheaper initial cost, but more in the long run, and a person's level of restoration experience plays an important part.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Investment">Investment</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Investment">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Investment" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Comedian and avid car collector <a href="/wiki/Jay_Leno" title="Jay Leno">Jay Leno</a> stated, "Any car can be a collector car, if you collect it."<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Antique car collecting as an investment can be rewarding but most serious investment collectors seek rare or exotic cars and original unmodified cars hold a more stable price. Collecting as an investment requires expertise beyond enthusiast collecting and the standard of quality is far higher as well as a need for investment protection such as storage and maintenance. A <a href="/wiki/Short-term" title="Short-term" class="mw-redirect">short-term</a> investment collector must be able to find a vehicle that has market value that is expected to rise in the foreseeable near future. A <a href="/wiki/Long-term" title="Long-term" class="mw-redirect">long-term</a> investment collector would be less interested in any short-term value seeking to capitalize on an expected value rise over a period of years and a vehicle must have certain <a href="/wiki/Intrinsic_theory_of_value" title="Intrinsic theory of value">intrinsic values</a> that are common to other investors or collectors of both short and long term.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Rarity">Rarity</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rarity">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rarity" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Antique vehicles have a higher value according to the rarity that usually (but not always) resulted in some reason for a lack of numbers at production. Certain year and model cars became popular to turn into <a href="/wiki/Hot_rod" title="Hot rod">hot rods</a> thus destroying the original condition. Other models were produced in such quantities that the price is still not inflated. <a href="/wiki/Market_trend" title="Market trend">Market trend</a> is an important part in the price of a vintage car. An "almost" original and in perfect shape <a href="/wiki/Ford_Model_A_(1927-1931)" title="Ford Model A (1927-1931)" class="mw-redirect">model A</a> that was abundantly produced can be purchased for $20,000.00.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> A collector as an investor would have to know the potential market and have a belief that the future market will bring a return on an investment. Many collectors also tend to have a direction or like for certain vehicles that reflects their expertise.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Condition_categories">Condition categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Condition categories">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Condition categories" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>To collectors and investors, a vehicle's condition rating<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> is important, and there are two systems, the category and the points system. The category system has six categories used to rate the condition of a vehicle. The points system assigns points from 40 to 100 that corresponds with the category system and below 40 there are three for other conditions. Both systems are listed together for ease of comparison:</p>
<ul>
<li>Category I, Perfect; 90 to 100 points. A vehicle is considered as good or better than the day produced.</li>
<li>Category II, Excellent; 80 to 89 points. A vehicle in excellent original or superior restored, near-flawless condition.</li>
<li>Category III, Fine; 70 to 79 points. A vehicle with an older restoration or an original car with minimal wear. These are considered "show quality".</li>
<li>Category IV, Very good; 60 to 69 points. A vehicle that is in complete original condition, or possibly an older restoration, that is usually a well-cared-for daily driver.</li>
<li>Category V, Good; 50 to 59 points. A vehicle that shows wear, needs attention or work, and needs only minor restoration, with no major flaws. Points from 40 to 59 fall into this category.</li>
<li>Category VI, Driver; 40 to 49 points.</li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_categories">Other categories</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other categories">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vintage_car&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Other categories" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<ul>
<li>Restorable; 30 to 39 points . This vehicle would be in need of restoration of the motor, body, interior and/or chassis. A car in this class should be more or less complete, needing some parts but requiring a tremendous amount of work to get to show quality.</li>
<li>PARTIAL; 20 to 29 points. This vehicle would require extensive restoration with a significant amount of parts and labor—a very time-consuming and costly prospect.</li>
<li>PARTS CAR; 10 to 19 points. This would generally be an inoperative vehicle in poor condition, kept as a source or 'donor' of spare parts. With the exception of very rare vehicles, complete restoration of this category is usually not feasible.</li>
</ul>
