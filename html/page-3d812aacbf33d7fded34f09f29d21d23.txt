<p><b>Minivan</b> is an American English term to describe a type of <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">van</a>, typically either <a href="/wiki/Three-box_styling" title="Three-box styling">two-box or one box</a> designs for maximum interior volume&#160;– and are taller than a <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(car)" title="Sedan (car)" class="mw-redirect">sedan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">hatchback</a>, or a <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a>.</p>
<p>Worldwide, minivans are called <b>multi-purpose vehicles</b> (MPVs), <b>multi-utility vehicles</b> (MUVs), <b>people-carriers</b> or <b>people-movers</b>.</p>

<p>Antecedents for the minivan may include the <a href="/wiki/A.L.F.A_40/60_HP" title="A.L.F.A 40/60 HP">A.L.F.A 40/60 HP Aerodinamica</a> and the 1936 <a href="/wiki/Stout_Scarab" title="Stout Scarab">Stout Scarab</a>, which featured a removable table and second row seats that turn 180 degrees to face the rear&#160;– a feature that Chrysler marketed as <i>Swivel 'n Go.</i><sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/DKW_Schnellaster" title="DKW Schnellaster">DKW Schnellaster</a>, manufactured from 1949 to 1962 was a small monospace (or <i>one-box</i>) design featuring its front wheels set forward of the passenger cabin, a short, sloping aerodynamic hood, <a href="/wiki/Front-wheel_drive" title="Front-wheel drive">front-wheel drive</a>, <a href="/wiki/Transverse_engine" title="Transverse engine">transverse engine</a>, flat load floor throughout with flexible seating and cargo accommodations&#160;– the key design ingredients that describe the modern minivan configuration popularized in such notable examples as the <a href="/wiki/Renault_Espace" title="Renault Espace">Renault Espace</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Caravan" title="Dodge Caravan">Chrysler Voyager/Caravan</a> minivans.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Other predecessors of minivans were <a href="/wiki/Compact_van" title="Compact van">compact vans</a>. In 1950, the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Type_2" title="Volkswagen Type 2">Volkswagen Type 2</a> adapted a bus-shaped body to the compact <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Beetle" title="Volkswagen Beetle">Volkswagen Beetle</a>. It placed the driver above the front wheels, sitting behind a flat nose, with the engine mounted at the <a href="/wiki/RR_layout" title="RR layout" class="mw-redirect">rear</a>. This approach to the driver being placed on top of the front axle is known as a "<a href="/wiki/Cab_over" title="Cab over">cab over</a>". The two hinged side doors were opposite to the driver's side, with optional doors on the driver's side, <a href="/wiki/Fiat" title="Fiat">Fiat</a> built a similar vehicle, Multipla based on the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_600" title="Fiat 600">Fiat 600</a> with the same "cab over" engine and door layout. Japanese and American manufacturers responded with <a href="/wiki/Compact_van" title="Compact van">compact vans</a> since the 1960s. Usually based on front-engined compact cars with a <a href="/wiki/FMR_layout" title="FMR layout" class="mw-redirect">FMR layout</a>, the engine was mounted behind or under the front seat with a flat, vertical nose. Examples include the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Econoline" title="Ford Econoline" class="mw-redirect">Ford Econoline</a>, <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Van" title="Chevrolet Van">Chevrolet Van</a>, <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_Carry" title="Suzuki Carry">Suzuki Carry</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Hiace" title="Toyota Hiace" class="mw-redirect">Toyota Hiace</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Subaru_Sambar" title="Subaru Sambar">Subaru Sambar</a>. When Volkswagen introduced a sliding side door on their van in 1968, it then had all the features that would later come to define a minivan: compact length, three rows of forward-facing seats, station wagon-style top-hinged tailgate/liftgate, sliding side door, passenger car base.</p>
<p>As the U.S. vehicles such as the Econoline evolved into larger full-sized vans, the term minivan came to use in North America, when <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chrysler" title="Chrysler">Chrysler</a> launched their respective smaller minivan products for the 1984 model year. The <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Van" title="Toyota Van" class="mw-redirect">Toyota Van</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Caravan" title="Dodge Caravan">Dodge Caravan</a> / <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Voyager" title="Plymouth Voyager">Plymouth Voyager</a> featured very different structural designs: the Dodge Caravan / Plymouth Voyager had a <a href="/wiki/FF_layout" title="FF layout" class="mw-redirect">FF layout</a> and <a href="/wiki/Unibody" title="Unibody" class="mw-redirect">unibody</a> construction, while the Toyota Van Wagon featured a <a href="/wiki/FMR_layout" title="FMR layout" class="mw-redirect">FMR layout</a> and was built on a <a href="/wiki/Body-on-frame" title="Body-on-frame">body-on-frame</a> chassis. The <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Astro" title="Chevrolet Astro">Chevrolet Astro / GMC Safari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Aerostar" title="Ford Aerostar">Ford Aerostar</a> were introduced for the 1985 model year with <a href="/wiki/FR_layout" title="FR layout" class="mw-redirect">FR layout</a>.</p>
<p>A European minivan design was conceived in the late 1970s by the <a href="/wiki/Rootes_Group" title="Rootes Group">Rootes Group</a> in partnership with the French automaker <a href="/wiki/Matra" title="Matra">Matra</a> (which was also affiliated with <a href="/wiki/Simca" title="Simca">Simca</a>, the former French subsidiary of the Chrysler Corporation, sold in 1977 to the <a href="/wiki/PSA_Group" title="PSA Group" class="mw-redirect">PSA Group</a>). The Matra design was originally intended to be sold as a <a href="/wiki/Talbot" title="Talbot">Talbot</a> and be a replacement for the <a href="/wiki/Matra_Rancho" title="Matra Rancho">Talbot-Matra Rancho</a>. Early prototypes were designed to use Simca parts and a grille like the <a href="/wiki/Simca_1307" title="Simca 1307">Simca 1307</a>. Matra took their idea to <a href="/wiki/Peugeot" title="Peugeot">Peugeot</a> who thought it too expensive and risky so the project was then presented to <a href="/wiki/Renault" title="Renault">Renault</a>, becoming the <a href="/wiki/Renault_Espace" title="Renault Espace">Renault Espace</a> introduced in 1984. The Renault had traditional hinged car doors on both sides. Chrysler had also been developing a minivan based on the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_K_platform" title="Chrysler K platform">Chrysler K platform</a>, releasing the <a href="/wiki/Dodge_Caravan" title="Dodge Caravan">Dodge Caravan</a> / <a href="/wiki/Plymouth_Voyager" title="Plymouth Voyager">Plymouth Voyager</a> earlier than the Espace, in 1983.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">Characteristics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Characteristics" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">Overview</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overview">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Overview" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>MPVs are usually between 1.6–1.8 metres (5&#160;ft 3&#160;in–5&#160;ft 10&#160;in) tall, which is around 20&#160;cm (8&#160;in) taller than a <a href="/wiki/Sedan_(car)" title="Sedan (car)" class="mw-redirect">sedan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Hatchback" title="Hatchback">hatchback</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Station_wagon" title="Station wagon">station wagon</a>. The engine is mounted very close to the front edge of the van, and its elements are grouped higher than in other vehicle types to minimize front overhang length. The rear overhang may be short as in a hatchback or long like in station wagons, changing the cargo area vs seat balance&#160;– the first option is more common in smaller minivans and the second in large minivans.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Seating">Seating</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Seating">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Seating" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Seats are located higher than in lower cars with a higher <a href="/wiki/H-point" title="H-point">H-point</a>, giving passengers a more upright posture and providing more legroom.</p>
<p>Larger MPVs usually feature three rows of seats, with two or three seats each: 2-3-2, 2-2-3 or 2-3-3 (front to rear) are the most common seating configurations. According to <i>Consumer Reports</i>, the most common large size configuration for the 2011 model year was 7 passenger seating. Smaller minivans tend to have two rows of seats, with a traditional 2-3 configuration. There are some exceptions, like the <a href="/wiki/Honda_FR-V" title="Honda FR-V">Honda FR-V</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Multipla" title="Fiat Multipla">Fiat Multipla</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_R-Class" title="Mercedes-Benz R-Class">Mercedes-Benz R-Class</a> which are six seaters (3-3 in the first two cases and 2-2-2 in the latter). On U.S. models, Chrysler copied the short bench, long bench format of their full-size vans for most of their minivan models. Other U.S. manufacturers followed suit. This setup allowed easy access to the rear from the right side (these vans were initially all 3-door models). However, it led to tight seating in the second row. Captain's chairs (the format typically used on U.S. conversion vans) soon began to gain popularity, beginning with top trim models and working its way down. Today, with 4-door models completely replacing 3-doors, the Captain chair setup has become the standard format, with many models no longer offering two bench seats.</p>
<p>MPVs may have seats, either benches or individual seats, that are designed to be relocated, removed, folded partially (on-floor) or folded completely under-floor&#160;– allowing variable <a href="/wiki/Seating_capacity" title="Seating capacity">seating capacity</a> and cargo room.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Chassis_and_drivetrain">Chassis and drivetrain</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chassis and drivetrain">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Chassis and drivetrain" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In contrast to <a href="/wiki/Van" title="Van">vans</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sport_utility_vehicle" title="Sport utility vehicle">sport utility vehicles</a> (SUV), and many <a href="/wiki/Crossover_SUV" title="Crossover SUV" class="mw-redirect">crossover SUVs</a>, most current MPVs are <a href="/wiki/Front-wheel_drive" title="Front-wheel drive">front-wheel drive</a>. This configuration allows a flat inner floor, due to the absence of the driveshaft hump. With rear seats removed, the cargo area in large minivans can hold a 4x8&#160;ft sheet of <a href="/wiki/Drywall" title="Drywall">drywall</a> or <a href="/wiki/Plywood" title="Plywood">plywood</a> flat. <a href="/wiki/Four-wheel_drive" title="Four-wheel drive">Four-wheel drive</a> was also introduced to minivans in North America with the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Van" title="Toyota Van" class="mw-redirect">Toyota Van Wagon 4WD</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Type_2#T3" title="Volkswagen Type 2">Volkswagen Vanagon Syncro</a>. Full-time <a href="/wiki/All-wheel_drive" title="All-wheel drive" class="mw-redirect">all-wheel drive</a> was introduced to North American minivans in 1990 with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Aerostar" title="Ford Aerostar">Ford Aerostar</a>'s E-4WD option, followed in 1991 by the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Previa" title="Toyota Previa">Toyota Previa</a>'s All-Trac, and in 1992 on models made by Chrysler.</p>
<p>Most modern MPVs feature <a href="/wiki/Unibody" title="Unibody" class="mw-redirect">unibody</a> architecture, which offers superior crashworthiness and a more comfortable ride than a <a href="/wiki/Body-on-frame" title="Body-on-frame">body-on-frame</a> chassis, and is typically lighter. The discontinued <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Astro" title="Chevrolet Astro">Chevrolet Astro / GMC Safari</a> were the last subframe-mounted rear-wheel drive minivans.</p>
<p>In the United States, in order to be governed by more lenient safety and <a href="/wiki/Emissions_regulations" title="Emissions regulations" class="mw-redirect">emissions regulations</a>, minivans are classified as <a href="/wiki/Light_truck" title="Light truck">light trucks</a>.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="dubious (April 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Unlike their European counterparts, manual transmissions have disappeared due to lack of demand;<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> 1995 was the last year for a manual transmission in the Ford Aerostar and Chrysler minivans while GM had previously discontinued the manual transmission in the Chevrolet Astro and GMC Safari. Although the <a href="/wiki/Mazda_5" title="Mazda 5" class="mw-redirect">Mazda 5</a> which can be considered a smaller version of a minivan is available with a 5-speed manual transmission.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Doors">Doors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Doors">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Doors" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Door configuration for MPVs are highly variable. Access to the rear interior may be through one or two sliding or out-swing rear side doors. Early minivans featured one rear side sliding door on the passenger's side, similar to full-sized passenger vans in the early 1980s. Many current minivans feature rear sliding doors on both sides; swinging doors are the norm for European and Japanese minivans, while most American models feature sliding doors. Some models feature power sliding doors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Minivans_by_market">Minivans by market</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Minivans by market">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Minivans by market" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="North_America">North America</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: North America">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: North America" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>

<p>The introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_minivans" title="Chrysler minivans" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler minivans</a> revolutionized the family-car market in the U.S. They soon displaced <a href="/wiki/Station_wagons" title="Station wagons" class="mw-redirect">station wagons</a> and became the car of choice for traveling "baby-boomers".<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Within one year the automaker was not able to build enough Voyager/Caravans to meet the market demand as the people-hauler of choice, while competitors were rushing to introduce their versions.<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Shortly after their introduction, Chrysler was met with competition from the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Astro" title="Chevrolet Astro">Chevrolet Astro/GMC Safari</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ford_Aerostar" title="Ford Aerostar">Ford Aerostar</a>; both slightly larger than the Chrysler vans, they borrowed many components from their compact-truck lineup. Utilizing the transverse-mounted engine, front-wheel drive, <a href="/wiki/Monocoque" title="Monocoque">monocoque</a> (unibody) construction and "one-box" design, the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_minivans" title="Chrysler minivans" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler minivans</a> offered better traction, size, and driving characteristics. <a href="/wiki/American_Motors" title="American Motors">AMC</a> planned imports of the <a href="/wiki/Renault_Espace" title="Renault Espace">Renault Espace</a> beginning in 1986; these plans ended with the company's sale to Chrysler.<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan" class="mw-redirect">Nissan</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mitsubishi" title="Mitsubishi">Mitsubishi</a> also introduced minivans to North America during the mid-1980s. In a fashion similar to the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Van" title="Toyota Van" class="mw-redirect">Toyota Van Wagon</a>, they had poor rear drive traction and a bouncy ride due to their short <a href="/wiki/Wheelbase" title="Wheelbase">wheelbase</a>. By 1990, all three would be withdrawn from the North American market. In 1989, Mazda introduced the first front-engined Japanese-brand minivan with the <a href="/wiki/Mazda_MPV" title="Mazda MPV">MPV</a>. Based on the <a href="/wiki/Mazda_929" title="Mazda 929">Mazda 929</a>, the MPV featured a swing-out door and optional four-wheel drive.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/General_Motors" title="General Motors">General Motors</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Lumina_APV" title="Chevrolet Lumina APV">Chevrolet Lumina APV</a>, <a href="/wiki/Oldsmobile_Silhouette" title="Oldsmobile Silhouette">Oldsmobile Silhouette</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Pontiac_Trans_Sport" title="Pontiac Trans Sport">Pontiac Trans Sport</a> in 1990. These minivans were GM's first <a href="/wiki/Front-wheel_drive" title="Front-wheel drive">front-wheel drive</a> minivans; built on a reworked version of GM's 1980s A-platform&#160;– with composite plastic body panels, a <a href="/wiki/Cab-forward" title="Cab-forward" class="mw-redirect">cab-forward</a> nose, steeply raked <a href="/wiki/Windshield" title="Windshield">windshields</a>, and deep <a href="/wiki/Dashboard" title="Dashboard">dashboards</a>.</p>
<p>That same year, <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Previa" title="Toyota Previa">Previa</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Previa" title="Toyota Previa">Toyota Previa</a> had a four-cylinder engine located under the floor of the vehicle, mounted nearly flat on its side, rather than straight up and down like in its predecessor.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford" class="mw-redirect">Ford</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nissan" title="Nissan" class="mw-redirect">Nissan</a> introduced models in 1993 with front-wheel drive, the <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Villager" title="Mercury Villager">Mercury Villager</a> and <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Quest" title="Nissan Quest">Nissan Quest</a> respectively. These minivans featured car-based <a href="/wiki/Automobile_platform" title="Automobile platform">platforms</a> and <a href="/wiki/V6_engine" title="V6 engine">V6</a> engines. Ford introduced a slightly larger front-wheel drive minivan (based on a reworked version of the 1980s Taurus platform) called the <a href="/wiki/Ford_Windstar" title="Ford Windstar">Windstar</a> in 1994.</p>
<p>In 1995 <a href="/wiki/Honda" title="Honda">Honda</a> introduced the <a href="/wiki/Honda_Odyssey_(North_America)" title="Honda Odyssey (North America)">Odyssey</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Honda_Accord" title="Honda Accord">Honda Accord</a> with outswing doors with roll-down second row windows, a rear seat that folded into the floor, and a 140&#160;hp <a href="/wiki/Inline_four" title="Inline four" class="mw-redirect">inline four</a> engine.</p>
<p>According to Autodata in 2006 Chrysler, Honda, and Toyota comprised 72% of the United States minivan market. General Motors and Ford made up 17%, <a href="/wiki/Kia_Sedona" title="Kia Sedona" class="mw-redirect">Kia Sedona</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hyundai_Entourage" title="Hyundai Entourage">Hyundai Entourage</a> sales made up 5%, and the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Quest" title="Nissan Quest">Nissan Quest</a> was 3%. By 2008, most North American minivans had adopted the size and configuration of the long-wheelbase Chrysler vans, with Chrysler dropping their shorter models as well. In 2008, only the Kia Sedona and Chevrolet Uplander offered both short and long-wheelbase configurations. Kia dropped the short-wheelbase version in 2010, and the Chevrolet Uplander has been discontinued due to poor sales. In 2008, Volkswagen debuted the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Routan" title="Volkswagen Routan">Routan</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Badge_engineering" title="Badge engineering">rebadged variant</a> of the Chrysler <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_S_platform#RT" title="Chrysler S platform">RT platform</a> minivans. Volkswagen has since stopped production of the Routan for a crossover being introduced in 2015.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">Europe</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Europe">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Europe" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Despite the <a href="/wiki/Chrysler_Minivans" title="Chrysler Minivans" class="mw-redirect">Chrysler Minivans</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Renault_Espace" title="Renault Espace">Renault Espace</a> did not have any direct rival during the 1980s. Other mainstream automakers began to develop multi-purpose vehicles designed with European tastes in mind. <a href="/wiki/PSA_Peugeot_Citro%C3%ABn" title="PSA Peugeot Citroën">PSA Peugeot Citroën</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Group" title="Fiat Group" class="mw-redirect">Fiat Group</a> founded a joint-venture, <a href="/wiki/Sevel" title="Sevel">Sevel</a>, and released in 1994 the <a href="/wiki/Eurovan_(PSA/Fiat_joint_venture)" title="Eurovan (PSA/Fiat joint venture)" class="mw-redirect">eurovan</a> under the nameplates Citroën Evasion (marked as Synergie in the UK), Peugeot 806, Fiat Ulysse and Lancia Zeta. The <a href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford" class="mw-redirect">Ford</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Group" title="Volkswagen Group">Volkswagen Group</a> JV <a href="/w/index.php?title=Auto-Europa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Auto-Europa (page does not exist)">Auto-Europa</a> similarly co-developed models on a common chassis and built them in a shared-plant in <a href="/wiki/Palmela" title="Palmela" class="mw-redirect">Palmela</a>, Portugal. While the VW/SEAT/Ford model had a length of 4.6&#160;m (15.1&#160;ft), the Espace and the eurovan were around 20&#160;cm (7.9&#160;in) shorter and would be considered today as compact MPVs. All of them were available as seven-seaters and the seats could be folded and removed. These models would be later called "large MPVs".</p>
<p>The trend towards <a href="/wiki/Compact_MPV" title="Compact MPV">compact MPVs</a> began in 1996 with the launch of the <a href="/wiki/Renault_Sc%C3%A9nic" title="Renault Scénic">Renault Scénic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opel_Zafira" title="Opel Zafira">Opel Zafira</a>. Compact MPVs were cars with tall bodies but based on the chassis and engines of a <a href="/wiki/Small_family_car" title="Small family car" class="mw-redirect">small family car</a> (in the case of the Scénic, the <a href="/wiki/Renault_M%C3%A9gane" title="Renault Mégane">Renault Mégane</a>). The runaway success of the Scénic saw the car spawn a multitude of similar vehicles, like the <a href="/wiki/Opel_Zafira" title="Opel Zafira">Opel Zafira</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_5008" title="Peugeot 5008">Peugeot 5008</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_Xsara_Picasso" title="Citroën Xsara Picasso">Citroën Xsara Picasso</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Touran" title="Volkswagen Touran">Volkswagen Touran</a>, the <a href="/wiki/SEAT_Altea" title="SEAT Altea">SEAT Altea/XL</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Ford_C-Max" title="Ford C-Max">Ford C-Max</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Nissan_Almera_Tino" title="Nissan Almera Tino">Nissan Almera Tino</a>. By the mid-first decade of the 21st century virtually all mainstream automakers in Europe had a compact MPV in their range. Citroën's Xsara Picasso was a strong seller and was followed up with the C4 Picasso range with 5- and 7-seat variants, a line-up which resulted in <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn" title="Citroën">Citroën</a> becoming Europe's largest MPV manufacturer.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Also in the mid-first decade of the 21st century, automakers began to use MPV-style designs on <a href="/wiki/Supermini_car" title="Supermini car" class="mw-redirect">supermini</a>-based chassis. Examples of <a href="/wiki/Mini_MPV" title="Mini MPV">mini MPVs</a> them are the <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_C3_Picasso" title="Citroën C3 Picasso">Citroën C3 Picasso</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Citro%C3%ABn_C3" title="Citroën C3">C3</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Idea" title="Fiat Idea">Fiat Idea</a>, based on the <a href="/wiki/Fiat_Punto" title="Fiat Punto">Punto</a> platform.</p>
<p>In 2000, the Auto-Europa triplets (Galaxy, Sharan and Alhambra) were heavily face-lifted. More recently, Ford quit the Auto-Europa joint venture to make its own Galaxy sharing many parts with the <a href="/wiki/Ford_S-Max" title="Ford S-Max">Ford S-Max</a>, another MPV. However the Auto-Europa production was not dissolved as VW and SEAT continued with their own models.</p>
<p>In 2010, the second-generation <a href="/wiki/Volkswagen_Sharan" title="Volkswagen Sharan">Volkswagen Sharan</a> and <a href="/wiki/SEAT_Alhambra" title="SEAT Alhambra" class="mw-redirect">SEAT Alhambra</a> were presented, making the debut of the brand new Volkswagen group's MQB platform, and like their predecessors did, they were both again almost identical in design one to another, with the exception of the front fascias, rear ends and some minor details. The production site would still be the Auto-Europa site in Portugal.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Engines">Engines</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Engines">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Engines" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h4>
<p>European Minivans (MPVs) are generally powered by four-cylinder engines, originally a mix of petrol and diesel units, but with petrol engines becoming increasingly rare as diesels have improved. V6 engines are rare due to the increased fuel consumption of larger engines being considered unacceptable with high fuel prices.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Asia">Asia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Asia">edit source</a><span class="mw-editsection-divider"> | </span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Minivan&amp;veaction=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Asia" class="mw-editsection-visualeditor">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/ASEAN" title="ASEAN" class="mw-redirect">ASEAN</a> nations, China and India, multi-utility vehicles tend to be smaller than North American minivans and European MPVs. Compact MUVs are more popular than models of other sizes. They are also often referred to as "Asian Utility Vehicles" (AUV).</p>
<p>They also differ in that they need to cope with uneven terrain as opposed to paved <a href="/wiki/Highway" title="Highway">highways</a>. Models from local manufacturers are usually based on Japanese designs from <a href="/wiki/Suzuki" title="Suzuki">Suzuki</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daihatsu" title="Daihatsu">Daihatsu</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toyota" title="Toyota">Toyota</a>. Popular models include <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Picnic" title="Toyota Picnic" class="mw-redirect">Toyota Picnic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Previa" title="Toyota Previa">Toyota Previa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mazda_MPV" title="Mazda MPV">Mazda 8</a> and <a href="/wiki/Honda_StepWGN" title="Honda StepWGN" class="mw-redirect">Honda StepWGN</a>.</p>
<p>MUVs vary widely in configuration. Whilst some MUVs might be replicas of European MPVs (such as the European <a href="/wiki/Ford_Fusion_(Europe)" title="Ford Fusion (Europe)">Ford Fusion</a>) or American-style minivans (like the <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Innova" title="Toyota Innova">Toyota Innova</a>), in some cases MUVs are similar to SUVs (such as the <a href="/wiki/Chevrolet_Tavera" title="Chevrolet Tavera">Chevrolet Tavera</a>). Other examples of MUVs are the <a href="/wiki/Maruti_Versa" title="Maruti Versa" class="mw-redirect">Maruti Versa</a>, <a href="/wiki/Isuzu_Panther" title="Isuzu Panther">Isuzu Panther</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Avanza" title="Toyota Avanza">Toyota Avanza</a>, <a href="/wiki/Mahindra_Xylo" title="Mahindra Xylo">Mahindra Xylo</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Alphard" title="Toyota Alphard">Toyota Alphard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Qualis" title="Toyota Qualis" class="mw-redirect">Toyota Qualis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Toyota_Innova" title="Toyota Innova">Toyota Innova</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">Korea</a>, full-size minivans are popular. <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">Korean</a> made examples include the <a href="/wiki/Kia_Carnival" title="Kia Carnival">Kia Carnival</a> and <a href="/wiki/SsangYong_Rodius" title="SsangYong Rodius">SsangYong Rodius</a>.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">Malaysia</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Proton_Exora" title="Proton Exora">Proton Exora</a> is <a href="/wiki/Proton_(carmaker)" title="Proton (carmaker)" class="mw-redirect">Proton</a>'s first MPV and the <a href="/wiki/Perodua_Alza" title="Perodua Alza">Perodua Alza</a> is <a href="/wiki/Perodua" title="Perodua">Perodua</a>'s first MPV.</p>
