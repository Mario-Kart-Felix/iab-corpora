    <h3 class="post-title">
	 
	 LA Auto Show: Where's the green?
	 
    </h3>
    

	         <div class="post-body">
	<div>
      <div style="clear:both;"></div><a onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}" href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/uploaded_images/honda_fc_sport-701914.jpg"><img style="margin: 0pt 0pt 10px 10px; float: right; cursor: pointer; width: 250px; height: 188px;" src="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/uploaded_images/honda_fc_sport-701903.jpg" alt="Hybrid cars at the LA Auto Show" border="0" /></a><span style="font-weight: bold; font-style: italic; color: rgb(102, 0, 0);">The coolest green debut in LA</span><br /><br />Yesterday, as I walked the floor of the LA Auto Show for what seemed like the 1000th time in two days, I felt very disappointed. While there were more <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_cars.htm">hybrid cars</a> and electric vehicles than ever displayed here, few will have any impact in the next couple of years. Many will never even be built.<br /><br />When it comes to fuel economy, particularly city fuel economy, the auto industry has little to offer. 25,000 Ford Fusion hybrids just isn't enough. Nor are small cars, such as the Mini Cooper and its EPA estimated 26 mpg city fuel economy.<br /><br />And, sure, maybe Nissan will have cost-competitive EVs in 2012, but until then we get nothing? Even then, are EVs the focus of the auto industry, or just a niche product to balance their guzzlers into CAFE compliance?<br /><br />Green automakers? That's an oxymoron.<div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/fuel%20economy.html">fuel economy</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Hybrid%20Vehicles.html">Hybrid Vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/LA%20Auto%20Show.html">LA Auto Show</a></p>

    </h3>

    <h3 class="post-title">
	 
	 Automakers turning green? Poppycock
	 
    </h3>
    

	         <div class="post-body">
	<div>
      <div style="clear:both;"></div>There has been a lot of buzz in the media about how automakers are going green and the Los Angeles Auto Show has been cited as an example. I say poppycock.<br /><br />Sure GM debuted the <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_car_types/Saturn_Aura_Hybrid.htm">Saturn Aura hybrid</a> and the <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_car_types/GMC_Yukon_Hybrid.htm">GMC Yukon hybrid</a> in LA, but GM's real green buzz was really generated by GM's <a href="http://www.hybridcarrevolution.com/hybrid_cars_articles/gm_working_on_vue_plug_in_hybrid_vehicle.htm">plug-in hybrid development</a> announcement - even though the company gave no timeline for an arrival. Is it 3 years, five years, 10 years?<br /><br />Anyway, at the LA Auto Show, GM put far more effort into marketing their massive lineup of <span style="font-weight: bold;">huge</span> Flex-fuel trucks and SUVs - not their hybrids. Saturn hybrids, for crying-out-loud, were practically hidden at the show.<br /><br />While I would applaud flex-fuel hybrids, or very fuel efficient flex-fuel compact vehicles, I cannot call GM's flex fuel trucks and SUVs a huge green move. Flex-fuel guzzlers are little better than gas-guzzlers. Even worse, flex-fuel guzzlers seem too much like another excuse for GM not to focus on real fuel economy.<br /><br />Aside from GM, Nissan did debut the <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_car_types/Nissan_altima_hybrid_car.htm">Altima hybrid</a>, and Ford displayed the newly designed <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_car_types/Mercury_Mariner_hybrid.htm">2008 Mercury Mariner hybrid</a> and the <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_car_types/Ford_Escape_Hybrid.htm">2008 Ford Escape hybrid</a>, but Ford's real buzz was generated by the new Mustang more than anything. In reality hybrids were minor drivers for both Ford and Nissan at the LA Show.<br /><br />Then there was a lot of talk about  clean diesel and hydrogen. Blah, blah, blah.<br /><br />Even Toyota, world leader in <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_cars.htm">hybrid cars</a>, was far more focused on promoting the Toyota Tundra, not its Hybrid Synergy Drive. Still, Toyota hybrids did drive significant buzz in LA and it was obvious many were checking out Toyota's hybrids as potential buyers, not just gawkers.<br /><br />Ultimately, automakers have realized that Americans are interested in green issues, however, the keyword is interested. Most Americans are still unwilling to help fund the green revolution, and I think automakers are keenly aware of this fact. At this point in time, green marketing is possibly more important than green automobiles.<br /><br />Americans might be interested in hearing and talking about the green revolution and energy independence, but they are definitely not yet willing to revolt against energy dependence by joining the green revolution.<div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Altima%20hybrid.html">Altima hybrid</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/clean%20diesel.html">clean diesel</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Ford%20Escape%20hybrid.html">Ford Escape hybrid</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/fuel%20economy.html">fuel economy</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Hybrid%20Vehicles.html">Hybrid Vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/LA%20Auto%20Show.html">LA Auto Show</a></p>

    <h3 class="post-title">
	 
	 Toyota hybrids at the LA Auto Show
	 
    </h3>
    

	         <div class="post-body">
	<div>
      <div style="clear:both;"></div>Heading to the Los Angeles Auto Show this weekend, I had few expectations regarding Toyota and its <a href="http://www.soultek.com/clean_energy/hybrid_cars/hybrid_cars.htm">hybrid vehicles</a>. Pre-show hybrid buzz focused on the Aura and Yukon hybrids and GM's new green identity. Since, I started out on the GM side of the convention center, I thought the best of the show's hybrid displays were behind me, especially when I walked into the Toyota side of the convention center and saw Toyota Tundras everywhere.<br /><br />Eventually, though, I did make my way through the Tundras and into Toyota's hybrid section.<br /><br />The difference between GM's hybrid offerings and Toyota's was absolutely stunning. In fact, there was simply no comparison between Toyota and any other automaker at the show regarding hybrid vehicles. Ironically, Toyota's hybrid section was all business and no show gimmicks. There was a huge information bar where you could sit down, talk to a rep and obtain product literature. Then of course, there were Toyota's three hybrids, the Highlander hybrid, Camry hybrid and the Prius.<br /><br />As I sat near the Toyota hybrid section I was amazed at the visitors to this area. This was a high traffic area, but it wasn't just people looking at hybrids. Here people were getting into hybrids - sitting in the front, then the back, opening the trunk, touching every gadget that the vehicles had to offer. These visitors looked like buyers.<br /><br />Wow, I thought, not bad for a company without any new hybrid debuts. Then, however, I noticed the Lexus hybrid section and realized Toyota did debut a hybrid at the show, the Lexus LS600h L hybrid. While Lexus had the GS 450h hybrid the RX 400h hybrid at the show, in addition to a split 450h show gimmick, the LS 600h was the show stopper. Still, the Lexus hybrid section was pure auto show marketing, unlike the Toyota section, and it worked. The buzz around the Lexus hybrids was nothing short of phenomenal.<br /><br />In terms of hybrid vehicles, Toyota didn't just dominate the LA Auto Show; it was the LA Auto Show.<div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Camry%20hybrid.html">Camry hybrid</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Highlander%20hybrid.html">Highlander hybrid</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Hybrid%20Vehicles.html">Hybrid Vehicles</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/LA%20Auto%20Show.html">LA Auto Show</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Lexus%20hybrid.html">Lexus hybrid</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/prius.html">prius</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/toyota.html">toyota</a></p>

    <h3 class="post-title">
	 
	 GM's green side at the LA Auto Show
	 
    </h3>
    

	         <div class="post-body">
	<div>
      <div style="clear:both;"></div>I spent the weekend at this year's LA Auto Show, and I have a lot to write on regarding that topic. I'll start with GM. There was a lot of pre-show hype regarding GM's new green revolution - as I've called it. Since I started my auto show tour on the GM side, I was very excited. Yet, I left the GM side of show unimpressed. Yes, I saw the Yukon hybrid and all of GM's Flex-fuel vehicles - every huge gas-guzzling one of them. Inevitably, a flex-fuel tank is still a tank. <br /><br />The Yukon hybrid is, well, huge. Fine. Yet, the hybrid engine on display near the vehicle seemed to attract more attention than the hybrid itself. So, I went looking for GM's other hybrid debut, the Saturn Aura hybrid.<br /><br />Saturn had a nice little hybrid section at the show that showcased a split Saturn Vue hybrid with lights demonstrating the different modes of its mild hybrid powertrain. It definitely caught people's attention - as they walked into the bathroom. Unfortunately, the display, just as with the Yukon hybrid, caught more attention than either the Vue hybrid or the Aura hybrid. And why did GM practically hide the Saturn hybrid display area?<br /><br />Ultimately, I thought GM's green efforts were completely unimpressive, unless one believes flex-fuel is the solution to the world's energy problems. Unfortunately, I do not hold that view.<div style="clear:both; padding-bottom:0.25em"></div><p class="blogger-labels">Labels: <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/GM.html">GM</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/LA%20Auto%20Show.html">LA Auto Show</a>, <a rel='tag' href="http://www.hybridcarblog.com/labels/Saturn.html">Saturn</a></p>
