<p>A <b>motorcycle</b> (also called a <b>motorbike</b>, <b>bike</b>, <b>moto</b> or <b>cycle</b>) is a two<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> or three wheeled<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Motor_vehicle" title="Motor vehicle">motor vehicle</a>. Motorcycles vary considerably depending on the task they are designed for, such as <a href="/wiki/Touring_motorcycle" title="Touring motorcycle">long distance</a> travel, navigating <a href="/wiki/Naked_bike" title="Naked bike" class="mw-redirect">congested urban traffic</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cruiser_(motorcycle)" title="Cruiser (motorcycle)">cruising</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_sport" title="Motorcycle sport">sport</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motorbike_racing" title="Motorbike racing" class="mw-redirect">racing</a>, or <a href="/wiki/Off-roading" title="Off-roading">off-road</a> conditions.</p>
<p>Motorcycles are one of the most affordable forms of motorised transport in many parts of the world and, for most of the world's population, they are also the most common type of motor vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-japantimes_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japantimes-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-csmonitor_4-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-csmonitor-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-brazil_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brazil-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> There are around 200 million motorcycles (including <a href="/wiki/Moped" title="Moped">mopeds</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scooter_(motorcycle)" title="Scooter (motorcycle)">motor scooters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motorized_bicycle" title="Motorized bicycle">motorised bicycles</a>, and other powered two and three-wheelers) in use worldwide,<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> or about 33 motorcycles per 1000 people. This compares to around 590 million <a href="/wiki/Cars" title="Cars" class="mw-redirect">cars</a>, or about 91 per 1000 people.</p>
<p>Most of the motorcycles, 58%, are in the developing countries of Asia&#160;–  Southern and Eastern Asia, and the Asia Pacific countries, excluding Japan&#160;–  while 33% of the cars (195 million) are concentrated in the United States and Japan. In 2006, China had 54 million motorcycles in use and an annual production of 22 million units.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> As of 2002<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Motorcycle&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>, India, with an estimated 37 million motorcycles/mopeds, was home to the largest number of motorised two wheelers in the world. China came a close second with 34 million motorcycles/mopeds.<sup id="cite_ref-Worldmapper31_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Worldmapper31-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Worldmapper32_10-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Worldmapper32-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century:_the_first_motorcycles">19th century: the first motorcycles</span></h3>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Experimentation_and_invention">Experimentation and invention</span></h4>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Daimler-1-motorcycle-1.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Daimler-1-motorcycle-1.jpg/220px-Daimler-1-motorcycle-1.jpg" width="220" height="213" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Daimler-1-motorcycle-1.jpg/330px-Daimler-1-motorcycle-1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Daimler-1-motorcycle-1.jpg/440px-Daimler-1-motorcycle-1.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Daimler-1-motorcycle-1.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf13/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
Replica of the Daimler-Maybach <i>Petroleum Reitwagen</i>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The first <a href="/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine" title="Internal combustion engine">internal combustion</a>, <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> fueled motorcycle was the <a href="/wiki/Daimler_Reitwagen" title="Daimler Reitwagen"><i>Petroleum Reitwagen</i></a>. It was designed and built by the German inventors <a href="/wiki/Gottlieb_Daimler" title="Gottlieb Daimler">Gottlieb Daimler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Maybach" title="Wilhelm Maybach">Wilhelm Maybach</a> in <a href="/wiki/Bad_Cannstatt" title="Bad Cannstatt" class="mw-redirect">Bad Cannstatt</a>, Germany in 1885.<sup id="cite_ref-ThePast1800s_11-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ThePast1800s-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> This vehicle was unlike either the <a href="/wiki/Safety_bicycle" title="Safety bicycle">safety bicycles</a> or the <a href="/wiki/Velocipede#Boneshaker" title="Velocipede">boneshaker</a> bicycles of the era in that it had zero degrees of <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_and_motorcycle_geometry#Steering_axis_angle" title="Bicycle and motorcycle geometry">steering axis angle</a> and no <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_and_motorcycle_geometry#Fork_offset" title="Bicycle and motorcycle geometry">fork offset</a>, and thus did not use the principles of <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_and_motorcycle_dynamics" title="Bicycle and motorcycle dynamics">bicycle and motorcycle dynamics</a> developed nearly 70 years earlier. Instead, it relied on two outrigger wheels to remain upright while turning.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The inventors called their invention the <i>Reitwagen</i> ("riding car"). It was designed as an expedient testbed for their new engine, rather than a true prototype vehicle.<sup id="cite_ref-Setright1979_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Setright1979-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Falco1999_14-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falco1999-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Many authorities who exclude <a href="/wiki/Steam_engine" title="Steam engine">steam powered</a>, electric or diesel two-wheelers from the definition of a motorcycle, credit the Daimler <i>Reitwagen</i> as the world's first motorcycle.<sup id="cite_ref-OED_15-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-OED-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kresnak2008_17-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kresnak2008-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>If a two-wheeled vehicle with steam propulsion is considered a motorcycle, then the first was the French <a href="/wiki/Michaux-Perreaux_steam_velocipede" title="Michaux-Perreaux steam velocipede">Michaux-Perreaux steam velocipede</a> of 1868.<sup id="cite_ref-Setright1979_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Setright1979-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Falco1999_14-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falco1999-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> This was followed by the American <a href="/wiki/Roper_steam_velocipede" title="Roper steam velocipede">Roper steam velocipede</a> of 1869, built by <a href="/wiki/Sylvester_H._Roper" title="Sylvester H. Roper">Sylvester H. Roper</a> <a href="/wiki/Roxbury,_Massachusetts" title="Roxbury, Massachusetts" class="mw-redirect">Roxbury, Massachusetts</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-Setright1979_13-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Setright1979-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Falco1999_14-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falco1999-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Roper demonstrated his machine at fairs and circuses in the eastern U.S. in 1867,<sup id="cite_ref-ThePast1800s_11-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ThePast1800s-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> and built a total of 10 examples.<sup id="cite_ref-Kresnak2008_17-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kresnak2008-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Beginnings_of_mass_production">Beginnings of mass production</span></h4>
<p>In 1894, <a href="/wiki/Hildebrand_%26_Wolfm%C3%BCller" title="Hildebrand &amp; Wolfmüller">Hildebrand &amp; Wolfmüller</a> became the first series production motorcycle, and the first to be called a motorcycle (<a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Motorrad</i></span>).<sup id="cite_ref-Setright1979_13-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Setright1979-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Falco1999_14-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Falco1999-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Kresnak2008_17-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kresnak2008-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-Brief_History_of_the_Marque:_Hildebrand_.26_Wolfmuller_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Brief_History_of_the_Marque:_Hildebrand_.26_Wolfmuller-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the early period of motorcycle history, many producers of <a href="/wiki/Bicycle" title="Bicycle">bicycles</a> adapted their designs to accommodate the new internal combustion engine. As the engines became more powerful and designs outgrew the bicycle origins, the number of motorcycle producers increased. Many of the nineteenth century inventors who worked on early motorcycles often moved on to other inventions. Daimler and Roper, for example, both went on to develop automobiles.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">20th century</span></h3>
<p>Until <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>, the largest motorcycle manufacturer in the world was <a href="/wiki/Indian_(motorcycle)" title="Indian (motorcycle)">Indian</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> producing over 20,000 bikes per year.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> By 1920, this honour went to <a href="/wiki/Harley-Davidson" title="Harley-Davidson">Harley-Davidson</a>,<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2010)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> with their motorcycles being sold by dealers in 67 countries.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> By the late 1920s or early 1930s, <a href="/wiki/DKW" title="DKW">DKW</a> took over as the largest manufacturer.<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Birmingham_Small_Arms_Company" title="Birmingham Small Arms Company">BSA Group</a> became the largest producer of motorcycles in the world, producing up to 75,000 bikes per year in the 1950s.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The German company <a href="/wiki/NSU_Motorenwerke_AG" title="NSU Motorenwerke AG" class="mw-redirect">NSU</a> held the position of largest manufacturer from 1955 until the 1970s.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<p>In the 1950s, streamlining began to play an increasing part in the development of racing motorcycles and the "dustbin fairing" held out the possibility of radical changes to motorcycle design. NSU and <a href="/wiki/Moto_Guzzi" title="Moto Guzzi">Moto Guzzi</a> were in the vanguard of this development, both producing very radical designs well ahead of their time.<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> NSU produced the most advanced design, but after the deaths of four NSU riders in the 1954–1956 seasons, they abandoned further development and quit <a href="/wiki/Grand_Prix_motorcycle_racing" title="Grand Prix motorcycle racing">Grand Prix motorcycle racing</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Moto_Guzzi" title="Moto Guzzi">Moto Guzzi</a> produced competitive race machines, and by 1957 nearly all the Grand Prix races were being won by streamlined machines.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The following year, 1958, full enclosure fairings were banned from racing by the <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_Internationale_de_Motocyclisme" title="Fédération Internationale de Motocyclisme">FIM</a> in the light of the safety concerns.</p>
<p>From the 1960s through the 1990s, small two-stroke motorcycles were popular worldwide, partly as a result of <a href="/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic" title="German Democratic Republic" class="mw-redirect">East German</a> Walter Kaaden's engine work in the 1950s.<sup id="cite_ref-Motocross_goes_International.2C_1947_through_1965_29-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Motocross_goes_International.2C_1947_through_1965-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">21st century</span></h3>
<p>In the 21st century, the motorcycle industry is mainly dominated by Japanese companies. In addition to the large capacity motorcycles, there is a large market in smaller capacity (less than 300&#160;cc) motorcycles, mostly concentrated in Asian and African countries. An example is the 1958 <a href="/wiki/Honda_Super_Cub" title="Honda Super Cub">Honda Super Cub</a>, which went on to become the biggest selling vehicle of all time, with its 60 millionth unit produced in April 2008.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Today, this area is dominated by mostly <a href="/wiki/Category:Motorcycle_manufacturers_of_India" title="Category:Motorcycle manufacturers of India">Indian companies</a> with <a href="/wiki/Hero_MotoCorp" title="Hero MotoCorp">Hero MotoCorp</a> emerging as the world's largest manufacturer of two wheelers. Its <a href="/wiki/Hero_Honda_Splendor" title="Hero Honda Splendor">Splendor</a> model has sold more than 8.5&#160;million to date.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Other major producers are <a href="/wiki/Bajaj_Auto" title="Bajaj Auto">Bajaj</a> and <a href="/wiki/TVS_Motors" title="TVS Motors" class="mw-redirect">TVS Motors</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>In numerous cultures, motorcycles are the primary means of motorised transport. According to the Taiwanese government, for example, "the number of automobiles per ten thousand population is around 2,500, and the number of motorcycles is about 5,000."<sup id="cite_ref-Sustainable_Development_Indicators_for_Taiwan_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sustainable_Development_Indicators_for_Taiwan-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> In places such as Vietnam, motorised traffic consist of mostly motorbikes<sup id="cite_ref-csmonitor_4-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-csmonitor-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> due to a lack of public transport and low income levels that put automobiles out of reach for many.<sup id="cite_ref-japantimes_3-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japantimes-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The four largest motorcycle markets in the world are all in Asia: China, India, Indonesia, and Vietnam.<sup id="cite_ref-japantimes_3-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-japantimes-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> The motorcycle is also popular in Brazil's frontier towns.<sup id="cite_ref-brazil_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-brazil-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> Amid the <a href="/wiki/Economic_crisis_of_2008" title="Economic crisis of 2008" class="mw-redirect">global economic downturn of 2008</a>, the motorcycle market grew by 6.5%.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Recent years have seen an increase in the popularity of motorcycles elsewhere. In the USA, registrations increased by 51% between 2000 and 2005.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> This is mainly attributed to increasing fuel prices and urban congestion.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> A <a href="/wiki/Consumer_Reports" title="Consumer Reports">Consumer Reports</a> subscribers' survey of mainly United States motorcycle and scooter owners reported that they rode an average of only 1,000 miles (1,600&#160;km) per year, 82% for recreation and 38% for commuting.<sup id="cite_ref-Travers2010_38-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Travers2010-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> Americans put 10,000–12,000 miles (16,000–19,000&#160;km) per year on their cars and light trucks.<sup id="cite_ref-EPA-Emissions_39-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-EPA-Emissions-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As motorcyclists age, there is a tendency for riders to choose touring bikes over sports bikes.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>While people choose to ride motorcycles for various reasons, those reasons are increasingly practical, with riders opting for a powered two-wheeler as a cost-efficient alternative to infrequent and expensive public transport systems, or as a means of avoiding or reducing the effects of urban congestion.<sup id="cite_ref-Rider_training_in_Europe_The_Views_and_the_Needs_of_the_Rider_41-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Rider_training_in_Europe_The_Views_and_the_Needs_of_the_Rider-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> In places where it is permitted, <a href="/wiki/Lane_splitting" title="Lane splitting">lane splitting</a>, also known as filtering, allows motorcycles to use the space between vehicles to move through stationary or slow traffic.<sup id="cite_ref-All_the_info_you_need_on_lanesharing_.28lanesplitting.29_42-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-All_the_info_you_need_on_lanesharing_.28lanesplitting.29-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the UK, motorcycles are exempt from the £10 per day <a href="/wiki/London_congestion_charge" title="London congestion charge">London congestion charge</a> other vehicles must pay to enter the city during the day. Motorcycles are also exempt from <a href="/wiki/Toll_(road_usage)" title="Toll (road usage)" class="mw-redirect">toll</a> charges at some river crossings, such as the <a href="/wiki/Severn_Bridge" title="Severn Bridge">Severn Bridge</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dartford_Crossing" title="Dartford Crossing">Dartford Crossing</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Mersey_Tunnels" title="Mersey Tunnels">Mersey Tunnels</a>. Some cities, such as <a href="/wiki/Bristol" title="Bristol">Bristol</a>, allow motorcycles to use <a href="/wiki/Bus_lane" title="Bus lane">bus lanes</a> and provide dedicated free parking. In the United States, those states that have <a href="/wiki/High-occupancy_vehicle_lane" title="High-occupancy vehicle lane">high-occupancy vehicle lanes</a> also allow for motorcycle travel in them in accordance with federal law,<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> in addition to a reduced fee on certain toll roads. Other countries have similar policies.</p>
<p>In New Zealand, motorcycle riders are not required to pay for parking that is controlled by a barrier arm;<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> the arm does not occupy the entire width of the lane, and the motorcyclist simply rides around it.<sup id="cite_ref-45" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> Many car parks controlled in this way supply special areas for motorcycles to park, so as not to unnecessarily consume spaces.</p>
<p>In many cities that have serious parking challenges for cars, such as <a href="/wiki/Melbourne" title="Melbourne">Melbourne</a>, Australia, motorcycles are generally permitted to park on the sidewalk, rather than occupy a space on the street which might otherwise be used by a car.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Construction">Construction</span></h3>
<div class="rellink boilerplate seealso">See also: <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_components" title="Motorcycle components">Motorcycle components</a>&#160;and <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_design" title="Motorcycle design">Motorcycle design</a></div>
<p>Motorcycle construction is the engineering, manufacturing, and assembly of components and systems for a motorcycle which results in the performance, cost, and aesthetics desired by the designer. With some exceptions, construction of modern mass-produced motorcycles has standardised on a <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">steel</a> or <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">aluminium</a> <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_frame" title="Motorcycle frame">frame</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_fork" title="Motorcycle fork">telescopic forks</a> holding the front wheel, and <a href="/wiki/Disc_brake" title="Disc brake">disc brakes</a>. Some other body parts, designed for either aesthetic or performance reasons may be added. A petrol powered <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_engine" title="Motorcycle engine">engine</a> typically consisting of between one and four <a href="/wiki/Cylinder_(engine)" title="Cylinder (engine)">cylinders</a> (and less commonly, up to eight cylinders) coupled to a <a href="/wiki/Manual_transmission" title="Manual transmission">manual</a> five- or six-speed <a href="/wiki/Sequential_gearbox" title="Sequential gearbox" class="mw-redirect">sequential transmission</a> drives the <a href="/wiki/Swingarm" title="Swingarm">swingarm</a>-mounted rear wheel by a chain, <a href="/wiki/Driveshaft" title="Driveshaft" class="mw-redirect">driveshaft</a> or <a href="/wiki/Gilmer_belt" title="Gilmer belt">belt</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Fuel_economy">Fuel economy</span></h3>
<p>Motorcycle fuel economy varies greatly with engine displacement and riding style<sup id="cite_ref-Motorcycle_Fuel_Consumption_.26_Real_World_Performance_Guide_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Motorcycle_Fuel_Consumption_.26_Real_World_Performance_Guide-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> ranging from a low of 29&#160;mpg<sub><small>-US</small></sub> (8.1&#160;L/100&#160;km; 35&#160;mpg<sub><small>-imp</small></sub>) reported by a Honda VTR1000F rider,<sup id="cite_ref-Total_Motorcycle_Fuel_Economy_Guide_47-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Total_Motorcycle_Fuel_Economy_Guide-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> to 107&#160;mpg<sub><small>-US</small></sub> (2.20&#160;L/100&#160;km; 129&#160;mpg<sub><small>-imp</small></sub>) reported for the Verucci Nitro 50&#160;cc scooter.<sup id="cite_ref-Verucci_Gas_Scooters_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Verucci_Gas_Scooters-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> A specially designed Matzu Matsuzawa Honda XL125 achieved 470&#160;mpg<sub><small>-US</small></sub> (0.50&#160;L/100&#160;km; 560&#160;mpg<sub><small>-imp</small></sub>) "on real highways&#160;–  in real conditions."<sup id="cite_ref-Doing_More_with_Less_Energy_49-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Doing_More_with_Less_Energy-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Due to low engine displacements (100&#160;cc–200&#160;cc), and high power-to-mass ratios, motorcycles offer good fuel economy. Under conditions of fuel scarcity like 1950s Britain and modern developing nations, motorcycles claim large shares of the vehicle market.</p>
<h4><span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_motorcycles">Electric motorcycles</span></h4>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Electric_motorcycle" title="Electric motorcycle" class="mw-redirect">Electric motorcycle</a></div>
<p>Very high fuel economy equivalents are often derived by electric motorcycles. Electric motorcycles are nearly silent, <a href="/wiki/Zero_emission" title="Zero emission">zero-emission</a> electric motor-driven vehicles. Operating range and top speed are limited by battery technology.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <a href="/wiki/Fuel_cell_vehicle" title="Fuel cell vehicle">Fuel cells</a> and petroleum-electric <a href="/wiki/Hybrid_vehicle" title="Hybrid vehicle">hybrids</a> are also under development to extend the range and improve performance of the electric drive system.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reliability">Reliability</span></h3>
<p>A 2013 survey of 4,424 readers of the US <i><a href="/wiki/Consumer_Reports" title="Consumer Reports">Consumer Reports</a></i> magazine collected reliability data on 4,680 motorcycles purchased new from 2009 to 2012.<sup id="cite_ref-ConsumerReports2013_50-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConsumerReports2013-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> The most common problem areas were <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_accessories" title="Motorcycle accessories">accessories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_brakes" title="Motorcycle brakes" class="mw-redirect">brakes</a>, electrical (including <a href="/wiki/Starter_(engine)" title="Starter (engine)">starters</a>, <a href="/wiki/Alternator#Automotive_alternators" title="Alternator">charging</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ignition_system" title="Ignition system">ignition</a>), and <a href="/wiki/Fuel_system" title="Fuel system" class="mw-redirect">fuel systems</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Types_of_motorcycles" title="Types of motorcycles">types of motorcycles</a> with the greatest problems were touring, off road/dual sport, sport-touring, and cruisers.<sup id="cite_ref-ConsumerReports2013_50-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConsumerReports2013-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> There were not enough sport bikes in the survey for a <a href="/wiki/Statistically_significant" title="Statistically significant" class="mw-redirect">statistically significant</a> conclusion, though the data hinted at reliability as good as cruisers.<sup id="cite_ref-ConsumerReports2013_50-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConsumerReports2013-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> These results may be partially explained by accessories including such equipment as fairings, luggage, and auxiliary lighting, which are frequently added to touring, adventure touring/dual sport and sport touring bikes.<sup id="cite_ref-Bartlett2013_51-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Bartlett2013-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Trouble with fuel systems is often the result of improper winter storage, and brake problems may also be due to poor maintenance.<sup id="cite_ref-ConsumerReports2013_50-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConsumerReports2013-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Of the five brands with enough data to draw conclusions, Honda, Kawasaki and Yamaha were statistically tied, with 11 to 14% of those bikes in the survey experiencing major repairs.<sup id="cite_ref-ConsumerReports2013_50-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConsumerReports2013-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Harley-Davidsons had a rate of 24%, while BMWs did worst, with 30% of those needing major repairs.<sup id="cite_ref-ConsumerReports2013_50-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConsumerReports2013-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> There were not enough Triumph and Suzuki motorcycles surveyed for a statistically sound conclusion, though it appeared Suzukis were as reliable as the other three Japanese brands while Triumphs were comparable to Harley-Davidson and BMW.<sup id="cite_ref-ConsumerReports2013_50-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConsumerReports2013-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Three fourths of the repairs in the survey cost less than US$ 200 and two thirds of the motorcycles were repaired in less than two days.<sup id="cite_ref-ConsumerReports2013_50-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConsumerReports2013-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> In spite of their relatively worse reliability in this survey, Harley-Davidson and BMW owners showed the greatest owner satisfaction, and three fourths of them said they would buy the same bike again, followed by 72% of Honda owners and 60 to 63% of Kawasaki and Yamaha owners.<sup id="cite_ref-ConsumerReports2013_50-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ConsumerReports2013-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Dynamics">Dynamics</span></h3>

<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Motorcycle_racing.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Motorcycle_racing.jpg/220px-Motorcycle_racing.jpg" width="220" height="147" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Motorcycle_racing.jpg/330px-Motorcycle_racing.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Motorcycle_racing.jpg/440px-Motorcycle_racing.jpg 2x" /></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Motorcycle_racing.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"><img src="//bits.wikimedia.org/static-1.22wmf13/skins/common/images/magnify-clip.png" width="15" height="11" alt="" /></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Superbike_racing" title="Superbike racing">Racing motorcycles</a> leaning in a turn.</div>

<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_and_motorcycle_dynamics" title="Bicycle and motorcycle dynamics">Bicycle and motorcycle dynamics</a></div>
<p>Different types of motorcycles have different dynamics and these play a role in how a motorcycle performs in given conditions. For example, one with a longer wheelbase provides the feeling of more stability by responding less to disturbances.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_tyres" title="Motorcycle tyres" class="mw-redirect">Motorcycle tyres</a> have a large influence over handling.</p>
<p>Motorcycles must be leaned in order to make turns. This lean is induced by the method known as <a href="/wiki/Countersteering" title="Countersteering">countersteering</a>, in which the rider momentarily steers the handlebars in the direction opposite of the desired turn. Because it is counter-intuitive this practice is often very confusing to novices&#160;–  and even to many experienced motorcyclists.<sup id="cite_ref-Steering_in_bicycles_and_motorcycles_53-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Steering_in_bicycles_and_motorcycles-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Short <a href="/wiki/Bicycle_and_motorcycle_geometry#Wheelbase" title="Bicycle and motorcycle geometry">wheelbase</a> motorcycles, such as <a href="/wiki/Sport_bike" title="Sport bike">sport bikes</a>, can generate enough torque at the rear wheel, and enough stopping force at the front wheel, to lift the opposite wheel off the road. These actions, if performed on purpose, are known as <a href="/wiki/Wheelie" title="Wheelie">wheelies</a> and <a href="/wiki/Stoppie" title="Stoppie">stoppies</a> respectively. If carried past the point of recovery the resulting upset is known as an "endo" (short for "end-over-end"), or "looping" the vehicle.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Accessories">Accessories</span></h3>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_accessories" title="Motorcycle accessories">Motorcycle accessories</a></div>
<p>Various features and accessories may be attached to a motorcycle either as <a href="/wiki/Original_equipment_manufacturer" title="Original equipment manufacturer">OEM</a> (factory-fitted) or <a href="/wiki/After-market" title="After-market" class="mw-redirect">after-market</a>. Such accessories are selected by the owner to enhance the motorcycle's appearance, safety, performance, or comfort, and may include anything from <a href="/wiki/Automotive_electronics" title="Automotive electronics">mobile electronics</a> to <a href="/wiki/Sidecar" title="Sidecar">sidecars</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_trailer" title="Motorcycle trailer">trailers</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Safety">Safety</span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_safety" title="Motorcycle safety">Motorcycle safety</a> and <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_safety_clothing" title="Motorcycle safety clothing" class="mw-redirect">Motorcycle safety clothing</a></div>
<p>Motorcycles have a higher rate of fatal accidents than automobiles or trucks and buses. <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Transportation" title="United States Department of Transportation">United States Department of Transportation</a> data for 2005 from the <a href="/wiki/Fatality_Analysis_Reporting_System" title="Fatality Analysis Reporting System">Fatality Analysis Reporting System</a> show that for passenger cars, 18.62 fatal crashes occur per 100,000 registered vehicles. For motorcycles this figure is higher at 75.19 per 100,000 registered vehicles&#160;–  four times higher than for cars.<sup id="cite_ref-FARS_trends_54-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-FARS_trends-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> The same data shows that 1.56 fatalities occur per 100 million vehicle miles travelled for passenger cars, whereas for motorcycles the figure is 43.47–28 times higher than for cars (37 times more deaths per mile travelled in 2007).<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> Furthermore for motorcycles the accident rates have increased significantly since the end of the 1990s, while the rates have dropped for passenger cars.</p>

Wearing a motorcycle helmet reduces the risks of death or head injury in a motorcycle crash</div>

<p>The two major causes of motorcycle accidents in the United States are: motorists pulling out or turning in front of motorcyclists and violating their rights-of-way, and motorcyclists running wide through turns.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The former is sometimes called a <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/SMIDSY" class="extiw" title="wikt:SMIDSY">SMIDSY</a>, an acronym formed from the motorists' common response of "Sorry mate, I didn't see you".<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> The latter is more commonly caused by operating a motorcycle while intoxicated.<sup id="cite_ref-The_Hurt_Report_57-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-The_Hurt_Report-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> Motorcyclists can anticipate and avoid some of these crashes with proper training, increasing their <a href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conspicuity" class="extiw" title="wikt:conspicuity">conspicuity</a> to other traffic, and not consuming alcohol or drugs before riding.<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The United Kingdom has several organisations dedicated to improving motorcycle safety by providing advanced rider training beyond what is necessary to pass the basic motorcycle licence test. These include the <a href="/wiki/Institute_of_Advanced_Motorists" title="Institute of Advanced Motorists">Institute of Advanced Motorists</a> (IAM) and the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_for_the_Prevention_of_Accidents" title="Royal Society for the Prevention of Accidents">Royal Society for the Prevention of Accidents</a> (RoSPA). Along with increased personal safety, riders with these advanced qualifications may benefit from reduced insurance costs.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In South Africa, the <a href="/wiki/Think_Bike" title="Think Bike">Think Bike</a> campaign is dedicated to increasing both motorcycle safety and the awareness of motorcycles on the country's roads. The campaign, while strongest in the Gauteng province, has representation in Western Cape, KwaZulu Natal and the Free State. It has dozens of trained marshals available for various events such as cycle races and is deeply involved in numerous other projects such as the annual Motorcycle Toy Run.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Motorcycle safety education is offered throughout the United States by organisations ranging from state agencies to non-profit organisations to corporations. Most states use the courses designed by the <a href="/wiki/Motorcycle_Safety_Foundation" title="Motorcycle Safety Foundation">Motorcycle Safety Foundation</a> (MSF), while <a href="/wiki/Team_Oregon" title="Team Oregon">Oregon</a> and Idaho developed their own. All of the training programs include a Basic Rider Course, an Intermediate Rider Course and an Advanced Rider Course.</p>
<p>In the UK and some Australian jurisdictions, such as <a href="/wiki/Victoria_(Australia)" title="Victoria (Australia)">Victoria</a>, <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">New South Wales</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Learner_riders_licence_60-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Learner_riders_licence-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Capital_Territory" title="Australian Capital Territory">Australian Capital Territory</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Learner_Licence_61-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Learner_Licence-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">Tasmania</a><sup id="cite_ref-TAS_Learner_Licence_62-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-TAS_Learner_Licence-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> and the <a href="/wiki/Northern_Territory" title="Northern Territory">Northern Territory</a>,<sup id="cite_ref-Motorcyclist_Education_Training_And_Licensing_.28METAL.29_63-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Motorcyclist_Education_Training_And_Licensing_.28METAL.29-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> it is compulsory to complete a basic rider training course before being issued a Learners Licence, after which they can ride on public roads with L plates in the UK and P plates in Australia.</p>
<p>In Canada, motorcycle rider training is compulsory in <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">Quebec</a> and <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">Manitoba</a> only, but all provinces and territories have <a href="/wiki/Graduated_driver_licensing" title="Graduated driver licensing">graduated licence</a> programs which place restrictions on new drivers until they have gained experience. Eligibility for a full motorcycle licence or endorsement for completing a Motorcycle Safety course varies by province. The <a href="/wiki/Canada_Safety_Council" title="Canada Safety Council">Canada Safety Council</a>, a non-profit safety organisation, offers the Gearing Up program across Canada and is endorsed by the Motorcycle and Moped Industry Council.<sup id="cite_ref-MMIC_Information_64-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MMIC_Information-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> Training course graduates may qualify for reduced insurance premiums.</p>

<p>There are three major types of motorcycle: street, off-road, and dual purpose. Within these types, there are many different sub-types of motorcycles for many different purposes.</p>
<p>Street bikes include <a href="/wiki/Cruiser_(motorcycle)" title="Cruiser (motorcycle)">cruisers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sportbike" title="Sportbike" class="mw-redirect">sportbikes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scooter_(motorcycle)" title="Scooter (motorcycle)">scooters</a> and <a href="/wiki/Moped" title="Moped">mopeds</a>, and many other types. Off-road motorcycles include many types designed for dirt-oriented racing classes such as <a href="/wiki/Motocross" title="Motocross">motocross</a> and are not street legal in most areas. Dual purpose machines like the <a href="/wiki/Dual-sport" title="Dual-sport" class="mw-redirect">dual-sport</a> style are made to go off-road but include features to make them legal and comfortable on the street as well.</p>
<p>Each configuration offers either specialised advantage or broad capability, and each design creates a different riding posture.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Motorcycle_rider_postures">Motorcycle rider postures</span></h2>
<p>The motorcyclist's riding position depends on rider body-geometry (<a href="/wiki/Anthropometry" title="Anthropometry">anthropometry</a>) combined with the geometry of the motorcycle itself. These factors create a set of three basic postures.<sup id="cite_ref-msf-usa.org_65-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-msf-usa.org-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Sport&#160;–  the rider leans forwards into the wind and the weight of the upper torso is supported by the rider's core at low speed and air pressure at high speed (e.g., above 50&#160;mph (80&#160;km/h)). The footpegs are below the rider or to the rear. The reduced frontal area cuts wind resistance and allows higher speeds. At low-speed this position throws the weight of the rider onto the arms, which can tire the rider's wrists.</li>
<li>Standard&#160;–  the rider sits upright or leans forward slightly. The feet are below the rider. These are motorcycles that are not specialised to one task, so they do not excel in any particular area.<sup id="cite_ref-Maher1998_66-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Maher1998-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-67" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> The standard posture is used with <a href="/wiki/Touring_motorcycle" title="Touring motorcycle">touring</a> and commuting as well as dirt and dual-sport bikes, and may offer advantages for beginners.<sup id="cite_ref-Stermer1_68-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Stermer1-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Cruiser&#160;–  the rider sits at a lower seat height with the upper torso upright or leaning slightly rearward. Legs are extended forwards, sometimes out of reach of the regular controls on cruiser pegs. The low seat height can be a consideration for new or short riders. Handlebars tend to be high and wide. The emphasis is on comfort, while compromising cornering ability because of low ground clearance and the greater likelihood of scraping foot pegs, floor boards, or other parts if turns are taken at the speeds other motorcycles can more readily accomplish.<sup id="cite_ref-69" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-70" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>Factors of a motorcycle's <a href="/wiki/Ergonomics" title="Ergonomics" class="mw-redirect">ergonomic</a> geometry that determine the seating posture include the height, angle and location of footpegs, seat and handlebars. Factors in a rider's physical geometry that contribute to seating posture include torso, arm, thigh and leg length, and overall rider height.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_definitions_and_restrictions">Legal definitions and restrictions</span></h2>
<div class="rellink relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Legal_definition_of_motorcycle" title="Legal definition of motorcycle">Legal definition of motorcycle</a></div>
<p>A motorcycle is broadly defined by law in most countries for the purposes of registration, taxation and rider licensing as a powered two-wheel motor vehicle. Most countries distinguish between mopeds of 49&#160;cc and the more powerful, larger vehicles (scooters do not count as a separate category). Many jurisdictions include some forms of <a href="/wiki/Three-wheeled_car" title="Three-wheeled car" class="mw-redirect">three-wheeled cars</a> as motorcycles.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Environmental_impact">Environmental impact</span></h2>
<p>Motorcycles and scooters' low fuel consumption has attracted interest in the United States from environmentalists and those affected by increased fuel prices.<sup id="cite_ref-LATimesEmissions_71-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LATimesEmissions-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-72" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Piaggio_Group_Americas" title="Piaggio Group Americas" class="mw-redirect">Piaggio Group Americas</a> supported this interest with the launch of a "Vespanomics" website and platform, claiming lower per-mile carbon emissions of 0.4&#160;lb/mile (113&#160;g/km) less than the average car, a 65% reduction, and better fuel economy.<sup id="cite_ref-73" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>However, a motorcycle's <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_emissions_control" title="Vehicle emissions control">exhaust emissions</a> may contain 10–20 times more <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen_oxides" title="Nitrogen oxides" class="mw-redirect">oxides of nitrogen (NOx)</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Unburned_hydrocarbon" title="Unburned hydrocarbon">unburned hydrocarbons</a> than exhaust from a similar-year passenger car or SUV.<sup id="cite_ref-LATimesEmissions_71-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LATimesEmissions-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-74" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-74"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup> This is because many motorcycles lack a <a href="/wiki/Catalytic_converter" title="Catalytic converter">catalytic converter</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/Emission_standard" title="Emission standard">emission standard</a> is much more permissive for motorcycles than for other vehicles.<sup id="cite_ref-LATimesEmissions_71-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LATimesEmissions-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> While catalytic converters have been installed in most gasoline-powered cars and trucks since 1975 in the United States, they can present fitment and heat difficulties in motorcycle applications.<sup id="cite_ref-LATimesEmissions_71-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-LATimesEmissions-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> Along with other emissions-reducing technologies that have taken longer to appear in motorcycles than in cars, such as <a href="/wiki/Fuel_injection" title="Fuel injection">fuel injection</a>, catalytic converters are becoming increasingly commonplace. Many newer motorcycles, such as the <a href="/wiki/Yamaha_R1" title="Yamaha R1" class="mw-redirect">Yamaha R1</a> and <a href="/wiki/Suzuki_GSXR1000" title="Suzuki GSXR1000" class="mw-redirect">Suzuki GSXR1000</a> do have catalytic converters, and most BMWs have been equipped with catalytic converters since the 1990s.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency" title="United States Environmental Protection Agency">United States Environmental Protection Agency</a> 2007 certification result reports for all vehicles versus on highway motorcycles (which also includes scooters),<sup id="cite_ref-75" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-75"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> the average certified emissions level for 12,327 vehicles tested was 0.734. The average "Nox+Co End-Of-Useful-Life-Emissions" for 3,863 motorcycles tested was 0.8531. 54% of the tested 2007-model motorcycles were equipped with a catalytic converter.</p>

<p><a href="/wiki/European_emission_standards" title="European emission standards">European emission standards</a> for motorcycles are similar to those for cars.<sup class="Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources.
