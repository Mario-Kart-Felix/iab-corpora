    $('ul.tabs').each(function () {

        // For each set of tabs, we want to keep track of
        // which tab is active and it's associated content
        var $active, $content, $links = $(this).find('a');

        // If the location.hash matches one of the links, use that as the active tab.
        // If no match is found, use the first link as the initial active tab.
        $active = $($links.filter('[href="' + location.hash + '"]')[0] || $links[0]);
        $active.addClass('active');
        $content = $($active.attr('href'));

        // Hide the remaining content
        $links.not($active).each(function () {
            $($(this).attr('href')).hide();
        });

        // Bind the click event handler
        $(this).on('click', 'a', function (e) {
            // Make the old tab inactive.
            $active.parent().removeClass('active');
            $content.hide();

            // Update the variables with the new link and content
            $active = $(this);
            $content = $($(this).attr('href'));

            // Make the tab active.
            $active.parent().addClass('active');
            $content.show();

            // Prevent the anchor's default click action
            e.preventDefault();
        });
    });</script>

    <div class="text">
        <p>Isn&rsquo;t nice to get something you asked for? We think so. How good is it?&nbsp; Take a look for a used Acura TSX, and you&rsquo;ll find that they hold their value exceptionally well. </p>

    <h4 class="font18"><a href="/Blog/post/Could-a-Wagon-Revival-Be-Underway.aspx" data-role="ajax-nav">Could a Wagon Revival Be Underway?</a></h4>

    <span class="author"><a href="/Blog/author/James-M-Flammang.aspx">James M. Flammang</a></span> on <span class="pubDate" itemprop="datePublished">01.03.2011 03:01</span>
    <div class="text">
        <p>Nissan marketers, promoting their redesigned Quest, wonder if 2011 might be the &ldquo;year of the minivan.&rdquo; A similar question could be asked about wagons. Both body styles have shrunk in popularity, due far more to issues of image than to disappointment with their practical virtues.</p>
<p>Minivans, which first appeared in the Chrysler/Dodge/Plymouth lineup in 1984, enjoyed a resounding surge of popularity through the late 1980s and &lsquo;90s. As the 21st century loomed, however, the gloss was mostly gone. Minivans were perceived as the province of family-focused &ldquo;soccer moms,&rdquo; at a time when women were seeking broader roles.</p>
<p>Station wagons&ndash;which dropped the &ldquo;station&rdquo; prefix decades ago&ndash;have a checkered history in the American market. Introduced by the 1920s as a practical people-carrier, often used to carry passengers to and from railroad stations, the wagon hit its heyday in the suburban world of postwar America. As the &ldquo;baby boom&rdquo; generation went through childhood in America&rsquo;s suburbs of the Fifties, chances are, the automobile in the driveway or carport was a station wagon. Most likely, a <em>big</em> wagon, with a mighty V-8 engine and rear-facing third-row seats, so the youngsters could see where they&rsquo;d just been.</p>
<p>During the first decade of the 21st century, most manufacturers abandoned wagons, though they remained popular in Europe&ndash;highly prized for their sensible characteristics. In the smaller-scale segments of the market, vehicles that might once have been considered small wagons are more likely to be branded hatchbacks, or even small crossover vehicles. Evidently, the term &ldquo;wagon&rdquo; itself suffers from an image problem similar to the derisive attitudes toward minivans.  <a href="/Blog/post/Could-a-Wagon-Revival-Be-Underway.aspx" >more</a>

        <p class="MsoNormal"><img src="~/Blog/image.axd?picture=auto_news/2010-VW-Jetta-SportWagen.jpg" alt="2010 Volkswagen Jetta SportWagen" align="left" border="2" height="201" hspace="2" width="320" />Let’s say you happen to
crave the sweet driving experience that only a European car can provide, but
need the added space and versatility of a wagon. Come right this way, and let
us introduce you to the VW Jetta SportWagen. While both the Jetta and Jetta
Wagon have been around for quite some time, the current model really is
something special—and even a bit of a bargain.</p><p>&nbsp;</p><p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">On the outside, the Jetta
SportWagen is handsome, with clean, crisp modern lines. Our bright red SE
tester featured the optional 17-inch alloy wheels, and looked quite upscale.
Helping make a solid first impression are standard items including dual
reflector headlamps, exterior mirrors with built-in turn signals and sporty
dual exhaust tips.</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">VW has been setting the bar
for quality interiors in affordable vehicles for some time now, and the Jetta
continues the trend. Deep zucchini-cut nacelles hold the main gauges, while a
grippy, three-spoke steering wheel feels great to the hands. </p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">A simple center stack with
a large LCD screen puts all infotainment features close at hand, and the
climate controls are easily deciphered at a glance. What really sells the
interior is the quality of the materials—the vinyl surfaces are soft, nicely
grained and look very high-end. Even the leatherette on the seats looks
quality. It just gives you the feeling of money well spent.</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">If you’re buying a wagon,
you obviously want space, and the Jetta proves to be comfy for five with plenty
of room in back. Drop the rear seats and you get an impressive 66.9 cubic feet
of cargo space, roomier than a BMW 3 Series or Audi A4 wagon.</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">Also impressive is how well
this wagon drives. It has that smooth, well-oiled feeling of a high-performance
German machine. The super-silky 2.5-liter, 5-cylinder sounds and pulls like an
Audi, yet gets you 30 mpg on the highway. The optional Tiptronic lets you
manually shift for added driving fun. The ride is firm and well controlled. </p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">The best part is all this
lovely European stuff is quite affordable. Our nicely equipped SE model
included standard goodies like Bluetooth, Sirius Satellite Radio, alloy wheels
and leatherette upholstery. Adding in the optional Panoramic Sunroof (highly
recommended) and 17-inch alloy wheels brought our tester to just over $25,000.
For eco-minded buyers, the TDI diesel wagon is worth a look, with an amazing 31
mpg city/ 41 mpg highway EPA fuel economy. </p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">A European driving
experience combined with style, room, quality and affordability (they even
cover maintenance for 3 years/36,000 miles) all add up to make the VW Jetta
SportWagen one great all-around vehicle.</p><p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p><p class="MsoNormal">By Ben Lewis, Contributing Editor, autoMedia.com</p><p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p><p class="MsoNormal">Read autoMedia&#39;s review of the <a href="http://www.automedia.com/2010_Volkswagen_Golf/rts20091001vg/1">2010 Volkswagen Golf/GTI</a> and review other VW models in our <a href="http://www.automedia.com/New_Cars/volkswagen">Volkswagen Research Center</a> and our <a href="http://www.automedia.com/cs/blogs/auto_news/archive/tags/Volkswagen/default.aspx">Volkswagen news blog</a>. </p>

driving.</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">And of course it wouldn’t be a Subaru without standard
all-wheel-drive for excellent all-weather traction. Add in the Outback’s high
ground clearance and long suspension travel and you’ve got a supple ride over
the most cratered city streets, along with sure-footed ability for light
off-roading. If you live in hilly areas, the standard hill-hold feature is a
great thing on a stick shift, keeping you from rolling backwards when pulling
away on an incline. </p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">Wherever your next adventure takes you, those riding along
will be happy with super comfortable bucket seats up front, and huge room out
back (sorry…) with reclining rear seats featuring four inches more legroom than
the previous model. Speaking of room, folding down the rear seats yields over
71 cubic feet of cargo space making it one of the roomiest crossovers. Another
clever feature to maximize hauling ability are roof rails that can swing inward
to double as crossbars. </p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">The interior is quite handsome with a two-tone
dark-and-light dash, large, easy-to-read gauges, and super-simple climate and
audio controls. Quality of materials is excellent throughout, reinforcing that
you’re well surrounded and protected.</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">What price goodness? The most basic 2.5i like our tester starts
at a very reasonable $22,995, and is well equipped, including standard air
conditioning, 4-speaker CD audio system with auxiliary audio input jack, cruise
control, power windows, locks, keyless remote entry, stability control,
traction control, all-wheel drive, and front, side and side-curtain airbags. </p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">You can build from there, with more expensive models adding
leather, premium audio, navigation – even a 256-hp 6-cylinder engine. But to
tell you the truth, we didn’t miss any of those niceties. We were having too
much fun enjoying a fun-to-drive, practical and uniquely Subaru experience the
all-new 2010 Outback provides.</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>

<p class="MsoNormal">By Ben Lewis, Contributing Editor, autoMedia.com</p>

    <span class="font18 bold"><a href="/Blog/post/quick-take-test-drive-all-new-2010-toyota-4runner.aspx" data-role="ajax-nav">Quick Take Test Drive: All-New 2010 Toyota 4Runner</a></span><br/>

    <span class="author"><a href="/Blog/author/Ben-Lewis.aspx">Ben Lewis</a></span> on <span class="pubDate" itemprop="datePublished">12.31.2009 02:11</span>
    <div class="text">
        <p class="MsoNormal"><img src="~/Blog/image.axd?picture=auto_news/2010-Toyota-%204Runner.jpg" border="2" alt="2010-Toyota-4Runner" hspace="2" width="320" height="213" align="left" />It&rsquo;s hard to believe, but the 2010 4Runner is the 5th generation of Toyota&rsquo;s popular SUV. And it&rsquo;s been 20 years since they first introduced a 4-door model. How time flies.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">Two decades later, almost every carmaker has at least one 4-door SUV in its fleet, if not a few. And while most SUVs have become more like giant station wagons, Toyota believes there is still a need from buyers for a vehicle with the ruggedness and off-road ability the 4Runner has always stood for.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">The new design is still instantly recognizable as a 4Runner, but also seems to have picked up some of the muscular chunkiness of the FJ Cruiser along the way&mdash;not a big surprise as the new 4Runner shares the rugged chassis of the FJ.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">What is a big surprise is the massive improvement in the driving. While we have no doubt in the new model&rsquo;s off-road abilities with a tough chassis and advanced off-road electronics, on-road performance is greatly improved with an especially smooth ride combined with commendably agile handling.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">Replacing last year&rsquo;s choice of six- and eight-cylinder engines are more fuel-efficient four- and six-cylinders. Our 4.0-liter V6 tester pumped an impressive 270 hp, 10 more than last year&rsquo;s V8. For a big vehicle with four-wheel drive, we were pleased with the 20-mpg average during our time with our vehicle.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">And it was time well spent, as the interior of the new 4Runner is also an impressive improvement over the previous edition. The biggest difference comes from a lower floor. In the old 4Runner, you always felt you were sitting on top of the vehicle rather than in it, and headroom also felt constrained. </p>
<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">The new interior feels much more spacious, and with interior cues including deep binnacles for the instruments, a beefy four-spoke steering wheel and upscale chrome-tone trim throughout. It feels modern, logically laid-out and, just as important, a fun place to be.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">As always there&rsquo;s a <a href="http://www.automedia.com/New_Cars/Toyota_4Runner_2010">nice choice of models and trims with the 4Runner</a>, starting at $27,500 for the 2WD four-cylinder SR5. Our heavily optioned SR5 V6 4WD stickered at just over $36,000, and was a perfect blend of tough and luxurious. It also garnered more than its fair share of compliments on the new look&mdash;always a good sign.</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">Come to think of it, we don&rsquo;t ever really remember a bad 4Runner. They just seem to get better every time around, which makes this the best 4Runner yet. </p>
<p class="MsoNormal">&nbsp;</p>
<p class="MsoNormal">By Ben Lewis, Contributing Editor, autoMedia.com</p>
