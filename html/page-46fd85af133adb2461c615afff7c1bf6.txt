	<p>The NHTSA standard for VIN assignment is applied to all motorized vehicles, motorcycles and trailers that travel on the roads, streets and highways of the US.&nbsp; However, the NHTSA has no jurisdiction over recreational vehicles not intended to ever be driven on a street or highway. &nbsp;These "off-road" recreational vehicles are hugely popular and account for millions of purchases and tens of millions of vehicles in use in the US every year. &nbsp;As a result there is a need to decode and identify these vehicles for initial sales and marketing efforts, resale, registration, insurance, and taxation. &nbsp;Are these vehicles able to be VIN decoded using the same principles and decoders used for light duty and passenger vehicles? &nbsp;The answer is a solid...maybe.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>ATV's, Side-by-Side and Dirt Bikes</strong></p>
<p>These "off-road" vehicles are not under NHTSA jurisdiction unless they are created with the intent to include "on road" use. &nbsp;When "on-road" use is intended by the manufacturer, the vehicle will be assigned a 17 digit VIN. &nbsp;The NHTSA standard does not address "off-road" vehicles, however you will find that most large manufacturers of these vehicles assign a 17 digit identification number that is compatible with the ISO VIN Standard. &nbsp;It may not, however, be compatible with more stringent <a href="http://vin.dataonesoftware.com/vin_basics_blog/bid/96920/What-s-in-the-Vehicle-Identification-Number" title="NHTSA VIN requirements" target="_self">NHTSA VIN requirements</a>. &nbsp;Also, since regulations did not require the migration to a 17-digit standard by a certain date, each manufacturer may have a different model year that they started use of the 17-digit identification numbers. &nbsp;Use may even vary for a manufacturer within a model year from model to model. &nbsp;</p>
<p>Before the widespread adoption of the 17-digit standard, many manufacturers used shorter (Sometimes as short as 8 digits) proprietary identification codes that require a decoder unique to the make. Some of the smaller and low end manufacturers still use a proprietary identification system that would not be compatible with modern VIN decoding solutions.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p><strong>Snowmobiles</strong></p>
<p>Snowmobiles are not required by the NHTSA to have a 17 digit vehicle identification number to be sold within the US. &nbsp;Transport Canada, however, requires a 17 Digit VIN be assigned to Snowmobiles that is specific to make, line/model and engine. &nbsp;As a result of this requirement, many manufacturers (Yamaha, Artic Cat, Bombardier, Polaris to name a few) assign a 17 digit VIN that meets the Canadian regulations to all the snowmobile models that are sold in the US and Canada.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>What can you expect if you are hoping to VIN Decode these off-road segments of the vehicle population? &nbsp;Unlike passenger vehicles in MY1981, there is not a clean start model year when off-road OEMs started using 17-digit VINs.&nbsp; So expect some inconsistencies in year ranges covered by different manufacturers. In addition, not all manufacturers assign 17 digit VINs. &nbsp;However, most of the large manufacturers do, and use them consistently.&nbsp;&nbsp; Based on these two facts, a VIN decoding solution is not a stand alone option if you are looking to identify off-road vehicles over a large span of years or across a large number of smaller manufacturers.&nbsp; On the other hand, since most major manufacturers use the VINs in recent years, a decoding solution can be a very helpful, important and time saving tool if your business works regularly with off-road vehicles.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
