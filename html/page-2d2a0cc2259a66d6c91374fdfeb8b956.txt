  <div class="meta">
        <div class="submitted"><span class="timestamp">February 3, 2011 @ 11:45am</span> | <span class="authors"><a href="/authors/lary_coppola" title="KPBJ Publisher">Lary Coppola</a></span></div>    <div class="nodecontent">
        <p><a href="http://kpbj.com/drupalfiles/kpbj_images/2011/2011DodgeCaravan_2402_lightbox.jpg" title="2011 Dodge Grand Caravan" rel="lightshow[kpbj]"><img src="http://kpbj.com/drupalfiles/kpbj_images/2011/2011DodgeCaravan_2402_large.jpg" alt="2011 Dodge Grand Caravan" title="2011 Dodge Grand Caravan" width="280" height="173" class="inlineimagesR" /></a>Chrysler is acknowledged as the creator of the modern minivan, and the company has re-staked its claim as leader of that market segment with major upgrades to the 2011 Dodge Grand Caravan and Chrysler Town &amp; Country family haulers. The overlap between the Chrysler and Dodge minivans is gone as well. Clearly defined marketing strategy aims Dodge at the under-30, under $30,000 crowd, while steering bigger spenders toward the Chrysler nameplate, which starts at about $31,000 and goes&nbsp;up.</p>
<p>Behind the new Ram-like grille, sits a smooth, gutsy 3.6-liter Pentastar V-6, which is now standard across both the Dodge and Chrysler lines, and replaces the previous three V-6 engine choices — none as powerful as the Pentastar. And the upgrades are targeted where buyers will notice it most — on upgraded materials in massively improved&nbsp;interiors.</p>
<p>It seems other major minivans competitors — the Honda Odyssey, Toyota Sienna and totally new Nissan Quest, have all suddenly received complete redesigns as well, so the Grand Caravan update doesn&#8217;t come a moment too&nbsp;soon.</p>
<p><strong>Model Lineup:</strong> The 2011 Dodge Grand Caravan comes in three trim levels — the base Express, mid-level Mainstreet, and topline&nbsp;Crew.</p>
<p>Pricing starts at $25,830 for the Express, which includes keyless entry, Stow &#8216;n Go second-row captain&#8217;s chairs, and a multifunction steering wheel. The Mainstreet adds body-color exterior trim and power rear windows. The upscale Crew starts at $28,695 and comes with three-zone automatic climate control, power sliding doors, a power driver&#8217;s seat, adjustable pedals, an infotainment system with a 30-gig hard drive, and a lot&nbsp;more.</p>
<p>A &#8220;sporty&#8221; R/T version is promised for later this year, with Dodge billing it as &#8220;the ultimate Man Van.&#8221; It will be equipped much like the Crew but with a number of visual tweaks such as a body-color grille, polished aluminum 17-inch wheels; a nine-speaker, 506-watt sound system; and what Dodge calls a performance suspension — all for $31,430. Dodge boasts it will deliver a &#8220;sportier driving experience,&#8221; and it&#8217;s supposedly aimed at male and female drivers who drove sports cars before they had kids. We&#8217;ll&nbsp;see.</p>
<p>Our test vehicle was a pretty much fully loaded Dodge Grand Caravan Crew, which stickered at $33,575, so we&#8217;ll primarily restrict commentary to that&nbsp;vehicle.</p>
<p><strong>Walkaround:</strong> Not much to say here. It&#8217;s a box — just like every other minivan on the planet. There&#8217;s not a lot of ways to execute exciting, adventurous, styling, and rounding the edges and adding big vertical taillights like the 80&#8217;s-era Oldsmobile vans really didn&#8217;t cut it, so visual tweaks are about all that&#8217;s possible. For 2011, the Grand Caravan received a new crosshair front grille and a more sculpted front fascia. In the rear is a redesigned bumper and a liftgate sporting sharper creases and new&nbsp;lettering.</p>
<p>Aiding fuel economy is a half-inch reduction in the van&#8217;s ride height, lower-rolling-resistance tires, and a drag-reducing roof&nbsp;spoiler.</p>
<p><strong>Interior:</strong> The Grand Caravan&#8217;s thoroughly refreshed interior features a new soft-touch dash and a sporty, tunneled gauge cluster. This is a major improvement over the cheap hard plastic that swathed the interior of the previous&nbsp;model.</p>
<p>The cabin has been upgraded with a larger center console, a two-dial instrumentation cluster and smooth integration of the climate-control buttons and vents into the dash. Everything is illuminated in Chrysler&#8217;s signature&nbsp;green.</p>
<p>The Stow &#8216;n Go seats are larger, softer and more comfortable then before, with new one-touch&nbsp;operation.</p>
<p>The large side doors are conveniently electrically operated both with a gentle touch, or from the key fob, as is the&nbsp;liftgate.</p>
<p>New options include a heated steering wheel, and driver&#8217;s knee airbag, along with the Safety Sphere package that includes a backup camera, parking sensors, and a blind-spot warning system with rain-sensing wipers and automatic&nbsp;headlights.</p>
<p><strong>Under The Hood:</strong> Like the Chrysler Town &amp; Country — and most other Chrysler products — the Dodge Grand Caravan features the aforementioned new 3.6-liter Pentastar V6 powerplant. It&#8217;s tuned to deliver 283 ponies with 260 lb-ft of torque. This is more power than previously offered by the 3.3, 3.8, and 4.0-liter sixes it replaces. The only transmission choice is a six-speed automatic with a &#8220;Fuel Economizer&#8221; button that adjusts shift timing for reduced fuel&nbsp;consumption.</p>
<p>The Pentastar is rated at 17/City and 25/Highway — pretty respectable for a&nbsp;minivan.</p>
<p><strong>Behind The Wheel:</strong> Let&#8217;s face it, driving a minivan isn&#8217;t usually an adrenaline-pumping experience — unless you take a twisty mountain-road corner too fast, and my expectations weren&#8217;t that high, so I was pleasantly&nbsp;surprised.</p>
<p>The Pentastar stays pretty busy lugging the 4,500-pound Grand Caravan around — especially when passing, as the transmission works through its six gears. That said, the Grand Caravan feels relatively quick for a minivan, and the throaty growl of the V-6 was a major surprise — as was the pleasant amount of road feel and impressive responsiveness of the&nbsp;chassis.</p>
<p>The extensive upgrades to the Grand Caravan&#8217;s suspension, which include everything from new bushings and dampers to revised spring rates and retuned steering gear, has vastly improved the ride comfort, steering feel, and&nbsp;handling.</p>
<p><strong>Whines:</strong> The level of road noise inside is a bit high, but then consider that you&#8217;re moving a steel box through the&nbsp;atmosphere.</p>
<p><strong>Bottom Line:</strong> The 2011 Dodge Grand Caravan is a much-improved product in every single category over the previous version, and will compete strongly against offerings from Ford and GM as well as the upgraded, and higher priced minivans from Honda, Toyota, and Nissan. Dodge has re-staked its claim to the minivan market with a great offering that will be a hard value to&nbsp;beat.</p>
